Frequently Asked Questions

What is AI governance?

AI governance refers to the systems, responsibilities and controls used to oversee how automated decision systems are designed, deployed and monitored. It ensures that organisations can explain how decisions are made, who is responsible for them and what records exist when those decisions are questioned.

What is automated decision making?

Automated decision making occurs when a computer system produces an outcome that affects people, services or operations without a human making that specific decision. These systems often rely on algorithms, statistical models or machine learning to process information and generate outputs.

Why is AI governance important?

AI governance matters because automated systems can influence decisions about individuals, markets and public services. When those decisions are challenged, organisations must demonstrate how the system worked, what controls existed and who was responsible for oversight.

What is human oversight in AI systems?

Human oversight refers to the ability of people to monitor, review or intervene in automated decision systems. Effective oversight means humans can detect problems, review outcomes and stop or override the system when necessary.

What is an AI audit trail?

An AI audit trail is a record of how a system operated during a specific decision. It typically includes the inputs used, the steps taken by the system and the output that was produced. Audit trails allow investigators to reconstruct what happened when a decision is questioned.

What is accountability in AI systems?

Accountability in AI systems refers to the assignment of responsibility for how automated systems operate and how their decisions affect people. It ensures that a specific person or role can answer questions about the system’s design, use and oversight.

What is the EU AI Act?

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act is a regulatory framework developed by the European Union to govern the development and use of AI systems. It classifies AI systems according to risk and imposes obligations on organisations that develop or deploy certain types of systems.

Can AI decisions be challenged?

Yes. Decisions influenced by automated systems can be examined by regulators, courts or internal investigators. Organisations may need to explain how the system worked, what data was used and what controls were in place.

What records should exist for AI systems?

Organisations typically need documentation describing the system’s intended use, monitoring processes, decision records and logs that allow outcomes to be reconstructed later.

How do regulators investigate AI systems?

Regulators typically examine documentation, decision records, system logs and oversight processes to determine whether an organisation governed the system properly.

Does AI governance apply only to machine learning systems?

No. AI governance applies to any automated system that produces outputs affecting people or important operations. This includes statistical models, algorithmic decision systems and machine learning models.

Where can I learn AI governance concepts in plain English?

AI Governance Reference provides plain-English explanations of the key concepts used when automated decision systems are governed and investigated.

What is AI Governance Reference?

AI Governance Reference is a plain-English reference library that explains the concepts used to govern automated decision systems. It provides short, structured briefings on topics such as accountability, human oversight, audit trails and regulatory investigation so readers can understand how automated systems are examined when decisions affect people.

Who is the AI Governance Reference for?

The AI Governance Reference is designed for anyone who needs to understand governance language quickly and clearly. This includes directors, managers, regulators, lawyers, journalists, students and researchers who encounter automated decision systems in their work.

What kind of information does the AI Governance Reference contain?

The AI Governance Reference contains short reference cards explaining individual governance concepts. Each card defines a concept, explains why it matters, describes where it appears in practice and outlines the risks that arise when it is missing or misunderstood.

How is the AI Governance Reference organised?

The cards are organised across five areas that together describe how automated decisions are governed:
  • System properties
  • Authority and responsibility
  • Operational controls
  • Records and evidence
  • Legal landscape
These areas reflect how automated decisions are designed, operated and investigated.

Do I need to read the AI Governance Reference in order?

No. Each card is designed to stand alone. You can look up a single concept and understand it without reading other cards. However, related cards are linked together so readers can explore how concepts connect.

Is the AI Governance Reference a governance framework?

No. The AI Governance Reference does not prescribe frameworks, policies or compliance programmes. It explains the concepts that appear in governance discussions and investigations.

Is the AI Governance Reference legal advice?

No. The AI Governance Reference explains governance concepts and the legal landscape in general terms. It does not provide legal advice or interpret specific legislation.

Why does AI governance matter?

Automated systems increasingly influence decisions about people, services and markets. When those decisions are questioned, organisations must demonstrate how the system worked, who was responsible and what records exist. Governance determines whether those questions can be answered.

Why does the AI Governance Reference focus on records and evidence?

When automated decisions are challenged, investigators and regulators rely on records to reconstruct what happened. Documentation, logs and decision records often determine whether an organisation can demonstrate responsible governance.

Does the AI Governance Reference explain specific laws such as the EU AI Act?

Yes. The AI Governance Reference includes Legal Landscape cards that describe areas of law commonly triggered by automated systems, including the EU Artificial Intelligence Act, data protection law, discrimination law and product liability.

Can the AI Governance Reference help organisations build governance systems?

The AI Governance Reference explains the concepts used in governance, but it does not instruct organisations how to design or implement governance programmes. It is intended as a reference, not a compliance guide.

How often is the AI Governance Reference updated?

The AI Governance Reference is updated periodically as governance practices, regulatory expectations and legal frameworks evolve.
