AI Governance Glossary | AI Governance Reference

This page provides the complete index of terms contained in the AI Governance Reference. Each card explains a concept used in discussions about AI governance, automated decision systems and accountability.

Automated systems increasingly influence decisions about people, services and markets. When those decisions are questioned, organisations must explain how the system worked, what it was allowed to do and who was responsible for oversight.

Understanding the language of governance is therefore essential. The cards listed below provide plain-English explanations of the concepts that appear in regulatory guidance, board discussions and legal proceedings when automated systems are examined.

SP-01 - Automated Decision Making

Automated decision making occurs when a system produces an output that affects a person — a credit decision, a risk score, a content removal — without a human making that specific choice. Understanding what constitutes an automated decision is the starting point for every governance, legal and accountability obligation that follows.

SP-02 - Training Data

Training data is what a system learned from. The patterns, gaps, errors and biases in that data are built into the system's outputs. Governance of automated decisions cannot begin without understanding what the system was trained on, where that data came from, and what it may have embedded.

SP-03 - System Limits

Every automated system has a range within which it performs reliably and conditions under which it should not be used. System limits define those boundaries. Deploying a system outside its limits — whether through scope creep, pressure or oversight — is a governance failure that regulators and courts examine closely.

SP-04 - Data Sources

The origin of the data a system uses determines the reliability and legitimacy of the decisions it produces. Data sources must be identified, documented and assessed for quality, legality and potential bias. An organisation that cannot account for where its system's data came from cannot account for what the system does.

SP-05 - Data Quality

Poor quality data produces unreliable decisions regardless of the sophistication of the system processing it. Data quality covers accuracy, completeness, consistency and currency. It is a governance obligation — not just a technical concern — because decisions made on poor data expose organisations to legal and regulatory challenge.

SP-06 - Dataset Bias

Dataset bias is a systematic pattern in training data that causes a system to treat people differently based on characteristics such as race, gender, age or socioeconomic status. Because the bias is structural rather than intentional, it persists in outputs regardless of what the organisation intended. Identifying and documenting dataset bias is a governance prerequisite.

SP-07 - Model Updates

When a system is updated — whether through retraining, new data or changed parameters — its behaviour changes. Those changes require the same governance scrutiny as the original deployment decision. Organisations that govern initial deployment but not subsequent updates have a structural governance gap.

SP-08 - System Scope

System scope is the defined boundary of what a system is authorised to do and the decisions it is authorised to make. Deployment outside that boundary — to new populations, use cases or decision types — is not a technical matter. It is an accountability and legal matter that requires formal approval and documented governance.

SP-09 - Intended Use

Intended use is the specific purpose for which a system was designed, tested and approved. It defines the limits of legitimate deployment. When a system is used for purposes beyond its intended use — even by the organisation that built it — the governance protections designed for the intended use no longer apply.

SP-10 - Unintended Use

Unintended use occurs when a system is applied to purposes, populations or contexts it was not designed for. It is one of the most common sources of AI harm and governance failure. Accountability for unintended use rests with the deploying organisation, not the system's developer.

SP-11 - Third-Party Systems

Third-party systems are components — data feeds, models, APIs, platforms — built and maintained by external parties but incorporated into an organisation's AI deployment. Using components built by others does not transfer responsibility for what those components do. The deploying organisation remains accountable for the full system's outputs.

SP-12 - Vendor Models

A vendor model is an AI model built by an external supplier and deployed by the organisation using it. The governance obligations attached to that model's outputs — explainability, fairness, audit trails, human oversight — belong to the deploying organisation. Vendor contracts cannot reassign those obligations away.

SP-13 - System Inputs

System inputs are the data fed into a system at the point of making a decision. Input quality, provenance and completeness directly determine output quality. Governance requires that inputs are documented, validated and retained — because without the inputs, the decision cannot be reconstructed or challenged.

SP-14 - System Outputs

System outputs are the decisions, scores, classifications and recommendations an automated system produces. They are the point at which governance obligations become concrete — the point at which a person is affected, a right is engaged, and accountability becomes real. Output governance begins with knowing exactly what the system produces and when.

SP-15 - Transparency

Transparency in AI governance means being open — in specific, accessible terms — about what a system does, what data it uses, and how it reaches its outputs. Publishing a general description of a system is not transparency. Transparency requires specificity, timing and accessibility: the right information must reach the right person at the right moment.

SP-16 - Explainability

Explainability is the ability to account for a specific decision in terms the affected person can understand and act on. A technical description of how a model works is not an explanation. An explanation must address the specific decision, the specific person, and the specific factors that produced that outcome. Regulators increasingly require it as a legal obligation.

SP-17 - Model Performance

Model performance measures how accurately a system does what it is supposed to do. Performance at deployment is not performance over time. Systems degrade, data drifts, contexts change. Ongoing performance monitoring is a governance requirement — not because the system may fail suddenly, but because it may fail gradually and invisibly.

SP-18 - Third-Party Risk

Third-party risk is the governance exposure created when parts of an AI system are built, maintained or operated by external suppliers. Outsourcing a component does not outsource accountability for what it does. Organisations that cannot demonstrate control over their third-party components cannot demonstrate control over their systems.

SP-19 - Fairness

Fairness in automated decision making has three distinct dimensions: equal treatment, equal outcomes and procedural fairness. Meeting one does not satisfy the others. Organisations must assess fairness across all three dimensions and document that assessment — because discrimination law, consumer protection law and financial regulation each address a different dimension.

AR-01 - Accountability

Accountability is the obligation of a named person to answer for what an automated system does. It must be assigned explicitly, documented formally, and understood by the person named. Accountability that exists only in an organisation chart or a vendor contract — without genuine authority and information — is not accountability in any regulatory or legal sense.

AR-02 - Approval Processes

Approval processes are the formal mechanisms through which decisions about an automated system — to build it, deploy it, change it or decommission it — are authorised. Formal sign-off distinguishes a governed decision from an assumed one. Without documented approval, accountability for deployment decisions cannot be established.

AR-03 - Human Oversight

Human oversight means a person with authority and information actively engaged with what an automated system does — not merely present near it. A person who receives a system's outputs, approves them routinely, or monitors without the power to intervene is not providing oversight. The standard requires genuine capability to act.

AR-04 - Decision Authority

Decision authority is the formal, documented power to make or approve specific decisions about an automated system. Authority that is assumed, shared informally or undocumented does not exist in governance terms. When something goes wrong, the absence of documented decision authority is the absence of accountability.

AR-05 - Role Definition

Role definition in AI governance means specifying the responsibilities, authorities and accountabilities attached to each position involved in a system's operation. General job descriptions are not sufficient. Governance requires roles defined specifically for each system, with named individuals who understand what is required of them.

AR-06 - Board Responsibility

Boards that approve AI deployment without understanding the risks, the system's capabilities, or the governance arrangements in place have not discharged their legal obligations as directors. Board responsibility for AI governance is not delegable. It requires informed decision making, documented scrutiny and ongoing oversight.

AR-07 - Management Responsibility

Management responsibility connects board-level accountability to the operational governance of individual systems. It requires that managers with day-to-day oversight of systems have the authority, information and resources to govern them — and that their accountability is documented rather than assumed from their general role.

AR-08 - Vendor Responsibility

An organisation that deploys an automated system is accountable for what that system does — regardless of whether it built the system itself or purchased it from a vendor. Vendor responsibility means the vendor may share some obligations, but the deploying organisation cannot contract its accountability away.

AR-09 - Delegated Authority

Delegated authority is the formal transfer of specific decision-making powers from one person to another. The person who delegates retains ultimate accountability. Delegation that is informal, undocumented or broader than intended creates responsibility gaps that become governance failures when examined under scrutiny.

AR-10 - Internal Governance

Internal governance is the structure — committees, policies, reporting lines, review processes — through which an organisation directs and controls its automated systems. Effective internal governance requires that the structure is operational, not merely documented, and that the people within it have genuine authority over the systems they are responsible for.

AR-11 - Policy vs Practice

The gap between policy and practice is where most governance failures actually occur. A written policy that staff do not follow, cannot access, or were never trained on is evidence of a governance gap rather than evidence of governance. Regulators assess whether policies are operational, not merely whether they exist.

AR-12 - Responsibility Gaps

Responsibility gaps are situations where no named person is clearly accountable for a specific aspect of an automated system's governance. They are the most common structural cause of AI governance failure. They arise most often at the boundaries between teams, between organisations and between the vendor relationship and internal oversight.

CS-01 - Monitoring

Monitoring is the ongoing observation of an automated system in operation. A system that runs without human attention runs without meaningful oversight. Monitoring must be active, capable of detecting problems, and connected to people with the authority to act on what they observe. Passive monitoring — logging without review — is not a governance control.

CS-02 - Escalation Procedures

Escalation procedures are the defined paths through which concerns about an automated system are raised, reviewed and resolved. Having a path that people know about and are willing to use is different from assuming concerns will surface naturally. Escalation procedures must be documented, communicated and tested to constitute a governance control.

CS-03 - Human Review

Human review means a person examining an automated decision with sufficient information, authority and independence to reach a different conclusion. A person who receives an output and approves it without scrutiny is not reviewing it. The standard requires that the reviewer can meaningfully engage with the decision — not merely observe it.

CS-04 - Intervention Authority

Intervention authority is the formally assigned power to act on what monitoring reveals — to override a decision, halt a process, change a system's parameters or stop it entirely. Oversight without the authority to intervene is observation. Governance requires both.

CS-05 - Operational Monitoring

Operational monitoring is the continuous observation of a live system during normal operation — distinct from periodic audit or review. It detects problems in real time rather than after the fact. The people doing the monitoring must have both the information to identify problems and the authority to respond to them.

CS-06 - Exception Handling

Automated systems will encounter cases they were not designed for. Exception handling is the defined process for identifying, routing and resolving those cases. Without a functioning exception handling process, edge cases either fall through governance or are processed by a system operating outside its reliable range.

CS-07 - Alert Systems

Alert systems are the mechanisms that trigger human attention when an automated system behaves unexpectedly or breaches defined thresholds. An alert that nobody receives, nobody acts on, or that fires so frequently it is ignored is not a governance control. Alert systems require defined response procedures and named responders.

CS-08 - System Testing

System testing is the structured verification that an automated system performs as expected — before deployment and after any significant change. Testing governance requires that tests are documented, results are retained, failures are investigated, and the people who approved deployment can demonstrate what was tested and what was found.

CS-09 - Operational Reviews

Operational reviews are structured, periodic examinations of a live system's performance, governance and risk profile. They are distinct from continuous monitoring and from one-time deployment testing. Regular review — with documented findings and traceable responses — is a standard expectation in regulated sectors.

CS-10 - Change Management

Changes to automated systems — new data, updated models, changed parameters, modified integrations — alter what the system does. Change management requires that those alterations are governed with the same scrutiny as the original deployment. Unrecorded changes are one of the most common sources of accountability failure in AI systems.

CS-11 - System Shutdown

The ability to stop an automated system quickly, safely and completely is a governance requirement. System shutdown must be technically possible, operationally tested, and within the authority of a named person to execute without needing approval from the system's own chain of command. A system that cannot be stopped is not under human control.

CS-12 - Incident Escalation

Incident escalation is the path a significant system failure travels from first identification to formal organisational response. It requires a defined trigger, a named recipient, a documented process and a timeframe. Systems that rely on informal escalation — someone telling someone else — have no meaningful incident governance.

CS-13 - Human Override

Human override is the ability of a named person with appropriate authority to reverse or change a specific decision made by an automated system. Three conditions make it real: the person must have the authority to override, the information to evaluate the decision, and the practical ability to act before the decision takes effect.

RE-01 - Audit Trails

Audit trails are the chronological record of what an automated system did, when it did it, and what inputs produced each output. They are the foundation of post-hoc accountability. Without an intact audit trail, a decision cannot be reconstructed, a governance failure cannot be investigated, and an organisation cannot demonstrate what its system did.

RE-02 - Decision Records

Decision records document individual automated decisions — what data was used, what the system produced, and when. They are the only way to reconstruct what happened in a specific case. When an individual challenges a decision, or a regulator investigates a pattern, decision records are the primary evidence of governance.

RE-03 - System Logs

System logs are the technical records an automated system generates during operation. They differ from governance records: a system log records what the system processed; a governance record establishes accountability for what the system did. Both are required, but they serve different purposes and must not be confused.

RE-04 - Incident Reporting

Incident reporting is the formal documentation of system failures, harmful outputs and governance breaches. It is both a governance obligation and the starting point for learning from failure. Organisations that do not report incidents cannot demonstrate that they detected them — and those that detected but did not report face regulatory exposure.

RE-05 - Evidence Preservation

Evidence preservation is the immediate securing of records after an incident — before they can be overwritten, deleted or altered. The window between incident identification and evidence loss is short. Governance requires a defined evidence preservation process, because records created after the fact are not the same as records that existed at the time.

RE-06 - Investigation Records

Investigation records document how an inquiry was conducted, what was examined, what was found, and what was decided. For an investigation to produce findings that withstand scrutiny — regulatory, legal or internal — its methodology and conclusions must be documented in a form that independent parties can review and challenge.

RE-07 - Documentation

Documentation in AI governance means maintaining the records that allow a system to be understood, accounted for and governed — not simply the act of writing things down. The test is whether the documentation that exists would allow an external party to reconstruct what the system does, why it was deployed, and what governance was in place.

RE-08 - Data Retention

Data retention defines how long records must be kept and the conditions under which they can be deleted. Governance requires that retention periods are defined before records are created — not decided retrospectively when records become inconvenient. Premature deletion of governance records is, in many jurisdictions, itself a legal breach.

RE-09 - Record Accuracy

Record accuracy means that governance records describe what actually happened — not what should have happened, what the organisation intended, or what a review concluded after the fact. A record altered to reflect a desired outcome rather than an actual event is not a governance record. It is evidence of a governance failure.

RE-10 - System Explanations

System explanations are accounts of why an automated system reached a specific decision for a specific person. They are distinct from technical descriptions of how a system works. A system explanation must be specific, accessible and actionable — sufficient for the person affected to understand and challenge the decision.

RE-11 - Internal Reviews

Internal reviews are periodic structured examinations of an automated system's governance, performance and compliance. A review that produces no documentation of what was examined, what was found, or what was changed is indistinguishable from no review at all. The record of the review is as important as the review itself.

RE-12 - External Investigation

External investigation occurs when a regulator, court or independent body examines an automated system and the governance that surrounded it. Organisations face two requirements: the substantive governance must have existed, and the records demonstrating that governance must be retrievable. Neither is sufficient without the other.

RE-13 - System Documentation

System documentation is the complete record of an automated system's design, training, testing, deployment, governance and performance. It differs from the documentation that describes the system to users. Regulators increasingly require system documentation as a compliance obligation — and its absence, in a high-risk deployment, is itself a regulatory breach.

LR-01 - Automated Decision Rights

Automated decision rights are the legal entitlements individuals hold when systems make decisions about them — rights to explanation, to contest a decision, to human review and to non-discrimination. These rights exist in data protection law, consumer law, financial regulation and equality law. They apply now, not when future AI legislation arrives.

LR-02 - Regulatory Oversight

Multiple regulatory bodies have authority to examine, investigate and act on automated decision systems. Understanding which regulators have jurisdiction — and what powers they hold — is a prerequisite for governance. Regulatory oversight is not a future risk. It is a current condition that organisations operating automated systems already face.

LR-03 - Compliance Duties

Compliance with legal requirements is the minimum the law demands. It is not a standard of good governance. The distinction matters because regulators assess conduct against both, and because the gap between legal compliance and genuine accountability is where the most significant governance failures are found.

LR-04 - Liability

When automated systems cause harm — financial loss, discrimination, privacy breach, physical injury — the legal and financial consequences attach to the organisations that deployed and controlled those systems. Liability does not transfer to the system's developer simply because the deployer did not build it. Governance determines who bears exposure.

LR-05 - Record-Keeping Duties

Record-keeping duties are the specific legal obligations — under data protection law, financial regulation, employment law and others — to maintain defined records for defined periods. They differ from governance record-keeping in that they are mandatory minimums, not governance aspirations. Failing to meet them is itself a legal breach.

LR-06 - Regulatory Investigation

Regulatory investigation means a formal examination by an authority with statutory powers to demand documents, interview staff and compel cooperation. Organisations facing investigation must be able to produce governance records quickly and completely. Governance arrangements that look adequate at deployment but cannot be demonstrated under investigation do not satisfy the standard.

LR-07 - Enforcement Powers

Enforcement powers are the actions available to regulators when they find that an organisation has failed to meet its legal obligations — including requiring system changes, imposing financial penalties, suspending operations and pursuing personal liability against individuals. The range and severity of powers varies by regulator and jurisdiction.

LR-08 - Legal Challenges

Legal challenges are the formal mechanisms through which individuals and organisations can contest automated decisions in court. They include judicial review of public body decisions, discrimination claims, data protection rights enforcement and contractual challenges. An organisation that cannot reconstruct and justify a challenged decision is in a structurally weak legal position.

LR-09 - Consumer Protection

Consumer protection law applies when automated systems influence what people buy, what they pay, or what choices they believe they are making. Misleading AI-driven recommendations, algorithmic pricing that disadvantages specific groups, and opaque automated offers all engage consumer protection obligations that exist independently of AI-specific regulation.

LR-10 - Data Protection Duties

Data protection duties apply whenever an automated system processes personal information. They include obligations to minimise data collection, maintain accuracy, limit retention, respond to individual rights requests, and conduct impact assessments for high-risk processing. These are current legal obligations in every major jurisdiction.

LR-11 - Regulatory Reporting

Regulatory reporting is the obligation to proactively inform regulators about incidents, significant changes to systems, and material risks. It differs from responding to investigation. Organisations that self-report promptly are treated differently from those whose failures are discovered by regulators. Governance must include defined reporting triggers and processes.

LR-12 - System Suspension

System suspension is the power of a regulator or court to order an automated system to stop operating. It can be temporary pending investigation or permanent following a finding of breach. Organisations that cannot stop their systems quickly, safely and completely face additional risk when suspension orders are issued.

LR-13 - Risk Classification

Risk classification assigns different governance and legal obligations to automated systems based on their potential for harm. High-risk systems — those affecting employment, credit, health, safety or fundamental rights — face the most demanding requirements. Classification is a governance prerequisite: it determines what obligations apply before deployment begins.

GV-01 - Audit

An audit is a structured, independent examination of an organisation's systems, processes and records against defined standards. It differs from monitoring, internal review and certification. Audit findings carry weight precisely because of their independence. The governance value of an audit depends entirely on the independence and scope of the examiner.

GV-02 - Bias

Bias in automated systems is systematic error — a consistent pattern of outputs that disadvantages specific groups. It is a governance problem because it is not self-correcting: biased systems produce biased outcomes repeatedly, at scale, without human intervention. Bias assessment, documentation and remediation are governance obligations, not optional quality improvements.

GV-03 - Harm

Harm is the purpose that governance exists to prevent. In automated decision making it takes multiple forms — financial loss, discrimination, reputational damage, denial of services, physical injury. Governance structures are assessed not by their design but by whether they prevented harm, detected it promptly, and responded to it appropriately.

GV-N05 - What Transparency Is Not

Transparency is not the same as disclosure. Publishing a system description, a model card or a privacy notice does not make a system transparent. Transparency requires that the right information reaches the right person at the right time — specifically enough to be meaningful, and accessibly enough to be actionable.

GV-N06 - What Fairness Is Not

Treating everyone the same is not the same as treating everyone fairly. Fairness requires equal outcomes as well as equal treatment — and a system that applies identical rules to groups with different starting positions can produce deeply unequal results. Stated commitments to fairness, diversity principles and bias training are not evidence of fair outcomes.

GV-N07 - What Explainability Is Not

Access to technical documentation is not an explanation. A feature importance score, a model architecture summary or a system description does not explain why a specific decision was made about a specific person. Explainability requires a decision-specific account — one the affected person can understand, evaluate and act on.

GV-N08 - What A Standard Is Not

Adopting a standard, referencing it in documentation or aligning with its principles does not constitute compliance. A standard defines what demonstrated conformance requires. Organisations that describe themselves as following a standard without evidence of conformance are making claims their governance cannot support.

GV-N09 - What A Framework Is Not

A governance framework is a set of principles, structures and processes. It is a precondition for governance, not governance itself. The gap between having a framework and operating one is where most AI governance failures occur. Regulators and courts assess what the organisation demonstrably did — not what its framework said it would do.

GV-N10 - What Certification Is Not

Certification records conformance with a defined standard at the point of assessment. It does not confirm that a system remains compliant after changes, after new data, or after deployment in contexts the assessment did not examine. A certificate cannot substitute for ongoing governance, monitoring and accountability.

GV-N13 - What An Audit Is Not

A periodic audit is not a substitute for continuous governance. An audit examines what it can reach during the time available. It cannot detect problems that occurred between audits, assess governance that was not documented, or substitute for the monitoring and oversight controls that should operate continuously.

GV-N14 - What A Risk Assessment Is Not

Completing a risk assessment is not the same as managing risk. A risk assessment identifies and documents risks. What happens next — the controls implemented, the decisions made, the monitoring in place — is the governance. An organisation that can produce a risk assessment but cannot demonstrate what it did in response is in a weak position under investigation.

GV-N15 - What A Model Card Is Not

A model card is a summary document describing a system's intended behaviour, training data and known limitations. It is documentation of what the developer intended — not evidence of what the deployed system does. A model card does not constitute the governance documentation that regulation and accountability require.

AR-N01 - What Accountability Is Not

Accountability is not an org chart entry, a named committee or a vendor contract clause. It requires that a specific named person had genuine authority over the system, adequate information about its operation, and real capacity to act when problems arose. Accountability that cannot be demonstrated through evidence did not exist in governance terms.

AR-N04 - What Human Oversight Is Not

Human oversight is not proximity to an automated system. A person who monitors a dashboard without authority to intervene, approves outputs without scrutinising them, or reviews decisions without adequate information is not providing oversight. The standard requires genuine capability — authority, information and practical ability to act.

AR-N11 - What A Policy Is Not

A written policy is not evidence of governance. It is evidence that governance was intended. The governance question is whether the policy was followed, whether staff knew about it, whether it was updated when the system changed, and whether compliance with it can be demonstrated. Policies that cannot answer those questions are records of aspiration, not governance.

LR-N03 - What Automated Decision Rights Are Not

Individual rights regarding automated decisions — to explanation, to human review, to contest — are not satisfied by offering a complaints process. Rights are legally enforceable entitlements, not discretionary services. An organisation that provides a complaints channel but cannot deliver explanation, review or meaningful redress has not provided the rights individuals are entitled to.

LR-N12 - What Compliance Is Not

Meeting legal minimum requirements is not good governance. Legal compliance sets the floor — what the organisation must do to avoid enforcement action. Governance requires more: the genuine accountability, oversight and evidential standards that allow decisions to be examined and justified. Compliance and governance are not the same problem.

SP-N02 - What Intended Use Is Not

A vendor's description of what a system is designed to do is not a defined and documented deployment purpose. Intended use must be specified by the deploying organisation — for its specific context, populations and decision types. A marketing description, a system prompt or a general capability statement does not constitute a governance definition of intended use.

GQ-01 - Can A Real Person Override An AI Decision?

The first governance question tests override authority in practice, not in principle. Can a named person, with sufficient authority and information, reverse a specific automated decision before it takes effect? The test is operational — whether the capability exists and has been demonstrated — not whether an override policy appears in documentation.

GQ-02 - Can You Reconstruct A Specific Decision?

The second governance question tests whether a specific past decision can be reconstructed from records that existed at the time. Reconstruction requires the inputs, the system state, the decision and the rationale. The inability to reconstruct a specific decision is a governance failure — not a technical limitation that documentation can explain away.

GQ-03 - Do You Keep Proper Records?

The third governance question tests whether the records an organisation holds would survive independent scrutiny — complete, accurate, unaltered and retrievable on demand. Records that were created after the fact, amended after a problem arose, or that cannot be located when needed are not governance records.

GQ-04 - Is Responsibility Formally Assigned?

The fourth governance question tests whether accountability is formally documented — in writing, attached to a named individual, understood by that person. Responsibility that is assumed from seniority, distributed across a team or implied by a job title does not satisfy the standard. Governance requires explicit, verifiable assignment.

GQ-05 - Can You Stop The System?

The fifth governance question tests shutdown capability in practice. Can a named person stop the system, with the authority and means to act, independently of the system's own chain of command? A system that can only be stopped through a lengthy approval process, or by the vendor, or not at all, is not under genuine human control.

GR-00 - What Governance Is Not

Governance is not a synonym for oversight, compliance, frameworks or controls. Those are components of governance — inputs that governance requires. Governance is the demonstrated ability to direct, account for and correct what an automated system does. The test is not what structures exist but whether they function and whether that function can be shown.

LL-00 - The Legal Landscape

Law and governance are the same problem viewed from different directions. Law defines the minimum that organisations must demonstrate. Governance determines whether they can. The legal landscape series maps the frameworks that automated decision systems commonly trigger — not as future risks, but as current obligations already in force.

LL-01 - EU Artificial Intelligence Act

The EU AI Act is the first binding legal framework designed specifically for AI systems. It classifies systems by risk, prohibits the highest-risk applications outright, and imposes conformity requirements, transparency obligations, human oversight standards and documentation duties on high-risk deployments. It applies from 2024 and is already shaping AI governance globally.

LL-02 - Data Protection and Privacy

Data protection and privacy law applies to any automated system that processes personal information — which includes most systems that make decisions about individuals. GDPR, UK GDPR, PIPL and equivalent frameworks impose obligations on automated decision making including rights to explanation, human review and non-discrimination that are enforceable now.

LL-03 - Discrimination and Civil Rights

Discrimination law applies when automated systems produce outcomes that disadvantage people on the basis of protected characteristics. The standard is outcomes-based, not intent-based. A system that produces discriminatory results — in employment, credit, housing or services — is in breach regardless of whether discrimination was intended or the system was designed neutrally.

LL-04 - Consumer Protection

Consumer protection law applies when automated systems influence what people buy, what they pay, or what choices they believe are available to them. Misleading AI recommendations, algorithmic pricing manipulation and deceptive automated communications are actionable under consumer law frameworks that predate AI but apply to it fully.

LL-05 - Product Safety and Liability

Product safety and liability law applies when an AI system is embedded in a product or performs functions that affect physical safety. The system inherits the safety obligations of the product it is part of. Liability for harm caused by a defective AI-enabled product rests with the organisation that placed it on the market.

LL-06 - Competition and Antitrust

Competition and antitrust law applies when algorithmic systems coordinate pricing or market behaviour in ways that would be unlawful if agreed between competitors explicitly. Price coordination through shared algorithms raises the same legal questions as a cartel. Regulators in the EU, US and UK are actively investigating AI-enabled competition concerns.

LL-07 - Financial Regulation

Financial regulation applies to AI systems used in financial services with full force. Decades of conduct, audit and oversight obligations — treating customers fairly, managing model risk, maintaining records, reporting to regulators — apply to AI-driven decisions in lending, insurance, investment and payments without modification.

LL-08 - Cybersecurity and Critical Infrastructure

Cybersecurity and critical infrastructure law imposes resilience obligations on AI systems embedded in essential services — energy, water, transport, finance, healthcare, communications. These obligations include incident reporting, recovery planning and demonstrable control over system behaviour under adverse conditions.

LL-09 - Corporate Governance and Director Duties

Corporate governance law and director duties apply when boards approve the deployment of automated systems without adequate scrutiny of the risks. A director who approves AI deployment without understanding its governance implications may have breached the duty of care. Senior manager accountability regimes in financial services impose personal liability for AI governance failures.

LL-10 - Securities and Disclosure

Securities and disclosure law applies when organisations make statements about their AI capabilities or risks to investors, in prospectuses, annual reports or public communications. Overstating AI capabilities, understating AI risks, or failing to disclose material AI-related governance failures creates legal exposure for organisations and their officers.

LL-11 - Sector-Specific Regulation

Sector-specific regulation applies where AI systems perform functions that are already regulated — medical diagnosis, financial advice, legal services, employment screening. The AI system inherits the full regulatory framework of the sector it operates in, in addition to any AI-specific requirements. Regulated functions require regulated governance.

JX-00 - Jurisdiction Series

Governance concepts are universal. The legal obligations that enforce them are not. The Jurisdiction Series maps the regulatory environment in thirteen countries and regions — identifying the frameworks in force, the regulators with enforcement authority, what triggers obligations, and how those obligations connect to the Atlas governance concepts.

JX-01 - United Kingdom

The United Kingdom has chosen a sector-led approach to AI governance rather than a single comprehensive AI law. Binding obligations arise from UK GDPR, the Equality Act, consumer law, financial regulation and the Companies Act — enforced by the ICO, FCA, CMA, EHRC and others. The regulatory environment is active and the obligations are real.

JX-02 - United States

The United States has no federal AI law but has a dense landscape of existing obligations — FTC consumer protection, SEC disclosure requirements, EEOC employment discrimination, CFPB credit decision rules — alongside rapidly expanding state-level AI legislation in Colorado, California, New York and others. Federal and state enforcement are both active.

JX-03 - China

China has built one of the world's most detailed AI regulatory frameworks — specific regulations governing algorithmic recommendations, generative AI, deep synthesis and personal data, all enforced by the Cyberspace Administration of China. The framework operates on different principles from Western models but imposes comparable governance demands within its jurisdiction.

JX-04 - Canada

Canada's AI governance landscape combines PIPEDA, Quebec's Law 25 and the Treasury Board's Directive on Automated Decision-Making with a proposed federal AI Act that stalled in 2025. Existing obligations are real and enforced. Quebec's Law 25 imposes specific requirements for automated decision-making that apply to any organisation processing data of Quebec residents.

JX-05 - Brazil

Brazil enacted a comprehensive AI Law in 2024 alongside the already-enforced LGPD data protection framework. The AI Law adopts a risk-based classification model aligned with the EU AI Act and imposes conformity, transparency, human oversight and accountability obligations on high-risk AI deployments. South America's most developed AI regulatory framework.

JX-06 - Singapore

Singapore has the world's most mature voluntary AI governance framework — the Model AI Governance Framework and FEAT principles — backed by binding sector regulation from MAS for financial services and PDPA enforcement from the PDPC. Singapore's approach is internationally influential and its financial services AI governance is among the most specific anywhere.

JX-07 - Japan

Japan enacted its AI Promotion Act in 2025, establishing a legislative foundation for AI governance while maintaining a principles-based, innovation-friendly approach. Existing obligations under the Act on Protection of Personal Information and sector regulation from the FSA and JFTC apply now. Japan's Hiroshima AI Process has shaped international AI governance norms.

JX-08 - South Korea

South Korea's AI Basic Act, taking full effect in 2026, establishes a comprehensive risk-based framework for AI governance — one of the few binding AI-specific laws outside the EU. Alongside the reformed PIPA and active sector regulation from the FSC and PIPC, South Korea has one of the most developed AI legal environments in the Asia-Pacific region.

JX-09 - India

India's AI governance framework is developing rapidly. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act creates current data protection obligations, while RBI model risk management guidance applies to financial institutions deploying AI. The National AI Mission signals significant government investment in AI capability alongside developing governance infrastructure.

JX-10 - Australia

Australia's AI governance framework combines the Privacy Act, Australian Consumer Law and the Corporations Act with active enforcement from the OAIC, ACCC and ASIC. APRA and ASIC have published specific AI governance expectations for financial services. Proposed mandatory guardrails for high-risk AI are under active government consultation.

JX-11 - South Africa

South Africa's AI governance framework is rooted in constitutional rights — to equality, dignity and privacy — backed by POPIA enforcement from the Information Regulator. Constitutional rights apply directly to automated decision systems and have been litigated. POPIA's human review rights for automated decisions are among the most clearly defined on the African continent.

JX-12 - Nigeria

Nigeria has the African continent's most active data protection enforcement regime. The Nigeria Data Protection Act, combined with the NDPC's September 2025 enforcement framework, makes mandatory Data Protection Impact Assessments a legal requirement for automated decision-making systems. CBN guidance adds specific obligations for AI in financial services.

JX-13 - Kenya

Kenya's Data Protection Act 2019 is actively enforced by the ODPC, with specific rights for individuals subject to automated decisions. The National AI Strategy 2025–2030 and Draft AI Code of Practice signal developing mandatory requirements. Kenya is moving faster on AI governance than most African jurisdictions and has positioned itself as a regional reference point.

SP-20 - Data Drift

Data drift occurs when the real-world data a system encounters in production diverges from its training data. The system continues operating while its outputs become less reliable. Detecting and responding to drift is a monitoring obligation, not a technical afterthought.

SP-21 - Model Drift

Model drift is the progressive deterioration of a deployed system's accuracy over time — not because inputs have changed, but because the relationships the model learned no longer hold. Without ongoing performance monitoring, model drift is invisible until the harm it causes is not.

SP-22 - Provenance

Provenance is the documented history of data — where it came from, who created it, what was done to it before it reached the system. Without provenance, the reliability and legitimacy of a system's outputs cannot be established or defended under regulatory scrutiny.

SP-23 - Robustness

Robustness is a system's ability to perform reliably under conditions that deviate from the expected — adversarial inputs, edge cases, distributional shift, partial failures. A system that functions well in testing but degrades under real-world stress has not been governed for the conditions it actually faces.

SP-24 - Deployment Context

Deployment context is the specific environment in which an automated system operates — the population it affects, the decisions it influences, and the regulatory environment that applies. Governance obligations are not abstract. They attach to the specific context in which a system is deployed.

CS-14 - Manual Fallback

Manual fallback is the defined, tested capability to continue operating by human means when an automated system fails, is suspended or must be stopped. Without it, system shutdown is not a governance control — it is an operational crisis that cannot be exercised in practice.

CS-15 - Access Control

Access control governs who can interact with an automated system — who can read its outputs, change its parameters, retrain its model, or override its decisions. Without defined and enforced access controls, actions cannot be attributed to individuals and accountability cannot be established.

RE-14 - Traceability

Traceability is the ability to follow a decision — forward from inputs to outputs, and backward from outputs to inputs, training data and governance decisions. Without traceability, accountability has no evidential foundation and decisions cannot be explained, challenged or defended.

LR-14 - Contestability

Contestability is the practical ability to challenge an automated decision — not merely the legal right to do so. The right exists in law. The ability depends on whether the organisation has built the processes, records and human review capacity to make challenge genuinely possible.

LR-15 - Redress

Redress is what happens after a successful challenge — the correction, the remedy, the reversal of harm. Without effective redress, contestability has no consequence, accountability has no meaning, and the people harmed by automated decisions have no practical recourse.

SP-25 - System Architecture

The governance implications of an automated system depend on its architecture. Explainability, reconstruction, oversight and third-party risk each operate differently across rules-based systems, machine learning models, large language models, RAG systems, fine-tuned models and API-accessed models.

JX-14 - European Union

The EU AI Act and GDPR together form the most comprehensive binding AI governance framework in the world, with extraterritorial reach that makes them the de facto global baseline. High-risk AI systems face mandatory conformity assessment, logging, human oversight and documentation obligations.

SP-26 - Distribution Shift

Distribution shift occurs when the real-world environment a system operates in differs materially from its training environment. Unlike data drift, it can occur from the moment of deployment. A system trained on one population or context may produce unreliable outputs when applied to another.

SP-27 - Feedback Loops

Feedback loops arise when a system's outputs influence the data used to evaluate or retrain it. The system begins to reinforce its own past decisions, potentially entrenching bias and distorting performance metrics. Standard evaluation metrics will not detect this — the loop makes the bias appear validated.

SP-28 - Automation Bias

Automation bias occurs when human operators defer to system outputs without adequate scrutiny. The presence of a human reviewer does not automatically provide meaningful oversight — it may simply record that a human was present. Governance must design for genuine oversight, not nominal compliance.

SP-29 - Silent Degradation

Silent degradation occurs when system performance declines gradually without producing obvious failures. The system continues producing plausible outputs while accuracy deteriorates. No alarm sounds. The failure is only visible through active performance measurement against a defined baseline.

SP-30 - Deployment Authorisation

Deployment authorisation is the formal recorded decision to put an automated system into operation — documenting who approved it, on what basis, and under what conditions. Without it, no one can demonstrate who started the system running or whether the organisation exercised meaningful governance over that decision.

MK-01 - Corporate Directors

Directors are accountable for the systems their organisations deploy — including automated ones they may never have seen. This card explains the governance obligations, professional stakes, decision lens and typical failure points of corporate directors in relation to AI governance.

MK-02 - Compliance Officers

Compliance officers translate regulatory obligations into operational controls. This card explains how compliance professionals engage with AI governance — the frameworks they map, the evidence they require, their role in investigations and the failure points most likely to affect them.

MK-03 - AI Engineers

AI engineers build and operate the systems that governance frameworks are designed to address. This card explains the governance implications of technical decisions, the evidence engineers produce and rely on, and the failure points that arise when technical choices are made without governance context.

MK-04 - Regulators

Regulators set the standards AI governance must meet and enforce them when organisations fall short. This card explains the regulatory perspective on AI governance — the evidence regulators require, how investigations proceed, and the failure points most likely to result in enforcement action.

MK-05 - Internal Auditors

Internal auditors provide independent assurance that governance controls are functioning as designed. This card explains how internal audit engages with AI governance — the evidence it requires, the methodological challenges it faces, and the failure points that produce false assurance.

MK-06 - Litigation Lawyers

Litigation lawyers act for parties in disputes involving automated systems. This card explains how litigation lawyers engage with AI governance evidence — the causation and liability questions they pursue, the records they require, and the failure points that determine whether governance holds up in court.

GF-01 - Deployment Without Formal Approval

Systems introduced into operation without a documented decision authorising their use. The accountability chain has no starting point — no named individual who reviewed the system, assessed its risks and signed off.

GF-02 - Responsibility Without Authority

A person is named responsible for a system but lacks the authority to govern or intervene in it. Accountability theatre — a named individual satisfies the governance requirement but is structurally prevented from discharging the responsibility assigned.

GF-03 - Oversight Without Intervention

Human oversight exists in principle but the reviewer lacks the ability to change or stop system decisions. The oversight is recorded. It is not real.

GF-04 - Policy Without Practice

Governance policies exist but are not followed operationally. The organisation documented what it should do and did not do it — a more serious evidential position than having no policy.

GF-05 - Monitoring Without Response

System monitoring exists but alerts and signals do not trigger meaningful action. The monitoring loop is open: signals go in, responses do not come out. Monitoring without response is documented negligence.

GF-06 - Vendor Reliance Without Governance

An organisation assumes vendor assurances substitute for internal governance. The vendor's documentation describes the vendor's governance of the system — not the deploying organisation's governance of the deployment.

GF-07 - System Scope Expansion Without Approval

A system is used for purposes beyond those originally authorised. The governance that authorised the original use does not govern the expanded use. The organisation may not know this is happening.

GF-08 - Change Without Governance

Models, data or system parameters change without formal review or approval. The compliance assessment and legal analysis were established for a system that no longer exists.

GF-09 - Records Created After the Event

Governance records appear only after an incident or investigation has begun. Contemporaneous records carry evidential weight that retrospective records do not — and post-event records may become evidence of the original failure and an attempt to conceal it.

GF-10 - Incomplete Decision Reconstruction

The organisation cannot reconstruct how a specific decision was produced. It can describe how the system generally works but cannot describe what happened in this specific case.

GF-11 - Responsibility Gaps Between Teams

Governance failures occur at organisational boundaries where no team owns the issue. Each team may be doing its job correctly within its scope. The failure sits in the spaces between scopes.

GF-12 - Human Oversight in Name Only

A human reviewer exists but lacks information, authority or time to intervene meaningfully. The human review is real as a process. It is not real as oversight.

GF-13 - Monitoring Without Context

Operational data is collected but not interpreted in a way that reveals governance risk. The monitoring produces data that confirms operation rather than data that reveals governance condition.

GF-14 - Governance Lost in System Integration

Multiple components and vendors combine to create a system no single party fully governs. Governance of each part is not governance of their combination.

GF-15 - Control Without Documentation

Governance actions occur but are not recorded in a way that can later be demonstrated. Governance that cannot be demonstrated is, for accountability purposes, governance that did not occur.
