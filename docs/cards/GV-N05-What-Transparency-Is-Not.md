# GV-N05 — What Transparency Is Not

> **The information is available. Now check: can anyone actually use it — or has availability been mistaken for transparency?**

---

## What It Is Not

Transparency is not publication. It is not a privacy policy, a terms of service document, a technical white paper, an algorithmic impact assessment posted on a website, or a press release about responsible AI. It is not satisfied by making information available in a form that the people it is meant to serve cannot find, cannot access, or cannot understand.

Transparency, properly understood, is the condition in which the people affected by an automated system have the information they need to understand what is being done to them, why, and on what basis — in a form they can actually use.

What organisations most often have instead is one or more of the following:

**Disclosure without comprehension.** A document exists. It is technically available. It describes the automated system in terms that require legal training to parse, technical expertise to interpret, or sustained effort to locate. The organisation has disclosed. The individual affected by the system has not been informed. These are different things. Disclosure is an act. Transparency is an outcome — the outcome of understanding in the person the information was meant to reach.

**Volume that defeats understanding.** An organisation publishes extensive documentation about its automated systems — technical specifications, governance frameworks, model cards, data processing notices. The volume is impressive. The individual trying to understand why a decision was made about them must navigate hundreds of pages of material none of which directly addresses their question. Transparency requires that the right information reach the right person in a form that is useful to them. A library of documents does not achieve that.

**Jargon that excludes.** Information about automated systems is frequently expressed in technical or legal language that is inaccessible to the individuals most affected by those systems. A notice explaining that decisions are made using "a regularised gradient boosted ensemble model trained on historical behavioural data to generate propensity scores calibrated against observed outcomes" has disclosed something. It has not communicated anything to the person who received a refusal and wants to know why.

**Transparency directed at the wrong audience.** Organisations sometimes achieve genuine transparency with sophisticated audiences — regulators, academics, technical reviewers — while remaining opaque to the individuals whose lives their systems affect. Transparency toward a regulator is valuable. It does not substitute for transparency toward the person whose credit was refused, whose application was rejected, or whose benefit was denied.

**Transparency that arrives too late or in the wrong moment.** Information provided after a decision has been made and cannot be challenged, or provided in a medium the individual does not use, or provided in circumstances where they have neither the time nor the capacity to engage with it, does not serve the purpose of transparency. The information existed. It did not function.

---

## Why Confusion Matters

When organisations mistake publication for transparency, they invest in producing documents rather than in achieving understanding. The documents multiply. The understanding does not improve. The organisation believes it is transparent because it has published — and that belief removes the incentive to ask whether the people affected by its systems can actually understand what is being done to them.

The function of transparency in automated decision-making is not to satisfy an auditor or a regulator that information was disclosed. It is to put individuals in a position where they can exercise informed judgment about the decisions that affect them — including the judgment to challenge those decisions, to seek correction, or to seek help. Information that cannot be understood cannot do that. Publication that does not lead to understanding has not achieved transparency.

---

## Where Confusion Appears

**Privacy policies that satisfy legal form but not communicative purpose.** A privacy policy that meets every formal legal requirement for content while being written in language that most readers cannot understand and presented in a format most readers will not engage with has achieved legal compliance in form. It has not achieved transparency.

**Technical documentation published for accountability without being readable by affected individuals.** Algorithmic impact assessments, model documentation, and governance frameworks are valuable accountability tools. When they are the primary or only transparency mechanism directed at affected individuals, they are transparency directed at the wrong audience in the wrong form.

**"Plain English" summaries that are not plain.** Organisations aware that their technical documentation is inaccessible sometimes produce summaries described as plain English. Those summaries, reviewed by the technical and legal teams that produced them, are plain relative to the full documentation — but still inaccessible to many of the individuals they are intended for.

**Transparency as a governance exercise rather than a communication one.** Organisations that treat transparency as a governance obligation to be met — documented, approved, published — rather than a communicative outcome to be achieved, produce the artefacts of transparency without its function. The document exists. Understanding does not follow.

---

## How To Tell The Difference

Five questions that distinguish genuine transparency from its substitutes:

- Can an individual affected by this system's decisions find out, in terms they can understand, that a decision about them was automated and why it produced the outcome it did — without professional assistance?
- Has the organisation tested whether its transparency mechanisms achieve understanding in the people they are directed at — not just whether they satisfy internal legal and governance review?
- Is transparency information provided at the moment it is most useful — when a decision is made and can still be questioned — rather than buried in a document the individual must locate independently?
- Is the language used in transparency communications calibrated to the comprehension level of the intended audience, not to the comfort level of the legal and technical teams that produced it?
- Does the organisation distinguish between transparency toward regulators and transparency toward affected individuals — and invest in both rather than treating one as a substitute for the other?

---

| Common Substitutes | What Transparency Actually Requires |
|---|---|
| A published privacy policy describing automated processing | Information that reaches the affected individual in a form they can understand and use |
| Technical documentation available on a website | Communication calibrated to the comprehension level of the people it is meant to inform |
| A jargon-compliant description of the system's logic | An explanation of what happened in this case that a non-specialist can follow |
| Volume of published material | The right information, for the right person, at the right moment, in the right form |
| Transparency toward regulators and technical reviewers | Transparency toward the individuals whose lives the system affects |

## The Law

Legal transparency obligations for automated systems are not satisfied by disclosure alone. Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require that information about automated processing be provided in a concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible form — using clear and plain language. Those are communicative standards, not publication standards.

Regulatory guidance consistently distinguishes between information that is technically available and information that is genuinely accessible. The standard is whether the individual can understand what is being communicated — not whether the organisation can point to a document that contains the relevant information somewhere within it.

AI-specific legislation is advancing transparency requirements that are increasingly specific about form, timing, and audience — moving beyond a requirement to disclose toward a requirement to communicate effectively to the people most directly affected.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that has published extensive documentation about its automated systems but cannot demonstrate that affected individuals can find, access, and understand what they need to exercise their rights has not met its transparency obligations. Publication is the beginning of transparency. Understanding is the test of it.
