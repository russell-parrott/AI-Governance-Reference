# JX-07 — Japan

> **Japan has chosen a soft-law, innovation-first approach to AI governance — but the enactment of the AI Promotion Act in 2025 marks a shift toward binding obligations, particularly for high-risk systems.**

---

## The Framework

Japan's approach to AI governance has historically been principles-based and voluntary, reflecting a policy preference for supporting AI development over imposing regulatory constraints. The government published AI Guidelines for Business in 2024, establishing seven principles including human-centricity, safety, fairness, privacy protection, security and accountability. These are guidance rather than law.

The AI Promotion Act, enacted in May 2025, represents Japan's first AI-specific legislation. The Act is primarily enabling and promotional in character — establishing governance principles, creating a framework for government AI strategy, and providing a basis for sector-specific rules. It does not yet impose the kind of detailed obligations found in the EU AI Act, but it creates the legislative foundation for more specific requirements to follow.

Existing law provides the binding layer. The Act on the Protection of Personal Information (APPI), significantly amended in 2022 and 2023, applies to AI systems that process personal information. The Antimonopoly Act applies to algorithmic pricing and anti-competitive behaviour. The Consumer Contract Act and Act against Unjustifiable Premiums and Misleading Representations apply to AI in consumer contexts. Financial regulation applies to AI in financial services.

Japan has been internationally active in AI governance — the Hiroshima AI Process, led by Japan during its G7 presidency in 2023, produced the Hiroshima AI Principles that influenced international AI governance norms. Japan's approach emphasises international interoperability and has worked to align its framework with EU and US approaches.

---

## Key Regulators

**Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI)** — AI policy, AI Guidelines for Business, industrial AI governance.

**Personal Information Protection Commission (PPC)** — APPI enforcement, personal data in AI systems, guidance on AI and privacy.

**Financial Services Agency (FSA)** — AI in financial services, banking regulation, insurance.

**Japan Fair Trade Commission (JFTC)** — algorithmic pricing, competition concerns in digital markets, platform regulation.

**Consumer Affairs Agency** — AI in consumer contexts, misleading AI claims, consumer protection.

**Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare** — AI in healthcare and employment contexts.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**The APPI** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal information of individuals in Japan. Amended provisions address third-party data provision, sensitive personal information, pseudonymised data and cross-border transfers. Automated profiling and decision-making using personal information require appropriate handling and, in some contexts, individual notification.

**The AI Promotion Act** triggers governance expectations for organisations developing or deploying AI. While primarily a framework law, it establishes principles that sector regulators are expected to implement through sector-specific guidance. High-impact AI applications are the priority focus for more detailed requirements.

**The Antimonopoly Act** triggers for algorithmic pricing and coordinated market behaviour. The JFTC has published reports on algorithmic pricing and has identified coordination through shared pricing algorithms as a competition concern.

**Financial regulation** triggers for AI in financial services. The FSA has published supervisory guidelines that address model risk, algorithmic trading controls and AI governance for financial institutions. These are among the more detailed sector-specific AI governance requirements in Japan.

**The Act against Misleading Representations** triggers for AI marketing claims that are exaggerated or false — a direct parallel to the AI washing concerns addressed by regulators in other jurisdictions.

## Governance Connection

Japan's governance environment rewards organisations that implement substantive governance rather than waiting for detailed regulation. The combination of existing law, sector guidance and the new AI Promotion Act creates genuine obligations now, with more specific requirements expected to follow.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a principle of both the AI Guidelines for Business and the AI Promotion Act. Japanese governance culture places significant weight on named responsibility — the concept of *sekinin* (responsibility) in corporate governance aligns with the Atlas accountability requirements.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is one of the seven AI governance principles. The APPI requires that individuals be informed about the purposes for which their personal information is processed. AI systems making decisions based on personal information must operate within disclosed purposes.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is addressed in the AI Guidelines. The guidelines distinguish between AI systems that operate autonomously and those with human involvement, and express a preference for human control in high-impact decision contexts.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under APPI accountability obligations. Organisations must maintain records of personal information processing and be able to demonstrate compliance. The PPC can request documentation in investigations.

**Safety** (→ SP-03, GV-03) is the first principle of Japan's AI governance framework. The emphasis on safety — particularly in AI systems that interact with physical environments, healthcare and critical infrastructure — reflects Japan's manufacturing and technology heritage and creates a natural alignment with product safety governance requirements.
