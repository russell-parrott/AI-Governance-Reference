# LL-04 — Consumer Protection

> **Consumer protection law applies when AI influences what people buy, what they pay, or what choices they believe they are making. The legal question is not whether the system was transparent — it is whether the consumer was misled.**

---

## What It Covers

Consumer protection law prohibits unfair, deceptive and manipulative commercial practices. It applies wherever organisations interact with consumers — in marketing, pricing, product recommendations, sales processes, customer service, and any context in which automated systems influence consumer decisions or experiences.

Major frameworks include the EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive and Consumer Rights Directive, the UK Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations, the US Federal Trade Commission Act and its prohibition on unfair or deceptive acts and practices, the Australian Consumer Law, and Canada's Competition Act consumer deception provisions. The EU is also developing a Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act that extend consumer protections specifically to algorithmic systems operating on digital platforms.

Consumer protection law is distinct from data protection law, though both may apply simultaneously. Data protection governs how personal data is processed. Consumer protection governs how commercial interactions are conducted and whether consumers are treated fairly.

---

## Why AI Systems Trigger It

Automated systems trigger consumer protection law in several distinct ways, each reflecting the particular capacity of AI to influence consumer behaviour at scale and with precision.

**Personalised pricing and price discrimination.** AI systems that adjust prices for individual consumers based on inferred willingness to pay, purchase history, location or behavioural signals may produce outcomes that trigger consumer protection concerns — particularly where consumers are charged more because of characteristics that correlate with vulnerability or protected status. The practice of showing different prices to different consumers for identical products or services is under increasing regulatory scrutiny in multiple jurisdictions.

**Manipulative recommendation systems.** Recommendation engines that optimise for engagement, purchase conversion or other organisational objectives rather than consumer benefit can constitute manipulative practices under consumer protection frameworks. Where a system steers consumers toward products that are more profitable for the organisation but less suitable for the consumer — without disclosing that the recommendation is commercially motivated — it may be applying a deceptive commercial practice.

**Undisclosed AI interaction.** Consumer protection law in multiple jurisdictions is moving toward requiring disclosure when consumers interact with automated systems rather than humans. The UK and EU have both addressed this in the context of chatbots and automated customer service. A consumer who believes they are receiving human advice when they are receiving automated output is being misled about the nature of the interaction.

**Misleading claims about AI capabilities.** Organisations that make false or misleading claims about what their AI systems can do — in marketing, product descriptions or investor communications — trigger consumer protection and potentially securities law. Regulatory bodies including the US Federal Trade Commission have issued guidance and enforcement actions specifically addressing misleading AI claims.

**Dark patterns.** Interface designs that exploit cognitive biases, create artificial urgency or obscure consumer choices — whether implemented by AI or not — are increasingly treated as unfair commercial practices. AI systems that personalise dark patterns to individual consumers, optimising their effectiveness based on behavioural data, represent an amplification of existing consumer protection concerns.

## Governance Connection

Consumer protection law connects to several Atlas governance concepts, particularly those concerning transparency, intended use and accountability for system outcomes.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, GV-N05) is a legal requirement in consumer contexts. Disclosure obligations apply to the automated nature of interactions, the commercial motivations behind recommendations, and the pricing logic applied to individual consumers. Transparency in the legal sense means consumers understand what is happening — not that the organisation has published documentation.

**Intended use** (→ SP-09, SP-N02) is directly relevant. A system deployed to serve consumers but optimised to extract value from them is operating outside the purpose that consumer protection law expects. The gap between stated purpose and actual optimisation objective is a governance and legal exposure simultaneously.

**System outputs** (→ SP-14) are the point of legal risk. What the system shows consumers, what prices it quotes, what it recommends — these outputs are the commercial acts that consumer protection law evaluates. Governance of outputs is governance of legal exposure.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, AR-08) matters because consumer protection liability attaches to the organisation the consumer interacted with — not to the vendor who built the system. An organisation that deploys an AI-driven recommendation engine built by a third party is accountable for what that engine does to consumers.

**Records** (→ RE-02, RE-13) of what the system showed specific consumers, what prices it quoted, and what recommendations it made are the evidentiary foundation for regulatory investigations and legal proceedings. Without them, the organisation cannot demonstrate what its system actually did.

---

## Jurisdictions

Consumer protection frameworks exist in all major trading jurisdictions. The EU frameworks have extraterritorial reach where EU consumers are affected. US enforcement is conducted primarily by the Federal Trade Commission at federal level, with additional state consumer protection laws. Australian, Canadian and UK frameworks apply domestically with increasing attention to algorithmic consumer practices.
