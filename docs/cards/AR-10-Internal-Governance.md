# AR-10 — Internal Governance

> **Beyond individuals — this card explains the committees, structures, and internal rules that make governance institutional rather than personal.**

---

## What Is It?

Internal governance is the organisational infrastructure through which an organisation exercises collective oversight of its automated systems — the committees, forums, policies, standards, and internal rules that give governance a structural form beyond the responsibilities of individual people.

Where role definition assigns obligations to named individuals, internal governance creates the institutional architecture within which those individuals operate. It establishes how decisions about automated systems are made collectively, how conflicts are resolved, how standards are set and enforced, and how accountability is maintained across the organisation as a whole rather than through individual chains of command.

Internal governance is what makes an organisation's approach to automated systems coherent, consistent, and durable — not dependent on the presence of particular individuals or the priorities of particular managers.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Individual accountability is necessary but not sufficient. A named individual who is accountable for a specific system can leave, be promoted, or be overwhelmed by competing priorities. An organisational structure — a committee with terms of reference, a policy with defined owners, a forum with a regular schedule — persists beyond individuals. It creates continuity of governance that individual accountability alone cannot provide.

Internal governance also provides the mechanisms for coordination across an organisation where automated systems cut across multiple functions. A credit system affects risk, compliance, customer service, and technology. Governing it effectively requires structures that bring those functions together — not just individual roles within each.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Governance that depends on individuals rather than structures.** An organisation whose automated system governance rests on the knowledge, commitment, and availability of specific individuals is one governance departure away from a significant gap. Internal governance structures survive personnel changes. Individual knowledge does not.

**Inconsistent standards across systems and functions.** Without internal governance structures that set and enforce standards consistently, different parts of the organisation govern their automated systems differently. Some functions apply rigorous oversight. Others do not. The organisation's overall governance is only as strong as its weakest function — and without central structures, it may not know where the weaknesses are.

**Decisions made without the right people in the room.** Consequential decisions about automated systems — deployment, significant changes, responses to incidents — benefit from collective judgment across multiple functions. Without governance structures that convene the right people for those decisions, they are made unilaterally, with incomplete information and insufficient challenge.

**No institutional memory.** Committees produce records. Policies create documented standards. Forums generate decisions that are formally recorded. Internal governance structures create the institutional memory that individual accountability does not. Without them, the organisation's governance history lives in people rather than in records.

Responsibility for establishing and maintaining internal governance structures sits with the board and senior leadership. Governance structures do not create themselves. They are built, resourced, and sustained by deliberate organisational decisions.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Internal governance structures for automated systems take different forms across different organisations and sectors:

- **Risk and compliance committees** — cross-functional forums that review automated system risks, assess new deployments, and monitor ongoing governance across the organisation
- **AI or technology governance boards** — dedicated internal bodies with terms of reference specifically covering automated and algorithmic systems
- **Model risk management functions** — specialist internal functions responsible for setting standards for model development, validation, and ongoing oversight
- **Ethics review panels** — internal forums that assess the fairness, appropriateness, and social impact of automated system deployments
- **Policy and standards frameworks** — internal rules that define how automated systems must be governed across the organisation, regardless of which function owns them
- **Cross-functional working groups** — operational forums that bring together the functions affected by a specific automated system to coordinate governance activity

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes internal governance seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Are there defined internal structures — committees, policies, standards — through which the organisation exercises collective oversight of its automated systems?
- Do those structures have terms of reference, defined membership, regular schedules, and documented outputs?
- Do governance structures cover all significant automated systems, or are some systems operating outside any formal internal governance framework?
- Is there a clear relationship between internal governance structures and individual accountability — do the structures support individual role-holders rather than substitute for them?
- When governance structures identify problems, are there mechanisms for those findings to reach management and the board in a form that produces action?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation's governance depends on individuals rather than institutions. That is governance with an expiry date — it lasts as long as the right people are in the right roles with the right priorities. Institutional governance lasts longer than any of them.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Creates governance continuity that survives personnel changes and organisational restructuring | Committees and governance bodies add overhead — they require time, preparation, and administration that must be resourced |
| Enables consistent standards across functions and systems rather than governance that varies by manager | Governance structures that meet regularly without producing meaningful decisions become bureaucratic rather than effective |
| Brings collective judgment to consequential decisions rather than leaving them to individuals | Cross-functional governance bodies can be slow — their deliberative nature can create delays when speed is needed |
| Produces institutional records — committee minutes, policy documents, standards — that support audit and investigation | Internal governance structures without genuine authority to enforce their decisions are advisory at best |
| Provides a mechanism for coordinating governance across systems that affect multiple functions | Governance bodies that become captured by any single function — typically technology or compliance — lose the breadth that makes them valuable |

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: regulators are looking beyond individual accountability to assess whether organisations have institutional governance structures adequate for the scale and risk of their automated systems.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require organisations to demonstrate that governance of automated processing is embedded in their organisational structure — not just assigned to individuals. Data protection impact assessments, model risk governance requirements, and algorithmic accountability obligations all presuppose internal structures that make governance collective and durable.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for defined internal governance structures as a condition of compliance for high-risk systems, with some frameworks specifying minimum governance body requirements, terms of reference standards, and documentation obligations. The shift from individual to institutional accountability is a consistent direction of regulatory travel.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that cannot point to functioning internal governance structures for its automated systems — structures with defined authority, regular activity, and documented outputs — cannot demonstrate that its governance is institutional rather than personal, durable rather than contingent, and adequate for the scale of what it is doing.
