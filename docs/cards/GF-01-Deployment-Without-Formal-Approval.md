# GF-01 — Deployment Without Formal Approval

> **Systems introduced into operation without a documented decision authorising their use.**

---

## What This Failure Is

The system went live. No one formally decided to deploy it. There is no documented approval — no named individual who reviewed the system, assessed its risks, attached conditions and signed off. The system moved from development or procurement into production through operational momentum rather than governance process.

This is not a failure of competence. It is a failure of procedure. The deployment decision was made implicitly — by the people who built or bought the system, by the team that started using it, by the absence of a checkpoint that required formal authorisation. Nobody chose to deploy without approval. Nobody chose to deploy at all. It just happened.

---

## Where It Occurs

Deployment without formal approval occurs most commonly at the transition from development or pilot to operational use; when vendor tools are procured by business units without central governance review; when existing systems are extended to new use cases without returning to a formal approval process; and when organisational change causes previously governed systems to lose their governance context.

---

## Why It Matters

When a system causes harm and investigators ask who authorised it, the answer is nobody — because no authorisation event occurred. The accountability chain has no starting point. Every subsequent governance claim — that the system was overseen, that its risks were assessed, that its use was appropriate — is weakened by the absence of the foundational decision that should have preceded them.

Regulators and courts treat the absence of deployment authorisation as evidence that the organisation did not exercise meaningful governance over the decision to deploy.

---

## How It Is Discovered

Deployment without formal approval is typically discovered during regulatory investigation, when auditors request the authorisation record and it cannot be produced; during litigation disclosure, when the defendant cannot identify who approved the system; and during internal audit, when governance frameworks are tested against operational records.

---

## Evidence That Reveals It

The absence of a deployment authorisation record is itself the evidence. The discovery is typically confirmed by the absence of board papers, approval minutes, risk assessment sign-offs or any contemporaneous documentation of a governance decision to deploy.

---

## Governance Concepts Involved

→ SP-30 (Deployment Authorisation) — the control whose absence defines this failure
→ AR-01 (Accountability) — the chain that has no starting point
→ AR-02 (Approval Processes) — the mechanism that was not used
→ AR-12 (Responsibility Gaps) — the consequence of undocumented deployment

---

## What Organisations Often Assume Instead

That informal agreement among technical and business teams constitutes sufficient authorisation. That a procurement decision is equivalent to a deployment decision. That the absence of objection is the same as the presence of approval.
