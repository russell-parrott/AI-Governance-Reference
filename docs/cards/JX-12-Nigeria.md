# JX-12 — Nigeria

> **Nigeria is Africa's largest economy and has the continent's most active data protection enforcement regime — including specific requirements for automated decision-making that took effect in 2025.**

---

## The Framework

Nigeria's AI governance framework is built primarily around data protection law, with sector-specific regulation adding obligations in financial services and telecommunications. The Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA), enacted in 2023 and replacing earlier regulations, established Nigeria's comprehensive data protection framework. Implementation has been active, with the Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC) issuing enforcement decisions and compliance guidance throughout 2024 and 2025.

The NDPC introduced an enforcement framework in September 2025 that specifically addresses high-risk data processing activities. Automated decision-making that significantly affects individuals is classified as high-risk under this framework, triggering mandatory Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) before processing begins.

Nigeria does not yet have AI-specific legislation. The National Information Technology Development Agency (NITDA) published an AI Policy Framework in 2023 that establishes principles and governance expectations for AI development and deployment. This framework is guidance rather than law, but it signals the direction of regulatory development and is referenced in government procurement and regulated sector contexts.

The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC Nigeria) have each issued guidance on AI and algorithmic systems in their regulated sectors. Nigeria's active fintech sector — one of the most developed in Africa — means financial services AI governance is particularly well-developed relative to the broader regulatory landscape.

---

## Key Regulators

**Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC)** — NDPA enforcement, data protection audits, DPIA requirements for high-risk processing. The NDPC has demonstrated willingness to pursue enforcement actions and has issued significant fines.

**Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN)** — AI in financial services, banking regulation, payment systems, fintech. Has published guidance on model risk management and responsible innovation.

**Securities and Exchange Commission Nigeria (SEC Nigeria)** — AI in capital markets, investment management, algorithmic trading.

**Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC)** — AI in telecommunications and digital services.

**Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission (FCCPC)** — consumer protection, digital markets, algorithmic pricing concerns.

**National Information Technology Development Agency (NITDA)** — AI policy, national AI framework, technology governance.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**The NDPA** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal data of Nigerian residents. The Act requires a lawful basis for processing, data minimisation, purpose limitation, individual rights responses and accountability documentation. Automated decision-making with significant effects triggers specific obligations — individuals must be informed and have the right to human review.

**DPIA requirements** trigger for high-risk data processing activities including automated decision-making. The NDPC's September 2025 framework makes DPIAs mandatory before deploying AI systems that make or significantly influence decisions about individuals. The DPIA must be documented, must identify risks, and must demonstrate that those risks have been addressed.

**CBN guidelines** trigger for financial institutions using AI. The CBN has published guidance on responsible innovation and model risk management that applies to AI systems in credit assessment, fraud detection, customer onboarding and payment processing.

**Consumer protection law** triggers for AI in consumer-facing contexts. The FCCPC has jurisdiction over misleading commercial practices including AI-driven pricing and recommendations.

---

## Governance Connection

Nigeria's governance environment is particularly active in data protection enforcement — organisations that process personal data of Nigerian residents face real enforcement risk from the NDPC, and the DPIA requirement for automated decision-making creates a specific governance obligation that must be met before deployment.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a NDPA principle. Data controllers must designate a Data Protection Officer and maintain records of processing activities. The NDPC conducts audits and can demand documentation of governance arrangements.

**Risk assessment** (→ GV-N14, LR-13) is mandatory under the NDPC's high-risk framework. The DPIA requirement makes pre-deployment risk assessment a legal obligation — not a governance recommendation — for AI systems making significant automated decisions. The assessment must be documented and available for NDPC review.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is required under the NDPA for significant automated decisions. The right to human review is a data subject right. Organisations must build functional review processes before deploying systems that trigger this right.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under NDPA accountability obligations. The NDPC can demand records of processing activities, DPIAs and governance arrangements. Given the NDPC's active enforcement posture, organisations should treat record-keeping as a compliance requirement rather than an administrative activity.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is required under the NDPA. Individuals must be informed about automated processing and its logic. Privacy notices must be clear and specific about AI decision-making.
