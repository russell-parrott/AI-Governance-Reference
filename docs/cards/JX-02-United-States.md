# JX-02 — United States

> **The United States has no federal AI law. What it has is a dense, overlapping patchwork of existing laws, sector regulators, state legislation and executive orders — all of which apply to AI systems right now.**

---

## The Framework

The United States does not have a comprehensive federal AI law. Repeated attempts at federal AI legislation have not produced binding law. What exists instead is a combination of existing federal statutes applied to AI contexts, sector-specific regulatory guidance, a growing body of state legislation, and executive orders that direct federal agencies but do not themselves create enforceable rights.

The Biden administration's Executive Order on AI (October 2023) directed federal agencies to develop AI governance standards and guidance within their remits. The Trump administration revoked that order in January 2025 and issued a replacement focused on removing barriers to AI development. The direction of federal policy has shifted, but the underlying laws — the FTC Act, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act, Title VII, HIPAA, financial regulation — remain in force and continue to apply.

State law is filling the federal gap. Colorado passed the first comprehensive state AI law in 2024, effective 2026, imposing obligations on developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems. California has enacted multiple AI-related statutes. New York has legislation addressing automated employment decisions. Illinois has the Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA). The state-level landscape is fragmented and evolving rapidly.

---

## Key Regulators

**Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** — unfair and deceptive practices, consumer protection, AI washing, algorithmic discrimination. The FTC has brought enforcement actions specifically addressing AI misrepresentation and has published extensive guidance on AI governance obligations under Section 5 of the FTC Act.

**Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)** — AI in investment management, algorithmic trading, AI-related securities disclosure. The SEC has issued guidance on AI disclosure obligations and brought enforcement actions for AI misrepresentation in investor communications.

**Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)** — discrimination in employment decisions made by AI systems. The EEOC has published guidance on AI and employment discrimination.

**Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** — AI in consumer credit, lending, and financial services. Has published guidance on adverse action notices for AI credit decisions.

**Office of Civil Rights (OCR, HHS)** — AI in healthcare, HIPAA compliance, discrimination in health AI.

**State Attorneys General** — enforcement of state AI and privacy laws. Colorado, California, New York and others have active enforcement programmes.

**Federal banking regulators (OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC)** — AI in banking and financial services, model risk management.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**The FTC Act** triggers whenever AI systems are used in commercial contexts that could be unfair or deceptive — which includes misleading claims about AI capabilities, algorithmic pricing that disadvantages consumers, and AI-driven marketing that manipulates. The FTC's enforcement reach is broad and its AI guidance is among the most developed of any US regulator.

**Equal Credit Opportunity Act and Fair Housing Act** trigger whenever AI systems influence credit, lending or housing decisions. Disparate impact — producing discriminatory outcomes for protected groups regardless of intent — is actionable. The CFPB has specifically addressed the obligation to provide meaningful adverse action explanations for AI credit decisions.

**Title VII and the ADA** trigger in employment contexts. AI systems used in hiring, promotion, performance assessment or termination decisions are subject to employment discrimination law. The EEOC has published specific technical assistance on this.

**HIPAA** triggers whenever AI systems process protected health information. AI in clinical settings, health insurance and healthcare operations faces significant federal healthcare privacy obligations.

**State privacy laws** — California (CCPA/CPRA), Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut and others — trigger whenever AI systems process personal data of residents of those states. Colorado's AI Act specifically addresses high-risk automated decisions.

**SEC rules** trigger for public companies and investment advisers using AI in ways that affect investor decisions or disclosures.

## Governance Connection

The US framework requires organisations to map their AI governance to multiple overlapping regulatory bodies simultaneously. The Atlas governance concepts provide the underlying structure that satisfies multiple regulatory expectations at once.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) matters in the US because enforcement actions are brought against organisations and individuals. FTC, SEC and state enforcement all carry personal liability potential for executives who approved or oversaw AI systems producing unlawful outcomes.

**Explainability** (→ SP-16, LL-02) is specifically required for adverse credit decisions under ECOA — the explanation must be specific enough for the applicant to understand and act on. The CFPB has been explicit that "the algorithm decided" is not an adequate explanation.

**Fairness and bias** (→ SP-19, GV-02, LL-03) are enforced through multiple channels simultaneously — EEOC in employment, CFPB in credit, FTC in consumer contexts, HUD in housing. An AI system that produces disparate impact faces potential action from multiple regulators.

**Records** (→ RE-07, LL-02) are required under sector-specific rules. SEC requires trading and investment records. Banking regulators require model risk documentation. HIPAA requires health data records. State privacy laws require processing records.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is increasingly expected. The CFPB's adverse action guidance, the EEOC's AI employment guidance and the FTC's AI principles all express expectations for human review of automated decisions affecting individuals.
