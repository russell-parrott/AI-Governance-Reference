# LR-01 — Automated Decision Rights

> **People have rights about automated decisions. This card explains what those rights are — and what organisations must be able to do to respect them.**

---

## What Is It?

Automated decision rights are the legal entitlements individuals hold in relation to decisions made about them by automated systems — rights to know a decision was automated, to understand the basis on which it was made, to request that a human review it, and in some circumstances to have the decision reconsidered or overridden.

These rights exist because automated decisions carry consequences for real people — consequences in access to credit, employment, housing, healthcare, public services, and more. The law recognises that individuals affected by those consequences have a legitimate interest in understanding what happened to them, in challenging decisions they believe are wrong, and in not having their fate determined entirely by a machine without any human engagement.

Automated decision rights are not general complaints procedures. They are specific legal entitlements that apply when defined conditions are met — typically where a decision is made solely by automated means and has a significant effect on the individual concerned.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Rights that exist in law but cannot be exercised in practice are not rights. They are statements. An organisation that has deployed an automated system without building in the capability to respond to individual rights requests has created obligations it cannot meet — from the first day the system went live.

When an individual exercises their automated decision rights, the organisation must respond. It must be able to identify the decision, explain how it was reached, facilitate human review if required, and provide correction where the decision was based on inaccurate information. Each of those obligations requires capability that must be designed into the system in advance, not improvised in response to a request.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Rights requests the organisation cannot fulfil.** An individual who requests an explanation of an automated decision, or asks for human review, is exercising a legal right. An organisation that cannot respond — because the system is opaque, because the decision record does not exist, because no human review process is in place — is not merely failing to provide good service. It is in breach of a legal obligation.

**Rights obligations that were not considered at deployment.** Systems deployed without considering individual rights obligations may be technically incapable of meeting them. Producing an individual-level explanation, facilitating a meaningful human review, or correcting a decision based on inaccurate data may require capabilities the system does not have and that cannot easily be added retrospectively.

**Responses that are technically compliant but substantively empty.** Providing a generic statement about how the system works in response to a request for an individual explanation satisfies the form of the obligation while ignoring its substance. Regulators and courts are increasingly unpersuaded by responses that appear to comply while providing the individual with nothing meaningful.

**Rights exercised only by those who know they exist.** Automated decision rights are most valuable to people who have been incorrectly or unfairly treated. Those individuals often belong to groups that are less likely to know their rights exist or how to exercise them. An organisation that relies on the low awareness of its affected population to reduce rights requests is not in a strong governance position when awareness increases.

Responsibility for ensuring automated decision rights can be respected sits with whoever deployed the system. Rights obligations cannot be contracted away to vendors or satisfied by pointing to the complexity of the system. They are the deploying organisation's obligation to meet.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Automated decision rights are relevant wherever systems make decisions that significantly affect individuals:

- **Financial services** — rights to explanation and human review of automated credit refusals, fraud flags, insurance decisions, and account actions
- **Employment** — rights to understand and challenge automated screening, assessment, and performance management decisions
- **Public services** — rights to explanation, review, and correction of automated eligibility, benefit, and enforcement decisions
- **Healthcare** — rights to understand the basis of automated diagnostic and triage recommendations and to have them reviewed by a qualified clinician
- **Platform and content systems** — rights to explanation and review of automated account actions, content removals, and access restrictions
- **Any context where an automated system makes or significantly influences a decision that affects an individual's access to services, employment, credit, or legal standing**

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes automated decision rights seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Does this system produce decisions that trigger automated decision rights under applicable law — and has that been formally assessed?
- Can the organisation produce an individual-level explanation of any decision this system has made, in terms the affected person can understand?
- Is there a functioning human review process that can be activated when an individual exercises their right to request it?
- Is there a mechanism for correcting decisions based on inaccurate data, and does that mechanism actually change the outcome where correction is warranted?
- Are individuals informed that a decision affecting them was made by an automated system, and are they told how to exercise their rights in relation to it?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation is making decisions about people that those people have legal rights to understand and challenge — rights the organisation is not positioned to respect. That is not a future compliance risk. It is a present legal failure.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Rights frameworks create external accountability for automated decisions that internal governance alone cannot provide | Rights obligations require system capabilities — explanation, review, correction — that must be built in at design, not added later |
| Responding to rights requests surfaces errors and unfairness that monitoring may not have detected | The definition of what constitutes a decision made "solely" by automated means involves legal interpretation that varies across jurisdictions |
| Rights obligations drive governance investment that benefits all individuals, not just those who formally exercise their rights | Rights awareness is uneven — the individuals most affected by automated decisions are often least likely to know their rights exist |
| A functioning rights response process demonstrates genuine accountability to regulators | Meeting rights obligations at scale in high-volume systems requires significant operational capability and resource |
| Rights frameworks are one of the clearest legal expressions of the accountability principles this series addresses throughout | The scope and content of automated decision rights varies across jurisdictions, creating complexity for organisations operating internationally |

## The Law

This card is one of the few in this series where specific legal frameworks are directly relevant enough to name.

In many jurisdictions with data protection frameworks, individuals have explicit rights in relation to automated decision-making — including the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produce significant effects, the right to obtain human intervention, the right to express their point of view, and the right to contest the decision. These rights are legal requirements, not guidance.

AI-specific legislation in multiple regions is extending and reinforcing individual rights in relation to automated decisions — requiring greater transparency, broader rights of review, and more specific remediation obligations, particularly for high-risk applications.

The practical legal risk is direct and established: automated decision rights have been enforceable law in multiple jurisdictions for years. Organisations that have not designed their systems to respect them are not behind an emerging requirement. They are in breach of an existing one — and individuals, regulators, and courts are increasingly aware of that.
