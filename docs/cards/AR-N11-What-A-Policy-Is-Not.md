# AR-N11 — What A Policy Is Not

> **The policy exists. Now check: is it governance — or a description of governance that has not been built?**

---

## What It Is Not

A policy is not governance. It is not an oversight mechanism, an accountability structure, a monitoring process, or a control. It is not evidence that governance is occurring, that obligations are being met, or that the systems the policy addresses are being managed in the way the policy describes. It is not enforced by its existence. It is not self-implementing.

A policy, properly understood, is a formal statement of intent — a documented commitment to govern in a defined way, produced by the organisation, for the organisation, describing what should happen. The gap between what a policy says should happen and what actually happens is the governance gap that this card is about.

What organisations most often confuse with a policy is one or more of the following:

**Governance itself.** An organisation that has an AI governance policy believes it has AI governance. The policy describes accountability structures — but those structures may not be genuinely populated. It describes monitoring processes — but those processes may not be occurring. It describes escalation procedures — but those procedures may never have been tested. The policy describes a governance system. That description is not the system.

**Evidence of compliance.** A policy is produced and published. A regulator asks whether the organisation meets its governance obligations. The organisation produces the policy. The policy describes meeting those obligations. The regulator asks for evidence that the obligations are being met in practice. The policy is the description of practice, not the evidence of it. Those are different things, and regulators know the difference.

**An accountability mechanism.** A policy that assigns responsibilities does not enforce them. A policy that requires monitoring does not conduct it. A policy that mandates review does not hold reviews. Policies describe what should be done and who should do it. They do not ensure it happens, create consequences when it does not, or provide any mechanism for identifying the gap between commitment and conduct.

**A substitute for governance investment.** Writing a policy requires time and expertise. Implementing the governance the policy describes requires significantly more of both — people, processes, technical capability, ongoing resource, and leadership attention. Organisations that invest in the policy but not in its implementation have produced a document that describes governance they do not have. The investment in description has substituted for the investment in reality.

**A static answer to a dynamic problem.** Policies are written at a point in time. Automated systems evolve. Regulatory requirements change. Organisational structures shift. A policy written at deployment may describe a governance system that was appropriate for a system that no longer exists, in a regulatory environment that has since moved on, allocating responsibilities to roles that have been reorganised. The policy persists. Its relationship to current reality degrades.

---

## Why Confusion Matters

When a policy is mistaken for governance, the organisation believes it has something it does not have — and that belief removes the pressure to build the real thing. The governance gap does not announce itself. It is invisible until it becomes consequential — until an incident occurs, a regulator investigates, or a legal challenge surfaces the distance between what the policy committed to and what actually happened.

The specific danger of the policy-as-governance confusion is that it produces documentation of non-compliance. A policy that commits to monthly monitoring reviews, documented escalation procedures, and named accountability holders creates an evidential baseline. When an investigation finds that monitoring was not conducted monthly, that escalation procedures were not followed, and that the named accountability holder had no functional role, the policy does not help the organisation. It demonstrates that the organisation knew what governance required and chose not to do it.

This is the precise point AR-11 in the main series makes — the policy demonstrates knowledge of the standard. This card extends it: the policy is the standard the organisation set for itself. Failing to meet your own stated standard is harder to defend than failing to meet an external one.

---

## Where Confusion Appears

**Policy suites that grow without corresponding governance investment.** Organisations accumulate governance policies — for data protection, AI governance, information security, vendor management, and more — without the corresponding investment in implementation. Each policy describes a governance system. The collective weight of policies creates the impression of a comprehensive governance framework. The governance the policies collectively describe does not exist.

**Policies cited in audit responses as evidence of controls.** An audit asks whether a specific control is in place. The organisation points to the policy that requires the control. The auditor asks for evidence the control is operating. The organisation's evidence is the policy requirement. The requirement is not the evidence of the control's operation. It is the description of what the control should be.

**Policy reviews that assess documentation rather than practice.** An organisation conducts annual policy reviews — checking that policies are current, that they reference the right frameworks, and that they are approved by the appropriate authority. The review does not assess whether the practices the policies describe are occurring. It reviews the policies. It does not review the governance.

**New policies written in response to incidents rather than governance failures addressed.** When an incident reveals a governance gap, the response is sometimes a new or revised policy — a documented commitment to do what the incident showed was not being done. The policy addresses the gap in documentation. It does not address the gap in practice unless it is accompanied by the investment and accountability needed to implement it.

---

## How To Tell The Difference

Five questions that distinguish genuine governance from policy:

- If the policy were removed tomorrow, would the governance activities it describes continue — because they are embedded in how the organisation operates — or would they stop, because the policy was the only thing that said they should happen?
- Can the organisation produce evidence of each governance activity the policy requires — monitoring records, review records, escalation records — not just the policy that requires them?
- When policies are updated, is the update accompanied by an assessment of whether the practice needs to change — or does updating the document constitute the governance response?
- Does someone in the organisation have accountability for verifying that the practices the policy describes are occurring — and is there a record of that verification?
- When the gap between policy commitment and actual practice is identified, does the organisation treat it as a governance failure requiring remediation — or as a documentation issue requiring a policy update?

| Common Substitutes | What A Policy Actually Is |
|---|---|
| Governance itself | A statement of intent — what governance should look like, not what it is |
| Evidence of compliance | A description of what compliance requires — not evidence it is being achieved |
| An accountability mechanism | An assignment of responsibility — without enforcement or consequence for non-fulfilment |
| A substitute for governance investment | A starting point — the investment in implementation must follow separately |
| A current description of practice | A document that degrades in accuracy as the organisation and its context evolve |

## The Law

Regulatory frameworks assess governance by examining what organisations do, not what they say they will do. A policy is relevant context — it demonstrates that the organisation understood its obligations and committed to meeting them. It is not evidence of compliance. The gap between a policy commitment and actual practice is the governance failure that regulators investigate.

In enforcement actions involving governance failures, the existence of a policy that committed to the governance that was absent is frequently an aggravating factor rather than a mitigating one. The organisation knew what was required. The policy says so. The practice did not match the commitment. That gap is harder to defend than genuine ignorance of the requirement.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for demonstrated governance — not just documented governance. The direction of regulatory travel is toward evidence of practice, not evidence of policy. Organisations that have invested in policy production without corresponding investment in implementation will find that the policies they produced are the clearest evidence of the standard they failed to meet.

The practical legal risk is the same one that runs through every card in this negation series: the document describes what should exist. The investigation examines what does. When the distance between those two things is large, the document does not close the gap. It measures it.
