# JX-04 — Canada

> **Canada's AI governance landscape combines well-established privacy law with a proposed AI-specific framework that stalled in Parliament — leaving organisations to navigate existing law while the regulatory picture evolves.**

---

## The Framework

Canada's approach to AI governance rests on existing law supplemented by proposed legislation that has not yet been enacted. The proposed Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), introduced as part of Bill C-27 in 2022, would have created a risk-based AI framework comparable in structure to the EU AI Act. Bill C-27 died when Parliament was prorogued in January 2025. A replacement framework is expected but not yet tabled.

In the interim, existing law governs AI systems. The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) — and its provincial equivalents in Quebec (Law 25), Alberta and British Columbia — applies whenever AI systems process personal data. Quebec's Law 25, which came into full effect in 2023, is the most demanding provincial framework and includes specific provisions on automated decision-making.

The Canadian Human Rights Act and provincial human rights codes apply wherever AI influences decisions about people in protected categories. The Competition Act applies to algorithmic pricing and deceptive practices. Federal and provincial financial regulation applies to AI in financial services.

Canada's Office of the Privacy Commissioner has published guidance on AI and privacy that provides a practical framework for organisations even in the absence of AI-specific legislation. The Treasury Board has published a Directive on Automated Decision-Making that applies to federal government AI deployments and is increasingly influential as a governance model.

---

## Key Regulators

**Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC)** — PIPEDA enforcement, AI and privacy guidance. Published a significant report on facial recognition and AI that signals enforcement priorities.

**Commission d'accès à l'information (CAI)** — Quebec's privacy regulator, enforcement of Law 25 including automated decision-making provisions.

**Canadian Human Rights Commission** — discrimination in federally regulated sectors including banking, telecommunications and transportation.

**Competition Bureau** — algorithmic pricing, deceptive AI marketing, abuse of dominance in digital markets.

**Financial sector regulators (OSFI, FSRA)** — AI in banking, insurance and financial services. OSFI has published guidance on model risk management.

**Treasury Board Secretariat** — federal government AI governance, Directive on Automated Decision-Making.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**PIPEDA** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal information of Canadians — which includes systems that make decisions about individuals based on their data. Organisations must identify the purpose of collection, obtain appropriate consent, and safeguard personal information. Automated decision-making that significantly affects individuals raises heightened obligations.

**Quebec Law 25** goes further than PIPEDA on automated decision-making. Organisations must inform individuals when a decision based exclusively on automated processing is made about them, state the personal information used, allow individuals to have a human review the decision, and submit comments. These obligations apply to significant automated decisions affecting Quebec residents.

**The Canadian Human Rights Act** triggers in federally regulated sectors when AI systems produce discriminatory outcomes based on protected grounds. The outcome-based standard applies — discriminatory effects are actionable regardless of intent.

**The Competition Act** triggers when algorithmic systems influence pricing or competitive behaviour. The Competition Bureau has specifically identified algorithmic pricing coordination as a competition law concern.

**The Treasury Board Directive** applies to federal institutions using automated decision-making systems. It establishes an impact assessment framework that classifies automated decisions by potential harm, with increasingly demanding requirements at higher risk levels — a model that private sector organisations are beginning to adopt voluntarily.

---

## Governance Connection

Canada's governance landscape rewards organisations that implement robust governance now rather than waiting for federal AI legislation. The existing legal obligations are real and enforced.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a PIPEDA principle. Organisations must designate an individual responsible for the organisation's compliance with privacy obligations — a governance requirement that extends naturally to AI systems processing personal data.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is required under both PIPEDA and Quebec Law 25. Individuals must be informed when automated decisions are made about them and what information was used. The obligation is specific and enforceable.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is specifically required under Quebec Law 25 for significant automated decisions. The right to human review is a legal right. Organisations must have a process for exercising it before they are asked to apply it.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under PIPEDA accountability obligations. Organisations must maintain records of what personal information they hold, how they use it, and the basis for automated decisions. The OPC can demand these records in an investigation.

**Risk assessment** (→ GV-N14, LR-13) is built into the Treasury Board Directive's impact assessment framework. The Directive classifies decisions by potential harm and requires progressively more documentation, human involvement and oversight at higher impact levels. It is the most developed public framework for AI risk classification in Canada.
