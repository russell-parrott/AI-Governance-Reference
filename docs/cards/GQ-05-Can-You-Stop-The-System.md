# GQ-05 — Can you stop the system if you need to?

> **Not in principle. Not via a process that takes three days and requires vendor approval. Now, today, if you had to — could you?**

---

## What

This question is asking whether the organisation has the practical, operational capability to halt an automated system — to stop it making decisions, to take it out of live operation, to suspend its activity — at the moment that capability is needed, not after the conditions for exercising it have been established.

A genuine yes requires: a defined and tested shutdown procedure, authority that is pre-assigned and available without requiring emergency escalation to find it, technical access that does not depend on the vendor being available or cooperative, and a plan for what happens to the processes and people the system was serving when it stops.

The question is not asking whether the system can eventually be stopped. Everything can eventually be stopped. It is asking whether it can be stopped fast enough to prevent ongoing harm — whether control is real and current, or theoretical and deferred.

---

## Why

The ability to stop a system is the ultimate test of whether the organisation controls it. A system that cannot be stopped — practically, quickly, by a person with the authority and means to do so — is a system the organisation depends on rather than governs. The dependency has replaced the control.

That dependency is a governance failure regardless of how well the system performs. Governance means retaining the ability to act when circumstances require it. Circumstances that require stopping a system — a failure causing harm, a regulatory order, an incident in progress — do not wait for governance processes to establish the capability. They require it to already exist.

The stopping question also reveals the organisation's real relationship with its automated systems. Systems that have become operationally indispensable — embedded so deeply in processes that stopping them would create immediate crisis — have been allowed to accumulate a kind of institutional authority that no governance structure authorised. The system's indispensability was not a governance decision. It was a governance abdication — the gradual transfer of control from the organisation to the system, in the absence of anyone asking this question.

---

## When

**When a system is causing harm.** An automated system is producing harmful outputs — wrong decisions, discriminatory patterns, cascading errors. Every moment it continues, the harm accumulates. The question is not whether to stop it. It is whether stopping it is fast enough to matter. The organisation that prepared for this moment can act. The one that did not discovers the capability gap at the worst possible time.

**When a regulator orders suspension.** A regulatory body issues an order requiring the organisation to stop operating a specific automated system. The organisation must comply, immediately, within the terms of the order. The ability to do so — without operational crisis, without losing the evidence the investigation requires, without cascading failures in dependent processes — requires preparation that cannot be done in the moment the order arrives.

**When an incident is under investigation.** An incident has occurred. The system that produced it must be suspended while the investigation proceeds — to prevent further harm and to preserve the evidential record in the state it was in at the time of the failure. Stopping the system for this purpose requires both the technical capability and the governance authority to act before the investigation's conclusions are known.

**When unexpected behaviour emerges.** A system begins producing outputs that nobody anticipated — not clearly harmful yet, but anomalous in ways that cannot be immediately explained. The prudent response is to stop the system while the anomaly is understood. That response requires the capability to stop it without requiring a lengthy governance process to authorise the pause.

**When the organisation tests its own control.** The most important moment for this question is not a crisis. It is the governance review, the business continuity exercise, the operational test — the occasion on which the organisation asks, in a controlled setting, whether it can actually do what its governance documentation says it can. The organisation that tests this capability before it needs it governs. The one that assumes the capability exists without testing it is hoping.
