# RE-06 — Investigation Records

> **The investigation is underway. This card explains what records the inquiry itself must produce — and why the record of how you investigated matters as much as what you found.**

---

## What Is It?

Investigation records are the documents created during the course of an inquiry into an automated system incident — the formal record of how the investigation was conducted, what evidence was examined, what conclusions were reached, and what actions were decided upon as a result.

They are distinct from the evidence preserved at the point of the incident, which captures what the system did. Investigation records capture what the organisation did in response — how it examined the evidence, who was involved, what questions were asked, what answers were found, and what the investigation concluded.

An investigation record is not a summary of findings produced at the end. It is a contemporaneous record built throughout the investigation — capturing each step as it is taken, so that the process of inquiry is as well documented as its outcome.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

An investigation that reaches sound conclusions but leaves no record of how it got there is an investigation that cannot be verified. Regulators examining an incident response want to know not just what was found but how the inquiry was conducted — whether it was thorough, whether it was independent, whether it examined the right evidence, and whether its conclusions followed from what it found.

Without investigation records, those questions cannot be answered. The organisation says it investigated thoroughly. It cannot show what thorough looked like. That is a weak position before any external body that has the authority to conduct its own inquiry.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Investigations that reach convenient conclusions.** Where investigation records are sparse or created retrospectively, there is no way to verify that the process was genuine. An investigation that consistently finds no systemic fault, no management failure, and no need for significant change — but whose records do not show how those conclusions were reached — is an investigation whose credibility cannot be assessed.

**Scope that was too narrow.** Investigation records reveal what the inquiry examined and what it did not. An investigation that focused on the immediate technical cause of an incident without examining the governance failures that allowed it to occur may have produced an accurate but incomplete conclusion. Records show whether the scope was adequate — and regulators will draw their own conclusions if it was not.

**Actions that were agreed but never implemented.** Investigation records typically include action points arising from findings. Where those actions are recorded but follow-up is not, there is no evidence that remediation was completed. A subsequent incident of the same type, against a backdrop of an investigation that recommended the same remediation previously, is a significantly more serious governance finding.

**Records created to satisfy external scrutiny rather than to document genuine inquiry.** Investigation records produced after the fact — written to describe a process that did not occur, or that occurred differently from the account — are not investigation records. They are a governance fiction that becomes a legal problem the moment external scrutiny reaches them.

Responsibility for creating and maintaining investigation records sits with whoever leads the investigation. That person is accountable not just for the findings but for the integrity of the record of how those findings were reached.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Investigation records are required whenever a formal inquiry into an automated system incident is conducted:

- **Financial services** — records of inquiries into automated trading, lending, or fraud system failures, including the evidence examined, the conclusions reached, and the remediation agreed
- **Healthcare** — documentation of investigations into automated diagnostic or clinical decision system failures, including clinical review, root cause analysis, and safety recommendations
- **Public services** — formal records of inquiries into automated eligibility or enforcement system failures, including scope, methodology, findings, and policy implications
- **Employment** — records of investigations into automated screening or monitoring system failures with employment consequences for affected individuals
- **Platform and content systems** — documentation of inquiries into significant automated moderation or recommendation failures, including scope, evidence reviewed, and remediation commitments
- **Any organisation responding to a regulatory investigation or significant complaint** — where the record of the organisation's own inquiry will be examined alongside the regulator's

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes investigation records seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Is there a contemporaneous record of the investigation — created as it proceeded rather than reconstructed at the end?
- Does the record capture scope, methodology, evidence examined, and the reasoning behind conclusions — not just the conclusions themselves?
- Are action points arising from the investigation recorded with named owners, defined timeframes, and a mechanism for verifying completion?
- Is there a record of who conducted the investigation, what their qualifications were, and whether they had appropriate independence from the systems and functions under review?
- Are investigation records stored in a way that ensures they can be produced to regulators, courts, or affected individuals in a usable form?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation's investigation produced findings without a verifiable process. Findings without process are conclusions without evidence — and conclusions without evidence do not withstand external scrutiny.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Creates a verifiable record of how the investigation was conducted — demonstrating thoroughness and independence | Contemporaneous record-keeping during investigation adds effort at a time when the investigation itself is the priority |
| Supports regulatory compliance by demonstrating that the organisation responded to incidents with genuine inquiry | Investigations conducted under time and resource pressure may produce records that are complete in form but thin in substance |
| Enables follow-up — action points are recorded and can be tracked to completion | The independence of an investigation is difficult to demonstrate from records alone when the investigators are internal |
| Builds organisational learning — recorded findings and recommendations are available for future reference | Investigation records that capture conclusions without the reasoning behind them are of limited value in subsequent scrutiny |
| Protects the organisation in litigation and regulatory proceedings by demonstrating good-faith response | Investigations whose records reveal scope limitations or methodological weaknesses may do more harm than good when produced externally |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: regulators and courts expect organisations to be able to produce records of their incident investigations — not just their findings — and treat the absence or inadequacy of those records as evidence of deficient response.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require organisations to document their response to personal data incidents, including the measures taken and the reasoning behind them. That documentation obligation extends to the investigative process, not just the outcome.

Regulatory frameworks in financial services, healthcare, and other sectors increasingly examine the quality of internal investigations as part of their own inquiries — assessing whether the organisation's investigation was thorough, independent, and honest, or whether it was designed to manage the incident rather than understand it.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that investigated an incident but cannot produce records demonstrating how that investigation was conducted is in a significantly weaker position than one that can. The investigation may have been entirely sound. Without records, that cannot be shown. And an investigation that cannot be shown to have been sound is, for external purposes, an investigation whose soundness is in doubt.
