# GV-N10 — What Certification Is Not

> **The certificate has been awarded. Now check: what does it actually attest to — and what has it already stopped being the moment it was issued?**

---

## What It Is Not

Certification is not proof of safety. It is not ongoing compliance, regulatory approval, a guarantee of governance quality, or a permanent status that persists once achieved. It is not a defence against liability, a substitute for internal governance, or a signal that the certified system or organisation has been found adequate by a regulator.

Certification, properly understood, is a formal finding — made by an accredited body, at a defined point in time, against defined criteria — that the system, process, or organisation assessed met those criteria at the time of assessment.

That definition contains everything important about what certification is and is not. It is a finding, not a guarantee. It covers a point in time, not an ongoing state. It assesses against specific criteria, which may not encompass everything that matters. And it is issued by a certification body, not a regulator — whose authority derives from accreditation, not from law.

What organisations most often confuse with certification is one or more of the following:

**Proof of safety or quality.** Certification establishes that defined requirements were met at assessment. It does not establish that the system is safe, that it produces fair outcomes, or that it will continue to perform as assessed. The criteria a certification scheme applies define what the scheme considers relevant. They do not define safety, quality, or governance adequacy comprehensively. A certified system can fail. A certified organisation can cause harm. The certification addressed the criteria it addressed.

**Ongoing compliance.** Certification is issued following an assessment. After the assessment, the organisation continues to operate. The system continues to run. Practices evolve. Personnel change. Risks shift. The certification reflects how things were at the point of assessment. It does not monitor what happens afterward. An organisation that was certified two years ago and has not been reassessed has a two-year-old certification — which is evidence of a past state, not a current one.

**Regulatory approval.** Certification bodies are not regulators. They are independent bodies accredited to assess conformity with defined standards. Their findings do not constitute regulatory approval, do not satisfy regulatory obligations unless law has specifically provided for that, and do not protect an organisation from regulatory action. A regulator examining an organisation's governance does not treat certification as equivalent to a regulatory finding of compliance.

**A defence against liability.** Certification is sometimes treated as protection — if something goes wrong, the organisation can point to its certification as evidence that it took its obligations seriously. Certification is relevant evidence. It is not a shield. It establishes that requirements were met at assessment. It does not establish that requirements were met at the time the harm occurred, that the certified criteria encompassed what mattered in this situation, or that meeting those criteria was sufficient to satisfy the legal standard.

---

## Why Confusion Matters

When organisations treat certification as a permanent status, they stop asking whether the governance the certification assessed is still functioning. The certificate was awarded. The governance question has been answered. In the time since, the system has been updated, the organisation has restructured, risks have evolved, and regulatory requirements have changed. The certificate has not changed. It still says what it always said — that requirements were met, on that date, by that version of the system.

The deeper problem is that certification, when mistaken for governance achievement, becomes a destination rather than a waypoint. Organisations govern in order to achieve certification, achieve certification, and then govern in order to maintain it at the minimum level required for renewal. Governance becomes a certification maintenance exercise — calibrated to the assessment criteria rather than to the actual governance requirements of the systems in operation.

---

## Where Confusion Appears

**Certification cited in regulatory responses as evidence of current compliance.** An organisation facing a regulatory inquiry produces its certification as evidence that its governance is adequate. The certification is three years old, predates a significant system update, and was issued under a standard that has since been revised. The regulator assesses current compliance. The organisation offered historical evidence of past conformity.

**Procurement requirements satisfied by certification without assessment of currency.** An organisation requiring governance assurance from vendors accepts certification without asking when it was issued, what it covered, or whether the certified state reflects current practice. A certificate that was accurate when issued may not be accurate now — but it looks the same.

**Certification scope misrepresented as comprehensive coverage.** A system is certified to a standard that addresses specific technical and process requirements. The organisation represents the certification as evidence of comprehensive governance adequacy. The certification covered what it covered. The governance questions it did not address remain open.

**Certification renewal treated as reconfirmation of original finding.** Periodic recertification involves reassessment against defined criteria. Where recertification is treated as a formality — conducted without genuine examination of whether governance has kept pace with evolving systems and risks — it produces a renewed certificate that attests to less than it appears to.

---

## How To Tell The Difference

Five questions that distinguish genuine governance assurance from certification:

- Is the certification current — was it issued recently enough to reflect the current state of the system, the organisation, and the regulatory environment?
- Does the certification scope encompass the governance questions that matter most in this context — or does it address a subset of requirements that leaves significant governance questions unassessed?
- Between certification assessments, does the organisation have internal mechanisms for maintaining the governance the certification assessed — so that the certified state is maintained, not just restored before each assessment?
- Does the organisation understand what the certification does and does not establish — and communicate that accurately to regulators, partners, and others who rely on it?
- Is certification a waypoint in the organisation's governance — one assurance mechanism among several — or is it the primary basis on which the organisation believes its governance is adequate?

| Common Substitutes | What Certification Actually Is |
|---|---|
| Proof of safety or quality | A finding that defined criteria were met at the point of assessment |
| Ongoing compliance assurance | A snapshot — the certified state at a moment in time |
| Regulatory approval | An independent assessment finding — not a regulatory determination |
| A defence against liability | Relevant evidence — not a shield against consequences |
| Comprehensive governance adequacy | Conformity with the specific criteria the certification scheme applied |

## The Law

Certification has no legal force beyond what law or contract has given it. In some regulatory contexts, certification under defined schemes is given specific legal significance — as a route to demonstrating conformity with legal requirements, or as a condition of authorisation. In those contexts, the legal significance is precisely defined and limited. Outside those defined contexts, certification is evidence — relevant, useful, but not determinative.

Regulators in most jurisdictions assess governance by examining current practice against current legal requirements. Certification is relevant context. It is not a finding of legal compliance, and regulators are not bound by it. An organisation that was certified under a standard whose requirements do not fully overlap with the legal requirements under examination has not demonstrated legal compliance by demonstrating certification.

AI-specific legislation is advancing conformity assessment mechanisms that reference certification — but those mechanisms are designed with specific limitations and renewal requirements that reflect exactly the temporal constraint this card addresses. Certification under AI legislation frameworks is not permanent. It is time-limited, scope-limited, and subject to ongoing post-market surveillance requirements that treat the certified state as a baseline, not an endpoint.

The practical legal risk is direct: a certificate is a historical document. It attests to a past state. The governance question — whether obligations are being met now — is a present one. An organisation that answers a present question with a historical document has not answered the question. It has produced evidence that there was once a point at which a defined set of criteria were met. What happened between then and now is the governance question that certification cannot answer.
