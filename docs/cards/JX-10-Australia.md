# JX-10 — Australia

> **Australia has robust existing law that applies to AI systems right now — and a government that has signalled mandatory guardrails are coming for high-risk applications. The governance gap between current obligations and future regulation is narrowing.**

---

## The Framework

Australia's AI governance framework combines existing law — privacy, consumer protection, discrimination, corporate governance — with voluntary governance guidance and a government commitment to mandatory safeguards for high-risk AI.

The Privacy Act 1988, currently under review and reform, applies to AI systems that handle personal information of Australians. The Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) govern how personal information is collected, used and disclosed. The government's review of the Privacy Act has proposed strengthening automated decision-making provisions and individual rights.

The Australian Consumer Law (ACL) prohibits misleading and deceptive conduct and unconscionable behaviour — provisions that apply directly to AI systems in consumer-facing contexts. The Competition and Consumer Act governs algorithmic pricing and anti-competitive conduct. The Equality legislation — the Racial Discrimination Act, Sex Discrimination Act and Disability Discrimination Act — applies to discriminatory automated decisions.

The government published a framework for AI regulation in 2024 that proposes mandatory safeguards for high-risk AI applications. The framework distinguishes between general governance guidance (voluntary) and sector-specific mandatory requirements for high-risk systems. Consultation on mandatory guardrails is ongoing.

The AI Safety Standard, developed by Standards Australia, and the voluntary AI Ethics Framework published by the Department of Industry provide practical governance guidance that organisations are increasingly expected to reference even where compliance is not yet mandatory.

---

## Key Regulators

**Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC)** — Privacy Act enforcement, AI and privacy guidance. Has published specific guidance on privacy and AI, including on automated decision-making.

**Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC)** — Australian Consumer Law enforcement, digital platform regulation, algorithmic pricing. The ACCC has been among the most active competition authorities globally in examining AI and digital market concerns.

**Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC)** — AI in financial services, investment management, credit. Has published guidance on AI in financial advice and is developing specific AI governance expectations.

**Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA)** — AI in banking, insurance and superannuation. Has published guidance on model risk management.

**Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC)** — discrimination law, human rights impact of AI. Published a significant report on human rights and technology.

**Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA)** — AI as a medical device, software as a medical device framework.

## What Triggers Obligations

**The Privacy Act** triggers whenever an AI system handles personal information of Australians. Automated decision-making that significantly affects individuals is an active area of Privacy Act reform — proposed amendments would strengthen individual rights and organisational obligations in this area.

**The Australian Consumer Law** triggers in any consumer-facing AI context. The prohibition on misleading and deceptive conduct applies to AI-generated communications, AI-driven pricing, and claims about AI capabilities. The ACCC has specifically addressed AI washing — misleading claims about AI functionality — as a consumer law concern.

**The Corporations Act and ASIC guidance** trigger for AI in financial services. ASIC has published regulatory guidance on the use of AI in financial advice that sets specific expectations for risk management, human oversight and client communication.

**APRA's prudential standards** trigger for AI in banking and insurance. APRA's model risk management guidance — CPS 220 (risk management) and related standards — applies to AI models used in regulated activities.

**Discrimination law** triggers for automated decisions in employment, finance, housing and services. The outcome-based discrimination standard applies — discriminatory effects are actionable under the Racial Discrimination Act, Sex Discrimination Act and Disability Discrimination Act.

## Governance Connection

Australia's governance environment is particularly relevant to organisations in financial services, where ASIC and APRA have developed some of the most specific AI governance expectations outside the UK and EU.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is addressed through the Corporations Act director duties framework and APRA's governance standards. APRA expects boards to understand and oversee material risks including model and AI risk. Named accountability for AI systems is an expected governance standard for regulated entities.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is a specific expectation in ASIC's guidance on AI in financial advice. ASIC has published that human oversight of AI-generated advice is expected and that passive oversight — approving outputs without genuine review — does not satisfy the standard.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is required under the Privacy Act and Australian Consumer Law. Individuals must be informed about automated processing of their personal information. Misleading silence — not disclosing that AI is being used in a consumer interaction — is actionable.

**Fairness** (→ SP-19, LL-03) is enforced through discrimination law across multiple domains. The ACCC has also raised concerns about AI systems that exploit consumer vulnerability — a consumer protection dimension of fairness beyond the discrimination law framework.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under Privacy Act accountability obligations, ASIC financial services record-keeping rules, and APRA prudential standards. The OAIC can require organisations to produce documentation of their AI processing activities in investigations.
