# LL-01 — EU Artificial Intelligence Act

> **The first comprehensive legal framework designed specifically for AI systems. It defines what counts as high risk, what must be demonstrated before deployment, and what regulators can do when obligations are not met.**

---

## What It Covers

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act is the first binding legal framework in the world to regulate AI systems specifically — not as a side effect of data protection or consumer law, but as a primary subject in its own right.

It applies to organisations that develop, sell, deploy or operate AI systems within the European Union — including organisations based outside the EU whose systems affect people inside it. It classifies AI systems by risk level, imposes specific obligations on each risk tier, and establishes enforcement powers for national and European regulatory authorities.

The Act does not regulate all AI equally. Its requirements are calibrated to harm potential. Systems that present unacceptable risk are prohibited outright. Systems that present significant risk to individuals or society are classified as high risk and subject to the most demanding requirements. Lower-risk systems face lighter obligations — primarily transparency and notification requirements. Minimal-risk systems are largely unaffected.

The Act came into force in 2024 and is being phased in over several years, with the most demanding requirements for high-risk systems taking full effect in 2026 and 2027.

---

## Why AI Systems Trigger It

An organisation triggers the EU AI Act when it develops, places on the market, or puts into service an AI system that falls within the Act's scope — which is deliberately broad.

**High-risk classification is the central trigger.** The Act defines high-risk systems by reference to their intended use. Systems used in employment and recruitment decisions, credit and insurance assessments, benefit eligibility determinations, law enforcement, border control, education, and critical infrastructure all fall into high-risk categories by definition. The classification attaches to the use case, not the technology — the same underlying model may be high risk in one deployment context and minimal risk in another.

**The obligations triggered by high-risk classification are substantial.** Before a high-risk system can be deployed, the organisation must conduct a conformity assessment — a structured examination of whether the system meets the Act's technical and governance requirements. Those requirements include maintaining comprehensive technical documentation, implementing a risk management system that operates throughout the system's lifecycle, ensuring the training data used meets defined quality standards, designing the system to enable human oversight, and achieving defined accuracy and robustness standards.

**During deployment, the obligations continue.** High-risk systems must be monitored after they enter service. Serious incidents must be reported to authorities. The conformity assessment must be updated when the system changes significantly. Records must be maintained and made available to regulators on request.

**The governance trigger is direct.** An organisation that deploys a high-risk AI system without conducting a conformity assessment, without maintaining required documentation, or without implementing required oversight arrangements has not made a compliance choice. It has committed a legal breach — one that applies from the first day the system operated without the required governance in place.

## Governance Connection

The EU AI Act translates governance obligations into legal requirements more directly than any framework before it. The concepts it requires in law are the same concepts the Atlas defines as governance practice.

**Risk classification** (→ LR-13) is the Act's organising principle. Every governance decision about a system — what documentation to maintain, what oversight to implement, what monitoring to conduct — depends on knowing the system's risk classification under the Act.

**Technical documentation** (→ RE-13) is a legal requirement for high-risk systems. The Act specifies what that documentation must contain. An organisation that does not maintain it is not just ungoverned — it is non-compliant.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is a mandatory design requirement for high-risk systems. The Act requires that systems be designed to allow human intervention. The capability must exist before deployment, not be added afterwards.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01) is addressed through the Act's distinction between providers — those who develop systems — and deployers — those who put them into service. Both carry obligations. The deployer cannot transfer its obligations to the provider by contract.

**Records and evidence** (→ RE-01, RE-07) are required throughout. The Act specifies records that must exist, how long they must be kept, and what regulators can demand to see. An organisation that governed its systems without these records has a historical gap that cannot be closed by what it does from the point of discovery.

**Enforcement** (→ LR-07) under the Act includes fines of up to 35 million euros or seven percent of global annual turnover for the most serious breaches — prohibited system violations — and up to 15 million euros or three percent of global annual turnover for high-risk system obligation failures.

---

## Jurisdictions

The EU AI Act applies directly in all 27 EU member states. It has extraterritorial reach — it applies to organisations outside the EU whose systems produce outputs used within the EU. It is the reference framework that other jurisdictions are actively examining as they develop their own AI-specific regulation.
