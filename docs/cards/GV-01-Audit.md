# GV-01 — Audit

> **Everyone refers to audits. This card explains what an audit actually is, what it examines, and how it differs from the monitoring that happens every day.**

---

## What Is It?

An audit is a structured, independent examination — conducted at a defined point in time, against defined criteria, by a person or body with the standing to conduct it — that produces documented findings about the state of what was examined.

Three properties distinguish an audit from other governance activities.

**Structure** — an audit follows a defined methodology. It does not examine whatever the examiner finds interesting. It examines what the audit scope and criteria require, systematically, with the consistency needed to produce findings that can be relied upon and compared over time.

**Independence** — an audit is conducted by someone who is not responsible for the activity being examined. Independence is not simply organisational distance. It requires that the examiner has no interest in the outcome and no relationship with the process being examined that would compromise their ability to reach an honest finding. Without independence, an examination is a review. It is not an audit.

**Documentation** — an audit produces a record. The findings, the methodology, the evidence base, and the conclusions are documented in a form that can be produced, examined, and relied upon. Verbal feedback from an examiner is not an audit finding. The documented record is.

Audits of automated systems typically examine one or more of: technical adequacy — whether the system was built and operates as specified; governance adequacy — whether the oversight, accountability, and control arrangements meet defined standards; compliance adequacy — whether the organisation's practices meet the legal and regulatory requirements that apply; and performance adequacy — whether the system performs within acceptable parameters.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Monitoring tells an organisation what is happening continuously. Audit tells it whether what is happening is adequate — whether the processes, controls, and governance arrangements in place meet the standards they are supposed to meet.

Those are different questions. Monitoring detects operational anomalies — outputs that diverge from expected patterns, performance metrics that fall outside acceptable ranges, incidents that require response. Audit examines whether the governance framework itself is sound — whether the monitoring is adequate, whether the accountability structures function, whether the documentation is complete, whether the oversight arrangements are genuine.

An organisation that monitors without auditing knows what its systems are doing but may not know whether its governance of those systems is adequate. An organisation that audits without monitoring produces periodic assessments of a governance framework that operates without continuous observation between assessments. Both are needed. They serve different governance purposes and cannot substitute for each other.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Audit findings that are received but not acted upon.** An audit produces findings. The organisation reviews them, notes the gaps identified, and files the report. The gaps are not remediated. The next audit finds the same gaps. The audit has become a recurring description of persistent governance failure rather than a mechanism for addressing it. The organisation has the audit. It has not used it.

**Audit scope that avoids the most significant governance questions.** Audit scope is defined before the audit begins. Scope that focuses on easily assessed technical and process questions, while excluding harder questions about governance culture, accountability genuineness, and the gap between documented and actual practice, produces findings that are accurate within their scope and misleading as an overall picture of governance adequacy.

**Independence that is nominal rather than genuine.** An audit conducted by a team with close relationships to the function being examined, by an internal team that reports to the same leadership, or by an external firm whose continued engagement depends on producing findings the client finds comfortable, does not have the independence that makes audit findings reliable. The appearance of independence and the reality of it are different things — and the difference matters when findings are relied upon.

**Audit frequency that does not match the pace of change.** A system that is audited annually but updated quarterly has a governance audit trail that is always nine months behind the current system. The audit assessed the system as it was. The governance question concerns the system as it is. Annual audit of rapidly changing systems produces findings that are historically accurate and currently unreliable.

## Where Does It Appear?

Audit of automated systems occurs across every sector where those systems are subject to governance accountability:

- **Financial services** — internal audit of model risk management, algorithmic trading controls, and credit decision systems; external audit of financial statements that include automated system reliance; regulatory audit of compliance with sector requirements
- **Data protection** — supervisory authority audits of automated processing compliance; internal audit of data protection governance and records management
- **Healthcare** — clinical audit of automated diagnostic and triage system performance; regulatory inspection of clinical governance arrangements
- **AI governance** — conformity assessment audits under AI legislation; third-party audits of AI governance frameworks; internal audit of AI accountability structures
- **Information security** — audit of technical controls, access management, and security governance in automated systems
- **Equality and fairness** — audit of automated system outcomes across protected characteristic groups; examination of discrimination risk management

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes audit seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Is audit scope defined to address the governance questions that matter most — including the hard ones about accountability genuineness and governance culture — rather than the questions that are easiest to assess?
- Is the independence of audit examiners genuine — are they free from relationships and interests that would compromise their ability to reach honest findings?
- When audit findings identify governance gaps, is there a defined remediation process — with owners, timeframes, and tracking to completion — that ensures findings are acted upon?
- Is audit frequency calibrated to the pace of change in the systems and governance arrangements being examined — or is an annual cycle applied uniformly regardless of how quickly things change?
- Does the organisation treat audit findings as governance intelligence — signals about where improvement is needed — or as compliance events to be managed and closed?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation's audit programme may be producing findings that are not acted upon, examining scope that avoids the most significant questions, or operating on a frequency that produces assessments of a historical state rather than the current one.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Independent audit provides external validation of governance that internal assessment cannot — the examiner has no interest in a favourable finding | Audit is a point-in-time assessment — it reflects governance as it was during the audit period, not as it is continuously |
| Structured audit methodology produces findings that are comparable over time and across systems | Audit scope is defined in advance — governance failures outside the defined scope are not found |
| Documented audit findings provide an evidential record that demonstrates governance activity occurred and was externally assessed | Audit quality varies significantly with the independence, competence, and rigour of the examiner |
| Audit findings that identify gaps provide actionable intelligence for governance improvement | Findings that are not acted upon become evidence of known governance gaps — potentially more damaging than undiscovered ones |
| Regular audit creates governance rhythm — defined occasions for stepping back from operational activity to assess whether the governance framework is working | Audit is resource-intensive for both the examining body and the organisation being examined |

## The Law

Audit obligations for automated systems arise across multiple regulatory frameworks — as mandatory requirements in regulated sectors, as conditions of authorisation, and as components of conformity assessment under AI-specific legislation.

Financial services regulation in many jurisdictions requires independent internal audit of model risk management, algorithmic trading controls, and automated decision systems as a standard governance requirement. Healthcare regulation imposes clinical audit obligations that extend to automated systems used in patient care.

AI-specific legislation is advancing audit requirements for high-risk systems that include both pre-deployment conformity assessment — a structured examination against defined requirements before the system enters service — and post-market monitoring that has the character of ongoing audit. Third-party audit of high-risk AI systems is emerging as a regulatory requirement in multiple frameworks.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that relies on internal assessment without independent audit for systems where independent audit is required has not met its governance obligations — regardless of how thorough the internal assessment was. Independence is not a procedural nicety. It is the property that makes audit findings reliable rather than self-serving.
