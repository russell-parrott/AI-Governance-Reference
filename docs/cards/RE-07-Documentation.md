# RE-07 — Documentation

> **Reviews need records. This card introduces the concept of writing things down — and why it matters more than it sounds.**

---

## What Is It?

Documentation is the practice of creating and maintaining written records of decisions, processes, assessments, and activities related to an automated system — records that exist independently of the people who created them and that can be produced, examined, and relied upon by others.

It is not note-taking. It is not filing emails. Documentation is a deliberate governance activity — the systematic creation of records that capture what was decided, what was assessed, what was found, and who was responsible. Records produced with sufficient clarity, completeness, and structure that someone who was not present can understand what happened and why.

Documentation is what makes governance visible. Every other governance activity — monitoring, review, testing, approval — produces value only to the extent that it is documented. An undocumented governance activity is, for practical purposes, an activity that cannot be demonstrated to have occurred.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Governance depends on memory — organisational memory that persists beyond the individuals who were present when decisions were made. People leave. Roles change. Time passes. The automated system continues operating. When questions arise — from regulators, from affected individuals, from internal reviews, from litigation — the answers must come from records, not from recollection.

Documentation is also what makes accountability real over time. It is easy to say, after the fact, that proper processes were followed, that risks were assessed, that decisions were made responsibly. Documentation is the difference between saying it and being able to show it.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Governance that happened but cannot be demonstrated.** An organisation that conducted genuine assessments, held meaningful reviews, and made considered decisions, but did not document them, cannot prove any of it. In a regulatory investigation or litigation, undocumented governance is effectively absent governance. The work was done. The evidence does not exist.

**Organisational memory that walks out the door.** When the people who made decisions about an automated system leave the organisation, their knowledge of what was decided and why leaves with them — unless it was documented. Systems outlast the people who built and governed them. Documentation is what bridges that gap.

**Inconsistent practice across people and time.** Without documented standards and records, governance activities are performed differently by different people at different times. Consistency cannot be demonstrated because consistency was never recorded. When an incident requires reconstruction of what the governance process looked like, the answer is different depending on who is asked.

**Documentation that exists but cannot be found or used.** Records stored in personal drives, buried in email threads, or scattered across systems without structure are not usable documentation. They exist. They cannot be produced on demand, cannot be searched systematically, and cannot be relied upon in an investigation. Documentation that cannot be retrieved is documentation that does not function.

Responsibility for documentation sits with whoever holds governance authority over the system — not with the individuals who perform governance activities. Creating records is everyone's obligation. Ensuring a documentation system exists and is used is a leadership responsibility.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Documentation is a requirement at every stage and in every governance activity related to automated systems:

- **Deployment** — records of approval decisions, risk assessments, intended use statements, and sign-off by authorised individuals
- **Monitoring and review** — records of what was observed, when, by whom, and what conclusions were reached
- **Testing** — records of what was tested, under what conditions, what was found, and who reviewed the results
- **Changes** — records of what was proposed, what impact assessment was conducted, who approved it, and when it was implemented
- **Incidents** — records of what happened, when it was identified, how it was escalated, and what was done in response
- **Vendor and third-party governance** — records of contractual obligations, audit findings, and ongoing oversight activity

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes documentation seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Is there a defined standard for what must be documented in relation to this system, and is that standard being followed?
- Are records stored in a way that makes them retrievable on demand — not dependent on searching personal files or asking individuals to reconstruct events?
- Do documentation records capture not just what was decided but the basis for the decision — what was reviewed, what was considered, and who was responsible?
- Is there a retention policy that ensures records are kept for long enough to serve their governance and legal purposes?
- When was the documentation for this system last reviewed to confirm it is complete, current, and accurate?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation's governance exists in activity but not in evidence. The reviews happened. The assessments were made. The decisions were reached. None of it can be shown. In governance terms, that is the same as none of it having happened.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Creates a permanent, retrievable record of governance activity that survives personnel changes | Documentation takes time and discipline — under operational pressure it is frequently deferred or abbreviated |
| Enables accountability over time — decisions can be traced to the people and processes that made them | Documentation that captures conclusions without reasoning provides a record but not an explanation |
| Supports regulatory compliance by providing evidence of governance activity on demand | Poorly structured documentation systems make records difficult to retrieve, reducing their practical value |
| Protects the organisation in investigation and litigation by demonstrating that due process was followed | Excessive documentation requirements can create compliance overhead that displaces genuine governance activity |
| Creates organisational memory that persists beyond the individuals who were present | Documentation standards that are not enforced produce inconsistent records that cannot be relied upon |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: organisations must be able to produce documentary evidence of their governance activities in relation to automated systems — on demand, not reconstructed after the fact.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions impose explicit record-keeping obligations on organisations processing personal data through automated systems. Those obligations require not just that governance activities occur but that they are documented in ways that demonstrate compliance. The absence of records is treated as evidence of absent governance, not as evidence of informal compliance.

AI-specific legislation is advancing detailed documentation requirements for high-risk systems, specifying what must be recorded, how long records must be kept, and in what circumstances they must be produced to regulators. Documentation is increasingly treated as a substantive governance obligation, not an administrative by-product.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that cannot produce documentation of its governance activities is in the same position as one that did not conduct them. The standard is not whether governance happened. It is whether governance can be demonstrated — and demonstration requires records.
