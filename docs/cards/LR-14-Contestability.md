# LR-14 — Contestability

> **Contestability is the practical ability of a person to challenge an automated decision that affects them — not merely the legal right to do so. The right exists in law. The ability depends on whether the organisation has built the processes to make it real.**

---

## What Contestability Is

Contestability is the genuine, operational capacity for a person affected by an automated decision to challenge that decision and have the challenge meaningfully considered. It requires more than a complaints channel. It requires that the person has sufficient information to formulate a challenge, that the organisation has the records and processes to consider it, that someone with appropriate authority reviews it, and that the outcome of the review can result in a changed decision.

Contestability has both a legal dimension and a governance dimension. The legal dimension — the right to contest — is addressed in automated decision rights (→ LR-01). The governance dimension — whether contestability is real in practice — is what this card addresses. The gap between the two is where most contestability failures occur.

A legal right to contest an automated decision is not satisfied by providing a complaints email address. It is not satisfied by a process that reviews complaints but never changes decisions. It is not satisfied by a process that operates so slowly that the decision has already taken irreversible effect before the challenge is considered. Contestability requires that challenge is possible, that it is meaningful, and that it can make a difference.

---

## Why Contestability Is a Governance Problem

Contestability failures are structural. They arise from the same conditions that produce other governance failures: absence of records that would allow a decision to be reviewed; absence of human reviewers with the authority to change a decision; absence of explanation sufficient for a person to know what to challenge; and absence of processes designed to receive, consider and respond to challenges.

The scale of automated decision-making compounds the problem. When systems make thousands or millions of decisions, the fraction of people who exercise challenge rights is small. The absence of contestability may be invisible in aggregate — challenges are rare, the process handles what few arrive, and the structural inability of most affected people to mount an effective challenge goes unexamined.

Regulators are increasingly attentive to this gap. The EU AI Act requires that high-risk AI systems have mechanisms for human oversight and redress. GDPR requires meaningful human review of solely automated decisions with significant effects. The standard being applied is not whether a challenge mechanism nominally exists — it is whether it functions.

---

## What Contestability Requires

**Accessible challenge routes** — the process for challenging an automated decision must be communicated to affected individuals in terms they can understand, at the point when the decision is made.

**Adequate explanation** — an individual cannot challenge what they do not understand. Contestability requires that affected persons receive an explanation (→ SP-16, RE-10) sufficient to identify the basis for a challenge.

**Records to support review** — the organisation must retain the records necessary to review a challenged decision — the inputs, the model state, the decision and its context. Without those records, review is impossible.

**Genuine human review** — challenge must be reviewed by a person with the authority and information to reach a different conclusion. Review that mechanically confirms the original automated output is not review — it is a second automated decision performed by a person.

**Decision authority** — the reviewer must have the authority to change the decision. Review without authority is not contestability — it is the appearance of it.

**Timely response** — contestability requires that challenges are considered before the decision has produced irreversible effects. A review process that takes months to reach a conclusion about a decision that took effect in days has not provided meaningful contestability.

**Documented outcomes** — the outcome of challenge processes must be documented. Patterns in challenge outcomes — the rate at which original decisions are upheld, overturned or modified — are governance information that should inform system review.

---

## Governance Connection

Contestability connects the legal rights framework to the operational governance of the Atlas. Automated decision rights (→ LR-01) establish the legal entitlement. Human review (→ CS-03) and human override (→ CS-13) are the operational mechanisms. Explainability (→ SP-16) and decision records (→ RE-02) are the evidential prerequisites. Redress (→ LR-15) is what follows a successful challenge. The governance questions ask whether these capabilities exist in practice — contestability is the test of whether they do.
