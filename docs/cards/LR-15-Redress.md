# LR-15 — Redress

> **Redress is what happens after a successful challenge to an automated decision — the correction, the remedy, the reversal of harm. Without redress, contestability has no consequence and accountability has no meaning.**

---

## What Redress Is

Redress is the set of actions available to correct the effects of an automated decision that was wrong, unfair, unlawful or harmful. It includes reversing the decision itself, correcting records that were created as a result of it, compensating for losses caused by it, and taking steps to prevent the same error from affecting others.

Redress is the endpoint of the accountability chain. Governance structures exist to prevent harm. When they fail — when a system produces a decision that should not have been made — redress is how the harm is addressed. An accountability framework that can identify a governance failure but cannot deliver a remedy for the person harmed by it is incomplete.

Redress takes several forms depending on the nature of the decision and the harm caused:

**Decision reversal** — changing or cancelling the original automated decision and any actions taken as a result of it.

**Record correction** — amending data records that were created, updated or shared as a result of the incorrect decision — credit files, employment records, regulatory filings, shared databases.

**Consequential remedy** — addressing harm caused by reliance on the incorrect decision by third parties — credit denied on the basis of a flawed score, employment lost following an automated assessment, benefits refused on the basis of incorrect data.

**Systemic correction** — where a decision error reflects a systematic problem with the system, redress includes identifying and correcting the decisions of all affected individuals — not only those who challenged.

---

## Why Redress Is a Governance Problem

Redress failures are among the most consequential outcomes of AI governance failure because they occur after all other governance controls have already failed. The system made a wrong decision. The oversight mechanisms did not catch it. The person affected managed to challenge it. And then the organisation was unable — or unwilling — to correct the harm.

The barriers to effective redress are often structural. Records may not exist to identify what records need correction. Authority to reverse a decision may be unclear or too high in the organisation to act quickly. Third-party records created on the basis of the incorrect decision may be outside the organisation's control. Compensation frameworks may not exist. And the scale of automated decision-making means that systemic errors may have affected thousands of people, most of whom never challenged and will never receive redress unless the organisation proactively identifies and corrects them.

Regulators treat the absence of effective redress seriously. The EU AI Act, GDPR and national consumer protection frameworks all require that individuals have access to effective remedies for AI-related harms. "Effective" is the operative word — a remedy that exists on paper but cannot be delivered in practice does not satisfy the standard.

---

## What Redress Requires

**Authority to act** — reversing a decision, correcting a record or authorising compensation must be within someone's authority. Redress that requires escalation through multiple levels of approval before anything can be done is redress that arrives too late or not at all.

**Records sufficient to reverse** — the organisation must retain the records necessary to understand what the incorrect decision did — what records were created, what actions were taken, what third parties were informed — so that the effects can be traced and corrected.

**Third-party correction processes** — where automated decisions result in records shared with third parties — credit reference agencies, regulatory bodies, other organisations — the process for correcting those records must be defined and operable.

**Proactive identification** — where a systematic error is discovered, governance requires that the organisation identifies all affected individuals, not only those who challenged. Waiting for challenges is not a redress mechanism for systemic failure.

**Compensation frameworks** — for harm that cannot be fully reversed — employment lost, opportunities missed, financial loss incurred — governance requires a framework for considering and delivering compensation. The absence of such a framework is not a governance position. It is a governance gap.

**Documentation** — redress actions must be documented: what was corrected, when, by whom, and what the outcome was. Redress records are governance records.

## Governance Connection

Redress is the terminal point of the governance chain that runs through contestability (→ LR-14), automated decision rights (→ LR-01), human override (→ CS-13) and accountability (→ AR-01). Traceability (→ RE-14) is the evidential prerequisite — redress requires knowing what the decision did before it can be corrected. Liability (→ LR-04) is the legal framework within which redress obligations arise. Record correction intersects with data protection rights (→ LR-10). The governance question redress raises is the most fundamental one in the Atlas: when a system caused harm, what did the organisation do about it?
