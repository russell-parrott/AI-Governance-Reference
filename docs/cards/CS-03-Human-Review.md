# CS-03 — Human Review

> **The alert has fired. Now what? A human looks at it. This card explains the review process.**

---

## What Is It?

Human review is the structured process by which a person examines an automated decision, a flagged output, or a system alert — and reaches a considered judgment about whether it is correct, appropriate, and safe to act on.

It is not a formality. It is not a rubber stamp applied to an automated output to create the appearance of human involvement. A genuine human review means that the person conducting it has the information, the knowledge, and the authority to reach an independent conclusion — and that their conclusion is what determines what happens next.

Human review is the point at which the organisation's accountability for its automated system becomes active rather than theoretical.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Automated systems produce outputs. Human review determines what those outputs mean and what should be done about them. Without genuine review, the organisation has not added human judgment to its automated process. It has added a human signature to a machine decision — which is a different thing entirely.

The distinction matters because accountability follows judgment. If a human reviewer genuinely examined an output and reached a conclusion, the organisation can demonstrate that a person with appropriate knowledge and authority stood behind that outcome. If the reviewer simply confirmed what the system produced without genuine scrutiny, the accountability is nominal. And when things go wrong, nominal accountability provides no protection.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Automation bias.** Human reviewers consistently exposed to automated outputs tend to defer to them, particularly when outputs are presented with apparent confidence. The review happens. The scrutiny does not. This is one of the most well-documented failure modes in human-machine systems and one of the hardest to govern against.

**Review without resource.** When reviewers are given more cases than they can meaningfully examine in the time available, review becomes a processing task rather than a judgment task. Volume pressure converts genuine oversight into a bottleneck that produces approvals, not assessments.

**No record of reasoning.** A review that produces a decision without recording the basis for it creates accountability in the moment but not over time. When a reviewed decision is later challenged, an organisation that cannot show what the reviewer considered and why is in a weak position.

**Inconsistent standards.** Where review is conducted by multiple people without defined criteria, the same automated output may be assessed differently depending on who reviews it. Inconsistency undermines both the fairness of the process and the organisation's ability to defend individual outcomes.

Responsibility for ensuring that human review is genuine — not nominal — sits with whoever manages the oversight function, not with the individual reviewer.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Human review is a component of governance wherever automated outputs carry consequences significant enough to require human confirmation:

- **Financial services** — review of automated credit, fraud, and insurance decisions that fall outside confident thresholds or have been flagged for scrutiny
- **Healthcare** — clinical review of automated diagnostic and triage outputs before consequential treatment decisions are made
- **Public services** — caseworker review of automated eligibility and assessment decisions, particularly in high-impact benefit or enforcement contexts
- **Employment** — management review of automated screening outputs before shortlisting or rejection decisions are communicated to candidates
- **Legal and compliance** — review of automated risk assessments and compliance flags before formal action is taken
- **Content and platforms** — editorial review of automated moderation decisions that have been appealed or that fall into ambiguous categories

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes human review seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Does the reviewer have access to all the information needed to reach an independent judgment?
- Is the reviewer's workload consistent with genuine scrutiny, or does volume make meaningful review impractical?
- Is there a record of what each review considered and what conclusion it reached?
- Are review criteria defined and applied consistently across all reviewers?
- When a reviewer disagrees with an automated output, what happens — and is that outcome recorded?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation has review in process but not in substance. A signature on a machine decision is not governance. It is cover.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Introduces genuine human judgment at the point where automated outputs have real consequences | Automation bias means human reviewers frequently defer to system outputs rather than scrutinising them |
| Creates a documented point of accountability for consequential decisions | High volumes make meaningful individual review impractical without significant resource |
| Provides a mechanism for catching errors and edge cases the system was not designed to handle | Inconsistent review criteria produce inconsistent outcomes, undermining both fairness and defensibility |
| Gives affected individuals a genuine human point of contact in the decision process | Review processes that are under-resourced create false assurance rather than genuine control |
| Supports regulatory compliance by demonstrating active human involvement | A review record that captures only the outcome, not the reasoning, is of limited value in an investigation |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is moving toward requirements that human review of automated decisions be genuine and demonstrable — not merely procedural.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions establish rights for individuals to obtain human review of automated decisions that significantly affect them. Those rights create a legal obligation on the organisation to maintain a review process that is substantive, not symbolic — and to be able to demonstrate that the review genuinely considered the individual's circumstances.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for human oversight that go beyond the existence of a review step to require evidence of its quality — that reviewers had adequate information, appropriate competence, and sufficient time to exercise genuine judgment.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that cannot demonstrate its human review process involved real scrutiny, applied consistent criteria, and produced documented reasoning is exposed when a reviewed decision is challenged — whether by an individual, a regulator, or a court.
