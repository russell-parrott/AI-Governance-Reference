# MK-05 — Internal Auditors

> **Internal auditors provide independent assurance that governance controls are functioning as designed. For AI systems, their challenge is assessing controls that are technically complex, rapidly evolving and often documented in ways that describe intent rather than practice.**

---

## What This Market Is

Internal auditors are independent assurance professionals within organisations. They examine whether governance frameworks, risk management processes and internal controls are adequate and operating effectively. They appear in any organisation with a formal governance structure — financial institutions, listed companies, public bodies, healthcare organisations and regulated entities of all kinds.

Their position in the governance structure is deliberately independent of the operational functions they audit. They report to the audit committee or equivalent oversight body, not to the management responsible for the activities they assess. Their role is to provide the board and senior management with objective evidence about whether controls are real or nominal — whether what the governance documentation says is happening is actually happening.

For AI systems, internal auditors occupy a critical and often uncomfortable position: they must provide assurance about systems they may not fully understand, operating under governance frameworks that may not have been designed for automated decision-making.

---

## Why AI Governance Matters to Them

Internal auditors face a structural challenge with AI governance: the technical complexity of automated systems outpaces the audit methodologies that most internal audit functions have developed to assess them. Traditional audit approaches — examining policies, testing controls, reviewing documentation — are necessary but not sufficient for AI governance assessment.

The professional stakes are significant. An internal audit function that provides assurance on AI governance without the capacity to assess it meaningfully is providing false assurance — exactly the outcome independent audit exists to prevent. When a governance failure occurs in a system that internal audit had assessed as adequately controlled, the audit function faces questions about the quality and adequacy of its work.

Internal auditors are also increasingly asked by boards and regulators to provide specific assessments of AI governance quality — a request that requires methodological development most internal audit functions are still undertaking.

---

## What Questions They Ask

Internal auditors approach AI governance through a controls assessment lens:

- Do governance controls for this system exist on paper and in practice?
- Are the controls designed to address the actual risks the system creates?
- Are the controls being operated by people who understand what they are doing and why?
- Is there an evidence trail that demonstrates the controls functioned — not just that they were designed?
- Are there gaps between the governance framework's stated scope and the systems it actually covers?
- What would a control failure look like, and would the organisation detect it?

Internal auditors are the professionals most focused on the gap between policy and practice — between what the governance documentation says and what the organisation is actually doing.

---

## What Evidence They Expect

Internal auditors require operational evidence — proof that controls functioned rather than proof that controls were designed:

- Governance documentation that describes controls in sufficient operational detail to be testable
- Operational records showing that controls were exercised — override logs, review records, escalation history
- Performance monitoring data and the documented responses to monitoring findings
- Incident records including root cause analysis and management response
- Training records confirming that staff responsible for controls understood their obligations
- Change management records showing that governance was maintained through system changes
- Evidence of independent challenge — that human oversight produced genuine review, not rubber-stamping

Internal auditors are specifically looking for the evidence that distinguishes functioning controls from documented controls.

---

## Where They Appear in Investigations

Internal auditors appear in AI governance investigations in two distinct roles. First, as the internal function whose prior assessments are examined — whether they identified risks that were not addressed, whether their methodology was adequate, whether their findings were acted on. Second, as a resource for reconstructing the governance record — internal audit's workpapers, findings and management responses create an independent contemporaneous record of governance quality.

In regulatory investigations, regulators frequently examine internal audit findings as evidence of whether the organisation knew about governance weaknesses. An internal audit report that identified a control gap — and which management did not address — is significant evidence in an enforcement proceeding.

Internal auditors may also be asked to conduct specific investigations or reviews in the aftermath of an AI governance failure, contributing to the organisation's understanding of what went wrong and providing evidence for regulatory and legal proceedings.

## Typical Failure Points

**Assurance without methodology.** Internal audit functions provide AI governance assurance using audit methodologies developed for non-AI controls. The assessment confirms that documentation exists and that stated controls are described — but does not test whether the controls address AI-specific risks or whether they function in practice.

**Scope that excludes the risk.** AI systems are audited as part of broader technology or operational audits rather than as a specific governance subject. The AI-specific risks — bias, drift, traceability, explainability — are not in scope because the audit was not designed to address them.

**Controls assessed at design, not at operation.** Internal audit tests whether controls were designed correctly rather than whether they operated effectively. The distinction matters for AI governance: a monitoring framework that was designed correctly but is not being operated, or an oversight process that is being rubber-stamped, will pass design-phase assessment and fail operation-phase assessment.

**Technical deference.** Internal auditors with limited technical understanding defer to technical teams' explanations of how systems work rather than independently assessing governance evidence. The result is assurance based on management representation rather than independent verification.
