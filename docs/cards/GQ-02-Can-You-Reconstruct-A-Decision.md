# GQ-02 — Can you reconstruct a specific decision after the fact?

> **Not what the system usually does. What it did, in this case, on that date, to that person.**

---

## What

This question is asking whether the organisation can produce a complete, accurate account of a specific automated decision — after it has been made, potentially long after — from records that were created at the time, not from memory, inference, or reconstruction.

Reconstruction means: here is what data the system received, here is what it produced, here is the version of the system that was running, here is what happened next. It means the account is verifiable — it comes from records that existed before the question was asked, not from a narrative assembled in response to it.

A genuine yes requires that the records exist, that they are complete, that they are accurate, and that they can be retrieved and assembled into a coherent account by someone who was not present when the decision was made. It requires that the record survived — that normal operational processes such as log rotation, data deletion, and system updates did not destroy it before it was needed.

---

## Why

Automated systems make decisions about people. Those people have a legitimate interest in knowing what was decided about them and why. Organisations have an obligation to be able to explain it. Regulators have the authority to require it. Courts can compel it.

None of those obligations can be met without reconstruction capability. A system that made a decision and left no trace that can be reliably retrieved has produced an outcome without accountability. The decision happened. The basis for it cannot be examined, cannot be challenged, and cannot be learned from.

The reconstruction question also tests the organisation's governance in a deeper way. A system whose decisions can be reconstructed was governed with accountability in mind — records were created because someone understood they would be needed. A system whose decisions cannot be reconstructed was governed, if at all, for the moment — with no provision for the future in which its decisions would be questioned.

When something goes wrong, reconstruction is the difference between an investigation that can establish what happened and one that cannot. Without it, findings are speculative, remediation addresses symptoms, and accountability cannot be traced to the decisions that produced the harm.

---

## When

**When an individual requests an explanation.** An affected person asks why the system decided what it did about them. The answer must come from records, not from a general description of how the system works. This is the moment reconstruction is tested against its most basic purpose.

**When a complaint is investigated.** A complaint arrives about a decision made three months ago. The system has been updated since. The team has changed. The investigation requires knowing what the system did then — not what it does now, not what it would have done, but what it actually did on that specific occasion.

**When a regulator examines a specific case.** A regulatory inquiry focuses on a decision that affected a named individual. The regulator asks to see the decision record — the inputs, the output, the system state, the data that was used. The organisation either has that record or it does not. There is no middle ground.

**When litigation requires disclosure.** Legal proceedings involving an automated decision require disclosure of the decision record. The organisation that cannot produce it cannot defend the decision. The one that can produce it but finds the record incomplete or inconsistent has a different but equally serious problem.

**When the organisation wants to learn.** After an incident, after a pattern of complaints, after an audit finding — the organisation wants to understand what its system was doing. That understanding requires the ability to examine specific decisions. Without reconstruction capability, learning from failure is impossible. The same decisions recur. The same failures follow.
