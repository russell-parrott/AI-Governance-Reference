# LL-00 — The Legal Landscape

> **AI systems do not operate in a legal vacuum. They operate in a legal environment that already existed — one built around accountability, evidence, oversight and the protection of people from harm. This series explains what that environment looks like.**

---

## What This Series Is

The Legal Landscape series explains the legal frameworks that organisations encounter when they develop, deploy or operate automated systems that make decisions affecting people.

Each card in the series covers one legal domain. It explains what the framework governs, why automated systems trigger it, and how it connects to the governance concepts in the Atlas.

The series does not cover every law in every jurisdiction. It explains the major frameworks and their logic — clearly enough that a non-specialist can understand why a legal domain is relevant, what kind of obligations it creates, and which governance concepts matter when that domain applies.

---

## What This Series Is Not

This series does not constitute legal advice. It does not tell any organisation whether a specific law applies to it, whether it is compliant, or what it must do to meet any legal obligation. Those are questions for qualified legal advisers with knowledge of the specific organisation, its systems, and the jurisdictions in which it operates.

The distinction matters because it reflects the purpose of the Atlas as a whole. The Atlas explains concepts. It does not advise, prescribe or certify.

---

## Why It Belongs Here

The governance concepts in the Atlas — accountability, oversight, records, evidence, human control — are not abstract principles. They are the practical requirements that law and regulators demand when automated systems go wrong.

An organisation that understands the governance concepts without understanding the legal landscape in which they operate has only half the picture. The legal landscape explains why governance matters beyond good practice — it exists because the failure to govern automated systems produces consequences that the law was designed to address.

Reading the governance cards alongside the legal landscape cards reveals the connection: the accountability that company law demands is the same accountability the Atlas defines. The records that data protection law requires are the same records that evidence-based governance requires. The human oversight that financial regulation mandates is the same oversight that the Atlas identifies as a governance fundamental.
