# GQ-01 — Can a real person override an AI decision?

> **Not in theory. Not in policy. Right now, today — can someone actually do it?**

---

## What

This question is asking whether human control over automated decisions is real and operational — not whether it is described in a governance document, assigned to a role, or promised in a policy.

A genuine yes requires three things simultaneously: a person with the authority to override, the practical means to do so, and the information needed to know when an override is warranted. Remove any one of those three and the override capability is notional. The authority exists but cannot be exercised. The mechanism exists but nobody has the standing to use it. The standing exists but the person cannot see what the system is doing.

The question is not asking whether overrides have occurred. It is asking whether they can occur — at any moment, without advance preparation, in the ordinary course of operation.

---

## Why

Automated systems make decisions continuously. Some of those decisions will be wrong. Some will be wrong in ways the system cannot detect, in circumstances its training did not represent, about individuals whose situations fall outside the patterns the system was built to recognise.

When that happens, the organisation needs a human who can intervene. Not after an investigation. Not after a complaint has escalated. At the moment the decision is made, or as soon after as the problem becomes visible.

If no such person exists — if override requires a chain of approvals that takes days, or technical access that only the vendor holds, or a governance process that has never been tested — the system is effectively autonomous. It makes decisions the organisation cannot correct in time to prevent harm. The governance structure describes oversight. The operational reality is none.

The deeper reason this question matters is what it reveals about the organisation's relationship with its own systems. A system that cannot be overridden is a system the organisation does not control. And an organisation that does not control its automated systems is not governing them — regardless of what its documentation says.

---

## When

**When an individual challenges a decision.** The person whose loan was refused, whose application was rejected, whose benefit was denied, asks for human review. The organisation's answer to this question determines whether that review is substantive or performative.

**When monitoring identifies an anomaly.** Operational monitoring flags that a system's outputs have shifted — distribution changes, error rates rising, edge cases accumulating. Someone needs to decide whether to intervene. That decision requires both the authority to act and the means to do so.

**When an incident is in progress.** A system is producing harmful outputs. Every moment it continues, the harm accumulates. The question is not whether to override. It is whether the organisation can override fast enough to matter.

**When a regulator asks.** Regulatory examinations of automated systems increasingly include a direct question about human control. The organisation that cannot demonstrate operational override capability — not policy commitment, but demonstrated capability — has a governance finding on its hands.

**When the system does something unexpected.** Not a flagged error. Not a triggered alert. Something nobody anticipated — a response to an input combination the system had never encountered, an output in a context the governance framework did not foresee. The question is whether a human can take the wheel before the unexpected becomes the consequential.
