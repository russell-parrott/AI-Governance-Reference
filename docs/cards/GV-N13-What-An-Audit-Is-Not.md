# GV-N13 — What An Audit Is Not

> **The audit has been completed. Now check: what has actually been established — and what remains exactly as it was before the auditors left?**

---

## What It Is Not

An audit is not a fix. It is not a guarantee, an ongoing monitoring mechanism, a governance system, or a finding of compliance. It is not proof that a system is safe, that governance is adequate, or that the problems it identified have been resolved. It does not make anything better by itself. It does not prevent future failures. It does not substitute for the governance it examines.

An audit, properly understood, is a structured examination — conducted at a point in time, against defined criteria, by a person or body with the standing to conduct it — that produces findings about the state of what was examined at the time of examination. It identifies gaps. It does not fill them. It finds problems. It does not solve them. It produces a report. The report does not govern anything.

What organisations most often confuse with an audit is one or more of the following:

**A fix.** An audit that finds a governance gap has not closed that gap. An audit that identifies a monitoring deficiency has not established monitoring. An audit that flags an accountability problem has not resolved it. Audit findings are a diagnosis. The treatment is the response the organisation makes to them — the investment, the remediation, the accountability for implementation. The audit cannot make that response. Only the organisation can.

**Ongoing assurance.** An audit was conducted six months ago. The organisation's governance has been assessed. Between then and now, the system has been updated, the team responsible for oversight has changed, and a new regulatory requirement has come into force. The audit report accurately reflects the state of governance at the point of examination. It does not reflect the state of governance today. Audits provide assurance at a moment. Ongoing assurance requires ongoing activity.

**A guarantee against liability.** An audit finding that governance was adequate at the time of assessment does not establish that governance was adequate at the time of a subsequent failure. An organisation that was audited favourably and then experienced a governance failure cannot use the audit to establish that its governance was adequate when the failure occurred — unless the audit was conducted after the failure, which defeats the purpose of using it as a defence.

**An independent assessment of regulatory compliance.** Audits are conducted against defined criteria. Those criteria may or may not align precisely with legal requirements. An audit that finds conformity with its defined criteria has not found regulatory compliance. It has found conformity with what it assessed. Where those two things differ, the favourable audit finding does not translate into a favourable regulatory finding.

**A substitute for internal governance.** Some organisations use periodic external audit as their primary governance mechanism — relying on external examination to identify problems rather than building internal capability to prevent and detect them. Audit as the primary governance mechanism is governance calibrated to catch failures after they occur. It is not governance designed to prevent them.

---

## Why Confusion Matters

When organisations mistake audit for assurance, they invest in being assessed rather than in governing. The audit cycle becomes the governance cycle — activity intensifies before an audit and subsides after it. The governance the audit assessed does not persist between assessments at the level the audit found. The next audit finds the same things as the last one, because the underlying governance capability has not been built.

The deeper problem is that audit findings not acted upon are not neutral. An organisation that received an audit finding, noted it, and did not remediate it has documented knowledge of a governance gap. If a failure subsequently occurs in exactly the area the audit identified, the audit finding is not a defence. It is evidence that the organisation knew of the gap and did not close it.

---

## Where Confusion Appears

**Audit completion treated as governance achievement.** An organisation commissions an AI governance audit. The audit is completed. The organisation communicates internally and externally that its governance has been assessed. The assessment is presented as assurance. The findings — which include identified gaps — are noted. The remediation is not tracked. The communication of assurance was not wrong in form. It was wrong in substance.

**Repeated audits finding the same issues.** An organisation conducts regular governance audits. Each audit identifies similar governance gaps — monitoring is inconsistent, documentation is incomplete, accountability is unclear. Each audit report is received, discussed, and filed. The gaps persist. The audit has become a recurring description of the organisation's governance failure rather than a mechanism for addressing it.

**Audit scope presented as comprehensive coverage.** An audit conducted against specific criteria covers what it covers. Organisations sometimes present a favourable audit finding as evidence of broader governance adequacy than the audit actually assessed. The scope of the finding is the scope of the assessment. What was not examined was not found adequate.

**Regulatory engagement based on audit reports rather than governance evidence.** An organisation facing regulatory scrutiny produces audit reports as its primary evidence of governance adequacy. The reports are well-produced and their findings are largely favourable. The regulator assesses current governance against current legal requirements. The audit reports describe past governance against defined audit criteria. The organisation answered a different question from the one it was asked.

---

## How To Tell The Difference

Five questions that distinguish genuine governance from audit:

- When an audit produces findings, is there a defined process for remediating them — with named owners, defined timeframes, and tracking to completion — or are findings noted and the audit considered concluded?
- Does the organisation's governance function between audits — is monitoring occurring, oversight active, accountability genuine — or does governance activity intensify before audits and subside after them?
- Is the scope of each audit defined to cover the governance questions that matter most — or is it defined by what is convenient to assess?
- When the same findings appear in successive audits, does the organisation treat that as a governance failure requiring escalation — or as a recurring observation that has become part of the audit furniture?
- Does the organisation use audit as one assurance mechanism within a broader governance system — or as the primary basis on which it believes its governance is adequate?

| Common Substitutes | What An Audit Actually Is |
|---|---|
| A fix for identified governance gaps | A finding — the remediation must follow separately |
| Ongoing governance assurance | A snapshot of governance at the point of examination |
| A guarantee against liability | Evidence of past state — not evidence of state at the time of a subsequent failure |
| A finding of regulatory compliance | Conformity with audit criteria — which may not align precisely with legal requirements |
| A substitute for internal governance | An examination of governance — not a replacement for it |

## The Law

Regulators treat audit findings as relevant evidence — not as conclusive findings of compliance. A favourable audit report is useful context in a regulatory examination. It does not establish current compliance, does not bind the regulator's assessment, and does not preclude enforcement action where current governance is found to be inadequate.

Unfavourable audit findings that were not remediated carry specific legal risk. Where an organisation received an audit finding identifying a governance gap, did not remediate it, and subsequently experienced a failure in the identified area, the audit finding is evidence that the organisation had knowledge of the risk. Knowledge of an unmitigated risk is a significantly more serious position than encountering an unexpected one.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for ongoing post-market surveillance and monitoring that explicitly move beyond audit as the primary assurance mechanism — requiring that organisations demonstrate continuous governance, not periodic examination of it.

The practical legal risk is direct: an audit that found governance adequate cannot speak to what happened after it concluded. An audit that found governance gaps that were not remediated speaks very clearly to what the organisation knew and chose not to address. In both cases, the audit is not protection. It is a record — of what was found, when, and what was done about it. That record speaks for itself.
