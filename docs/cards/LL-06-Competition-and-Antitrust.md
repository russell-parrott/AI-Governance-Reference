# LL-06 — Competition and Antitrust

> **Competition law was designed for human decisions about pricing and markets. Algorithmic systems that make the same decisions — faster, at greater scale, and without explicit coordination — raise the same legal questions under the same legal frameworks.**

---

## What It Covers

Competition and antitrust law prohibits practices that restrict competition, abuse dominant market positions, and distort markets to the disadvantage of consumers and competitors. It applies to agreements between organisations that fix prices, allocate markets or exclude competitors, and to unilateral conduct by dominant organisations that reinforces or exploits that dominance.

Major frameworks include EU Competition Law under Articles 101 and 102 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, the UK Competition Act 1998 enforced by the Competition and Markets Authority, the US Sherman Antitrust Act and Clayton Act enforced by the Department of Justice and Federal Trade Commission, and the Australian Competition and Consumer Act. Most jurisdictions with significant markets have equivalent frameworks.

Competition authorities in multiple jurisdictions have identified algorithmic pricing, algorithmic coordination and AI-enabled market power as active areas of concern and investigation.

---

## Why AI Systems Trigger It

Automated systems trigger competition law when they influence pricing, market behaviour or competitive strategy — a scope that encompasses a very large number of AI deployments in commercial settings.

**Algorithmic price fixing.** Competition law prohibits competitors from agreeing to fix prices. It does not require that the agreement be explicit. Where multiple competitors independently deploy pricing algorithms that monitor each other's prices and adjust in response, the result can be coordinated pricing behaviour — aligned prices, parallel price movements, suppressed competition — that competition law treats as equivalent to cartel conduct. The algorithms never communicated. The organisations never met. The anti-competitive effect is the same.

**Hub and spoke coordination.** Where multiple competitors use the same algorithm, the same pricing software, or the same AI platform — particularly one that incorporates market-wide data — the provider of that platform may function as a hub through which competitor pricing is effectively coordinated. This is a recognised competition law concern and the subject of active regulatory investigation in the US and EU.

**Personalised pricing and price discrimination.** AI systems that charge different prices to different customers based on inferred price sensitivity, purchasing history or demographic characteristics may trigger competition law concerns — particularly where they are used by dominant organisations, or where the price discrimination systematically disadvantages specific groups.

**Market foreclosure through AI.** Dominant organisations that use AI systems to identify and respond to competitive threats more quickly than human decision-makers — predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, tying — are engaging in conduct that existing competition law frameworks address, even if the speed and scale are new.

**Merger control and AI capabilities.** Acquisitions of AI companies — particularly those with unique datasets, foundation models or market-position advantages — are subject to merger review. Competition authorities are increasingly attentive to the argument that AI capabilities create barriers to entry that require pre-emptive merger control.

## Governance Connection

Competition law connects Atlas governance concepts to market conduct obligations that apply even when no individual decision-maker intended anti-competitive behaviour.

**Intended use** (→ SP-09) is directly relevant. An organisation deploying a pricing algorithm intended to respond dynamically to competitor pricing should understand that its intended use engages competition law. The intent of the design does not determine the legal character of the conduct — the effect on competition does.

**System scope** (→ SP-08) matters because the range of markets, products and geographies in which an automated system operates determines the competition law exposure. A pricing system operating across multiple markets simultaneously has competition law implications in each.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, AR-06) is acute in competition law because liability attaches to the organisation, not the algorithm. An organisation cannot defend a competition law investigation by demonstrating that the anti-competitive behaviour was produced by an automated system it did not understand. Boards and senior management who approved the deployment of pricing systems are accountable for their competitive effects.

**Records** (→ RE-02, RE-07) of pricing decisions, algorithm configurations, and the data used in algorithmic pricing are central to competition investigations. Competition authorities have well-developed powers to demand documents, conduct dawn raids, and compel the production of technical records. Organisations that cannot produce documentation of what their pricing algorithms were doing, and why, are at a significant evidential disadvantage.

**Monitoring** (→ CS-01, CS-05) of algorithmic pricing outputs is both a governance requirement and a competition law practical necessity. An organisation that monitors its algorithmic pricing regularly is in a position to identify and address conduct that creates legal exposure before regulators identify it externally.

---

## Jurisdictions

Competition law applies in all major economies. EU competition law has extraterritorial reach for conduct affecting EU markets. The UK Competition and Markets Authority has issued guidance specifically addressing algorithmic pricing. The US Federal Trade Commission and Department of Justice have both identified algorithmic competition concerns as enforcement priorities. Equivalent authorities in Australia, Canada, Japan, South Korea and other jurisdictions are actively examining AI's competition law implications.
