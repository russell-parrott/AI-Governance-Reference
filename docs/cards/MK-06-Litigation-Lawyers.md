# MK-06 — Litigation Lawyers

> **Litigation lawyers act for parties in disputes involving automated systems — pursuing or defending claims that a system caused harm, breached obligations or produced unlawful outcomes. Their challenge is building or dismantling cases about systems that were designed without litigation in mind.**

---

## What This Market Is

Litigation lawyers represent clients in disputes before courts, tribunals and arbitration bodies. In the AI governance context they appear for claimants seeking remedy for harm caused by automated decisions, for defendants seeking to establish that their governance was adequate, and for organisations managing regulatory proceedings where legal and civil liability intersect.

They appear in commercial litigation, employment tribunals, judicial review proceedings, class actions, regulatory enforcement appeals and civil liability claims. Their position in the governance structure is adversarial — they deploy governance evidence, and governance failures, as arguments in disputes whose outcome is decided by courts.

Litigation lawyers are the professional group most directly confronted with the question of whether AI governance was adequate — because adequacy is frequently the central question in the disputes they conduct.

---

## Why AI Governance Matters to Them

Litigation lawyers face a distinctive challenge with AI cases: the evidence they need to build or defend a case may simply not exist. Automated systems that lack traceability, explainability and adequate records leave both claimants and defendants in evidential difficulty.

For claimants' lawyers, the problem is establishing what the system did, why, and how it caused the harm claimed. Without decision logs, model documentation and provenance records, causation cannot be established to the required standard.

For defendants' lawyers, the problem is demonstrating that the system was adequately governed — that the deployment was properly authorised, that oversight was genuine, that the system performed as intended and that any failure was not the result of inadequate governance.

The legal landscape is also shifting in claimants' favour in several jurisdictions — through disclosure obligations, presumptions of causation and burden-shifting provisions that reduce the evidential burden on those harmed by automated systems.

---

## What Questions They Ask

Litigation lawyers approach AI governance through a causation, liability and evidence lens:

- What did the system actually do in relation to this specific individual, and can that be proved?
- Who was responsible for the system and its governance, and can that accountability be demonstrated or contested?
- What legal obligations applied, and is there evidence they were or were not met?
- Does the governance documentation reflect what the system actually did, or was it produced after the fact?
- Is the evidence sufficient to establish causation to the required standard?
- What are the available remedies, and what evidence supports or undermines them?

Litigation lawyers are asking the questions that judicial proceedings require — questions about evidence, causation, obligation and remedy.

---

## What Evidence They Expect

Litigation lawyers require evidence that will withstand adversarial scrutiny before a tribunal:

- Decision records showing exactly what the system produced for the specific individual and when
- Audit trails demonstrating the sequence of events surrounding the decision
- System documentation established at or before deployment — not reconstructed after the claim
- Governance records showing authorisation, oversight and accountability structures
- Performance and monitoring records for the period in question
- Internal communications about the system's known limitations or identified problems
- Expert evidence translating technical system characteristics into terms the court can apply

The distinction between contemporaneous governance records and post-hoc documentation is critical in litigation. Records created to satisfy a regulatory requirement at the time carry very different evidential weight from records created in response to a claim.

---

## Where They Appear in Investigations

Litigation lawyers become involved in AI governance disputes at the point where informal resolution has failed and adversarial proceedings begin — or where regulatory enforcement has escalated to contested proceedings.

In regulatory enforcement appeals, litigation lawyers challenge or defend the regulator's findings on the adequacy of governance, the existence of breaches and the proportionality of sanctions. In civil liability claims, they establish or contest whether the system caused the harm claimed and whether the defendant's governance was adequate. In judicial review proceedings, they challenge the lawfulness of automated decisions made by public bodies.

Litigation lawyers also shape the legal landscape through the cases they run. Decided cases on AI liability, discrimination and governance adequacy create the precedents that governance frameworks must be designed to satisfy.

## Governance Concepts Most Relevant to Them

The Atlas concepts most directly relevant to litigation lawyers are drawn from the Law & Regulation, Records & Evidence and Authority & Responsibility series:

- **Liability** (→ LR-04) — the legal frameworks that determine who is responsible for AI-caused harm
- **Contestability** (→ LR-14) and **Redress** (→ LR-15) — the rights and remedies that litigation enforces
- **Automated Decision Rights** (→ LR-01) — the legal rights whose breach creates claims
- **Audit Trails** (→ RE-01) and **Decision Records** (→ RE-02) — the primary evidential record
- **Traceability** (→ RE-14) — the governance property that determines whether decisions can be reconstructed for litigation
- **System Documentation** (→ RE-13) — the contemporaneous record whose absence weakens defendants
- **Accountability** (→ AR-01) — the governance concept that maps to legal liability
- **Responsibility Gaps** (→ AR-12) — the governance failure that leaves defendants unable to identify who was responsible

The AI-specific liability frameworks in development — including the EU AI Liability Directive — will define the legal terrain for AI litigation over the next decade.

## Typical Failure Points

**No contemporaneous records.** The governance documentation that would support a defendant's case does not exist, was not maintained, or was produced after the claim arose. Courts treat the absence of records that should have been maintained as significant — and sometimes adverse — evidence.

**Records that describe intent rather than practice.** Governance documentation describes the framework the organisation intended to operate rather than the controls it actually ran. When cross-examined, the distinction between the documented framework and the operational reality becomes the central issue in the case.

**Diffuse accountability that cannot be located.** Defendants cannot identify who was responsible for specific decisions because governance arrangements assigned responsibility to committees and functions rather than individuals. This is a defence liability rather than a neutral fact.

**Traceability gaps at the critical point.** The specific decision that is the subject of the claim cannot be reconstructed from available records. The general description of how the system works does not satisfy the court's requirement for evidence about what the system did in this specific case.

**Claimants cannot establish causation.** Claimants face the inverse problem — they cannot demonstrate that the automated system caused the harm because the records that would show what the system did and why are not available. Emerging legislative changes in several jurisdictions are designed to address this by shifting evidential burdens.
