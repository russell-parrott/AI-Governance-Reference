# JX-14 — European Union

> **The EU has done what no other jurisdiction has done: passed a comprehensive, binding AI-specific law with extraterritorial reach. The AI Act applies to systems deployed in the EU regardless of where they were built or who built them.**

---

## The Framework

The European Union is the dominant regulatory force in global AI governance. Through a combination of the EU AI Act, the General Data Protection Regulation, the Digital Services Act, the Digital Markets Act, the Product Liability Directive and sector-specific regulation, the EU has constructed the most comprehensive binding legal framework for automated systems in the world.

The EU AI Act — formally Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — entered into force in August 2024 and is phasing in across 2025 and 2026. It establishes a risk-based classification system for AI systems, imposes binding obligations on developers and deployers of high-risk systems, and prohibits certain AI applications outright. It is the first law anywhere that systematically addresses AI governance as a legal obligation rather than a voluntary commitment.

The GDPR — in force since 2018 — imposes obligations on any organisation processing personal data about EU residents, regardless of where that organisation is based. It gives individuals rights in relation to automated decisions, requires data protection impact assessments for high-risk processing, and mandates demonstrable accountability. In AI governance terms, it is the most enforced of the EU's frameworks, with substantial fines issued across multiple jurisdictions.

These two instruments sit alongside product safety law, consumer protection law, competition law, sector regulation and employment law to create an interlocking framework that reaches almost every aspect of automated decision-making.

---

## Key Instruments

**EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)** — risk-based classification of AI systems into prohibited, high-risk, limited risk and minimal risk categories. High-risk systems — including those used in credit, employment, education, essential services, biometrics, law enforcement and critical infrastructure — face mandatory conformity assessments, technical documentation requirements, logging obligations, human oversight requirements, transparency obligations and post-market monitoring. General-purpose AI models above defined capability thresholds face their own obligations.

**General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, Regulation 2016/679)** — applies to any processing of personal data about EU residents. Article 22 gives individuals the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects, and the right to obtain human review, express their point of view, and obtain an explanation. Data Protection Impact Assessments are mandatory for high-risk automated processing. Named Data Protection Officers are required in many contexts.

**Digital Services Act (DSA, Regulation 2022/2065)** — applies to online platforms and search engines. Very large platforms must conduct algorithmic risk assessments, provide transparency about recommender systems, and allow users to opt out of profiling-based recommendation.

**Digital Markets Act (DMA, Regulation 2022/1925)** — applies to designated gatekeepers in digital markets. Restricts use of data and algorithmic practices that entrench market dominance.

**Product Liability Directive (revised 2024)** — explicitly extends product liability to AI systems and software. Establishes rebuttable presumptions of causation to address the evidentiary difficulty of proving AI-related harm.

**AI Liability Directive (proposed)** — would harmonise national fault-based liability rules for AI-related harm and introduce disclosure obligations to help claimants access evidence. At advanced legislative stage.

## What Triggers Obligations

**The AI Act** triggers based on risk classification of the system. Organisations must determine whether their system falls within the high-risk categories defined in Annex III — which includes AI systems used in biometric identification, critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential private and public services, law enforcement, migration, administration of justice and democratic processes. High-risk classification triggers the full conformity and documentation regime. Prohibited applications — including real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces and social scoring — cannot be deployed at all.

**GDPR** triggers whenever personal data about EU residents is processed — which includes the use of that data to train a model, the processing of individuals' data as system inputs, and the automated generation of decisions that affect individuals. The high-risk threshold for mandatory DPIAs is low and will capture most consequential automated decision systems.

**The DSA** triggers for platforms with more than 45 million monthly active users in the EU, and with additional obligations for very large platforms and search engines.

**The DMA** triggers for designated gatekeepers — currently Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, ByteDance, Meta and Microsoft.

**Product liability** triggers when an AI system causes harm. The revised directive applies to software and AI systems placed on the EU market, including updates to existing products.

## Governance Connection

The EU framework maps directly onto the governance architecture of the Atlas — and in several areas the AI Act essentially codifies what the Atlas describes as sound governance.

**Risk classification** (→ LR-13, LL-01) is the AI Act's organising principle. An organisation cannot determine its obligations without first classifying its systems. The Atlas governance questions apply with full force to high-risk systems and remain relevant for lower-risk ones.

**Technical documentation** (→ RE-13, SP-22) is a mandatory obligation for high-risk systems. The AI Act specifies what must be documented — including system architecture, training data, performance metrics, known limitations and intended use. Documentation that satisfies the AI Act standard will satisfy most other governance frameworks.

**Logging and audit trails** (→ RE-01, RE-03, CS-01) are required for high-risk systems. The AI Act requires automatic logging of events during system operation, retained for a defined period, accessible to competent authorities.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is a specific AI Act obligation for high-risk systems. Deployers must ensure that natural persons can effectively oversee the system, interpret its outputs, and intervene or override. Nominal oversight does not satisfy the standard.

**Transparency and explainability** (→ SP-15, SP-16, LL-02) are required under both the AI Act and GDPR. Individuals must be informed when interacting with AI systems. Automated decisions must be explicable in terms meaningful to the affected individual.

**Conformity assessment** (→ GV-01, CS-08) is required before high-risk systems are placed on the market. Third-party assessment is mandatory for the highest-risk categories. Post-market monitoring must continue after deployment.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) runs through the entire framework. The AI Act distinguishes between providers — those who develop and place systems on the market — and deployers — those who use systems in their operations. Both carry obligations. Neither can fully discharge accountability to the other.

**Redress** (→ LR-15, LR-14) is addressed in the AI Act, GDPR and the proposed AI Liability Directive. Affected individuals have rights to explanation, human review and remedy. The revised Product Liability Directive and AI Liability Directive together address the evidential barriers that have historically made AI-related harm claims difficult to pursue.

---

## Extraterritorial Reach

Both the AI Act and GDPR apply extraterritorially. The AI Act applies to any provider placing an AI system on the EU market, any deployer using an AI system within the EU, and any provider or deployer located outside the EU whose system's outputs are used in the EU. An organisation based in the United States, United Kingdom, Singapore or anywhere else that deploys an AI system affecting EU residents is within scope.

This extraterritorial reach makes the EU framework the de facto global baseline for AI governance. Organisations operating internationally that comply with the EU framework will satisfy most — though not all — of the requirements of other jurisdictions covered in this series.
