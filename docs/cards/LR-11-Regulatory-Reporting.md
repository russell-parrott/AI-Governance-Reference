# LR-11 — Regulatory Reporting

> **Sometimes you must proactively tell the regulator things. This card explains what, when, and why waiting to be asked is not an option.**

---

## What Is It?

Regulatory reporting is the obligation to proactively provide information about automated systems to a regulatory body — not in response to an investigation or inquiry, but as a standing requirement that the organisation must meet on its own initiative, within defined timeframes, in defined forms.

It is distinct from responding to regulatory oversight, which is reactive. Regulatory reporting is active. The organisation does not wait to be asked. It discloses — incidents, significant changes, performance metrics, governance assessments, or other specified information — because the law requires it to do so, whether or not the regulator has shown any interest in the specific matter being reported.

Regulatory reporting obligations are the legal expression of transparency. They exist because regulators have concluded that certain information about automated systems is too important to be disclosed only when something has already gone wrong.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Regulatory reporting changes the dynamic between an organisation and its regulator in a fundamental way. An organisation with no proactive reporting obligations controls the information its regulator receives — disclosing what it chooses, when it chooses, framed as it chooses. An organisation with regulatory reporting obligations cannot control the flow of information in the same way. It must report, on schedule, what the law requires — regardless of whether the information is favourable.

That loss of information control is the point. Regulatory reporting exists precisely to ensure that regulators receive the information they need to assess whether organisations are meeting their obligations — before an incident creates the circumstances in which that information would emerge anyway.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Reporting obligations that are not known until they are breached.** Regulatory reporting requirements arise from multiple frameworks — data protection law, sector regulation, AI-specific legislation. An organisation that has not mapped its reporting obligations may breach them through ignorance — failing to report something it was required to disclose within a defined timeframe, with consequences that are independent of whatever it failed to report.

**Late reporting that triggers separate consequences.** Many regulatory reporting obligations specify timeframes — hours for serious incidents in some frameworks, days or weeks for periodic reporting. Reporting that occurs outside those timeframes is not merely late. It is non-compliant — and the lateness itself becomes a finding in any subsequent examination, regardless of the content of what was eventually reported.

**Reports that are technically compliant but substantively inadequate.** A report that meets the formal requirements — submitted on time, in the correct form, to the correct recipient — but that omits material information, presents a misleading picture, or understates the significance of what is being reported is not compliant in substance. Regulators increasingly examine the quality of reports, not just their existence.

**Periodic reporting that reveals governance gaps.** Organisations required to submit regular governance or performance reports about their automated systems sometimes discover, in the process of preparing those reports, that the underlying governance activity has not been conducted as required. The reporting obligation surfaces the gap — but at the point of reporting, not when the gap could most easily have been addressed.

Responsibility for identifying, meeting, and maintaining regulatory reporting obligations sits with senior leadership. Reporting obligations cannot be met by operational teams acting alone — they require governance visibility and legal awareness that must be coordinated at the top of the organisation.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Regulatory reporting obligations for automated systems arise across multiple frameworks and sectors:

- **Data protection** — mandatory reporting of personal data breaches, including those caused by automated system failures, within defined timeframes to supervisory authorities
- **Financial services** — periodic reporting of model risk, algorithmic system performance, and material changes to automated decision systems to sector regulators
- **Healthcare** — reporting of patient safety incidents involving automated diagnostic or clinical systems to healthcare regulatory bodies
- **AI-specific legislation** — emerging obligations to report serious incidents involving high-risk automated systems, material changes to deployed systems, and periodic conformity assessments to designated authorities
- **Consumer protection** — reporting obligations in some jurisdictions when automated systems produce widespread consumer harm or systemic failures
- **Competition and market oversight** — reporting requirements in some frameworks when algorithmic systems used in pricing or market behaviour meet defined thresholds of significance

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes regulatory reporting seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Has the organisation mapped all its proactive regulatory reporting obligations across every framework that applies to its automated systems?
- Are reporting timeframes known, calendared, and owned by named individuals responsible for ensuring reports are submitted on time?
- Is the information required for regulatory reports being captured routinely as part of governance activity — or does preparing a report require a separate data collection effort?
- Is there a process for reviewing reports before submission to ensure they are accurate, complete, and honestly representative of the organisation's position?
- When a reportable event occurs — an incident, a material change — is there a defined process for ensuring the reporting obligation is identified and met within the required timeframe?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation is carrying regulatory reporting obligations it may not be meeting — and in some cases may not be aware of. Regulatory reporting obligations do not become active when an organisation discovers them. They apply from the moment the relevant legal framework applies to the organisation's activities.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Proactive reporting builds regulatory trust — organisations that report consistently and accurately are treated differently from those that disclose only under pressure | Mapping reporting obligations across multiple simultaneous frameworks requires legal expertise and ongoing maintenance as requirements evolve |
| Reporting obligations create internal discipline — the need to report drives governance activity that produces the information to report | Preparing accurate, complete regulatory reports requires governance infrastructure that many organisations have not built |
| Early incident reporting limits the period of non-disclosure and can reduce the severity of regulatory response | Reporting obligations impose administrative burden that organisations under resource pressure may manage inadequately |
| Periodic reporting provides a structured occasion for honest assessment of governance performance | Reports submitted under time pressure may not accurately reflect the organisation's full position |
| Meeting reporting obligations demonstrates compliance culture to regulators in ways that passive compliance cannot | The consequences of late or incomplete reporting can be as significant as the consequences of the underlying matter being reported |

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent and expanding: proactive regulatory reporting obligations for automated systems are increasing in scope, frequency, and specificity — driven by data protection enforcement, sector regulation, and the emergence of AI-specific legislative frameworks.

Data protection frameworks in many jurisdictions impose mandatory breach notification obligations with defined timeframes — typically measured in hours or days for serious incidents. Those obligations are among the most actively enforced provisions in data protection law, with significant penalties for late or inadequate reporting.

AI-specific legislation is advancing mandatory incident reporting, periodic conformity reporting, and notification of material system changes as standard obligations for high-risk automated systems — extending proactive disclosure requirements significantly beyond what data protection law alone requires.

The practical legal risk is direct: regulatory reporting obligations are not optional disclosures. They are legal requirements with defined consequences for non-compliance. An organisation that reports late, reports incompletely, or fails to report altogether is not exercising discretion about how to manage its regulatory relationships. It is breaching a legal obligation — and the breach is often easier for a regulator to identify and act on than the underlying conduct that triggered the reporting obligation in the first place.
