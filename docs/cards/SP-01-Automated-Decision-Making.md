# SP-01 — Automated Decision Making

> **Read this first. If you do not know what automated decision-making is, nothing else in this series will make sense.**

---

## What Is It?

Automated decision-making is what happens when a computer system produces an outcome — an approval, a refusal, a score, a recommendation — without a person reviewing the case first.

The system takes in information, applies rules or a statistical model, and delivers a result. That result may affect a real person, a transaction, or an operational process. No human checks it before it lands.

This is not the same as a computer helping a person decide. It is a computer deciding instead of a person.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

When a person makes a decision, you can ask them to explain it, challenge it, and hold them responsible if it is wrong. There is someone to answer for what happened.

When a machine makes the decision, that accountability does not automatically follow. The system may produce thousands of results a day. Many will never be reviewed. Some will be wrong. Some will be unfair. And because no individual made the call, the question of who is responsible becomes genuinely difficult to answer.

Organisations that use these systems inherit that problem. If they cannot explain how a decision was reached, cannot show who authorised the system to make it, and cannot demonstrate that errors are being caught, they are operating without the control they may believe they have.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

The consequences of ungoverned automated decision-making are not theoretical:

**Errors at scale.** A flawed system does not make one bad decision — it makes the same bad decision thousands of times before anyone notices. The damage accumulates silently.

**No clear accountability.** When something goes wrong, the absence of a named human decision-maker makes it genuinely difficult to establish who is responsible. Regulators and courts find that unacceptable, and so do the people affected.

**Regulatory exposure.** Across most jurisdictions, organisations are increasingly required to demonstrate that automated decisions are monitored, explainable, and subject to human oversight. An organisation that cannot show this is exposed regardless of whether its system was technically well built.

**Reputational damage.** When automated systems produce harmful outcomes at scale — wrong benefit denials, discriminatory credit decisions, unfair employment screening — the damage to public trust is significant and often lasting.

Accountability for what an automated system does sits with the organisation that deployed it, not the team that built it.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Automated decision-making is in widespread use across most sectors:

- **Financial services** — loan approvals, credit scoring, fraud detection, insurance pricing
- **Public services** — benefit eligibility, tax risk profiling, permit processing
- **Employment** — CV screening, shift scheduling, performance monitoring
- **Healthcare** — triage prioritisation, diagnostic support, appointment routing
- **Retail and platforms** — content recommendation, pricing, account suspension
- **Policing and border control** — risk scoring, watchlist matching, entry decisions

In each case, a system is producing outcomes that affect people's access to money, services, employment, or freedom — often at a scale and speed no manual process could match.

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation using automated decision-making is responsible for what the system does in its name. That means someone must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- What decisions is this system making?
- On what basis is it making them?
- Who authorised it to make them?
- What happens when it gets one wrong?
- How would we know if the outputs had changed?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation has delegated authority to a system it does not fully control. That is not a technical problem. It is a governance problem.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Consistent — applies the same rules to every case | Consistency can mean consistently wrong if the model is flawed |
| Fast — processes large volumes without delay | Speed makes errors difficult to catch before they cause harm |
| Scalable — handles volumes no human team could match | Scale amplifies the impact of any systematic bias |
| Produces a record of each output | The record shows what was decided, not always why |
| Removes some forms of human inconsistency | Introduces different forms of error that are harder to see |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: organisations must be able to show that automated decisions are monitored, explainable, and subject to human accountability.

Data protection and privacy laws in many jurisdictions address automated decision-making directly, particularly where decisions affect individuals' rights or access to services. AI-specific legislation is advancing in multiple regions, with high-risk applications in employment, credit, healthcare, and public services attracting the most regulatory attention.

Sector regulators in financial services, healthcare, and public administration increasingly require organisations to demonstrate active oversight of automated systems, regardless of whether a specific AI law applies.

The practical legal risk is the same everywhere: an organisation that cannot explain what its system does, who authorised it, and how errors are caught is in a weak position when a regulator or court asks those questions.
