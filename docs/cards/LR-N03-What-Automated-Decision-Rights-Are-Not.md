# LR-N03 — What Automated Decision Rights Are Not

> **The individual has rights. Now check: does what the organisation offers actually meet them — or just resemble them?**

---

## What It Is Not

Automated decision rights are not a complaints procedure. They are not a customer service channel, a feedback form, a general enquiries email address, or a "contact us" page. They are not satisfied by an organisation being generally willing to discuss concerns, by a policy stating that the organisation takes fairness seriously, or by a process that allows individuals to raise issues and receive a response.

Automated decision rights, properly understood, are specific legal entitlements — the right to know a decision was automated, the right to a meaningful explanation of how it was reached, the right to request human review, and in defined circumstances the right to have the decision reconsidered. Each of these is a distinct obligation on the organisation, triggered by specific conditions, with defined content and defined consequences for failure to meet it.

What organisations most often have instead is one or more of the following:

**A generic complaints procedure presented as a rights response mechanism.** A process that allows individuals to complain about any aspect of the organisation's service, which will be reviewed and responded to in due course, does not constitute a rights response mechanism. Automated decision rights have specific content — explanation, human review, reconsideration — that a generic complaints process does not deliver. The fact that an individual can complain does not mean their legal rights have been met.

**Customer service contact presented as human review.** An individual asks for human review of an automated decision. They are connected to a customer service representative who reads the system's output back to them, confirms the decision, and closes the case. That is not human review. Human review requires a person with the knowledge, the authority, and the information needed to genuinely assess whether the automated decision was correct — and to change it if it was not.

**A privacy policy that mentions automated decision-making.** An organisation publishes a privacy policy that includes a paragraph noting that automated processing occurs and that individuals have rights under data protection law. That paragraph is not the exercise of those rights. It is a disclosure. The rights themselves require an operational response mechanism — the means to actually receive an explanation, request a review, and obtain reconsideration.

**A response that explains the system rather than the decision.** An individual asks why they were refused credit. The organisation responds with a description of the factors its credit model considers. That is not an explanation of the individual's decision. It is a description of the system. Automated decision rights require an account of what happened in this case — what information was used, how it affected the outcome, and why that outcome followed.

---

## Why Confusion Matters

When organisations mistake complaints procedures and customer service channels for automated decision rights mechanisms, individuals who attempt to exercise their legal rights receive processes that do not meet them. The individual who was refused a loan cannot find out why. The person whose benefit was denied cannot obtain human review. The job applicant who was screened out cannot get a meaningful explanation.

The harm is not only to individuals. It is structural. Organisations that believe their existing processes satisfy automated decision rights have no incentive to build the specific capabilities those rights require — individual-level explanation, genuine human review, operational reconsideration. The belief in adequacy prevents the investment in reality.

When a regulator examines whether an organisation is meeting automated decision rights, the question is not whether individuals can contact the organisation. It is whether, when they do, they receive what the law entitles them to. A complaints procedure that cannot deliver an individual-level explanation is not meeting the right to explanation. A customer service channel that cannot conduct genuine human review is not meeting the right to human review. The gap between what exists and what the right requires is the finding.

---

## Where Confusion Appears

**Rights requests routed to general customer service.** An individual invokes their right to an explanation of an automated decision. The request is treated as a complaint and routed to a customer service team without the technical access, the knowledge of the system, or the authority to provide what the right requires.

**Human review that is review in name only.** An organisation offers human review of automated decisions. The review consists of a member of staff looking at the system's output and the individual's data and confirming the decision. No independent assessment is conducted. No genuine exercise of judgment occurs. The review is a formality designed to satisfy the obligation without changing anything.

**Explanation letters that describe process rather than outcome.** An organisation provides a written explanation in response to a rights request. The explanation describes the general operation of the system, the categories of data it uses, and the organisation's commitment to fairness. It does not explain why this individual received this outcome. The letter satisfies the form of a response while missing the substance of the right.

**Timeframes that are not observed.** Automated decision rights in many jurisdictions must be met within defined timeframes. An organisation that responds — eventually — but outside those timeframes has not met the right even if the content of its response would otherwise have been adequate.

---

## How To Tell The Difference

Five questions that distinguish genuine rights mechanisms from their substitutes:

- When an individual invokes their right to an explanation, can the organisation produce an account of what happened in their specific case — what data was used, how it influenced the outcome, and why that outcome followed — rather than a description of how the system works in general?
- When an individual requests human review, is there a person available who has the knowledge, authority, and information needed to genuinely assess the decision and change it if it was wrong?
- Are rights requests handled within the timeframes that applicable law requires — not within general customer service response standards?
- Do the people who handle rights requests know that they are handling legal rights — not general complaints — and do they have what they need to meet them?
- Has the organisation tested its rights response mechanisms — by working through what would actually happen if an individual invoked each right — rather than assuming that existing processes are adequate?

---

| Common Substitutes | What Automated Decision Rights Actually Require |
|---|---|
| A general complaints procedure | A specific mechanism for receiving and responding to rights requests within defined timeframes |
| A customer service contact for "any concerns" | A person with the knowledge, authority, and information to conduct genuine human review |
| A privacy policy mentioning automated processing | An operational response capability that actually delivers explanation, review, and reconsideration |
| An explanation of how the system works | An account of what happened in this individual's specific case |
| A response that arrives eventually | A response within the timeframe the law requires |

## The Law

Automated decision rights are established law in multiple jurisdictions — not emerging requirements but existing legal entitlements that have been enforceable for years. Data protection frameworks in many jurisdictions give individuals explicit rights in relation to automated decisions that significantly affect them, with defined content and defined remedies when those rights are not met.

Regulatory enforcement of automated decision rights is active. Supervisory authorities have investigated and sanctioned organisations that failed to provide adequate explanations, that offered processes incapable of genuine human review, and that treated rights requests as general complaints without the specific response the law requires.

AI-specific legislation is extending and reinforcing individual rights — adding new categories of entitlement, strengthening remedies, and making it easier for individuals to identify and enforce their rights in relation to automated systems.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that has a complaints procedure but not a rights mechanism is not partially compliant. It is non-compliant. The right exists. The mechanism to meet it does not. Every individual who has attempted to exercise that right and received a complaints process instead has experienced a legal breach — whether or not they knew it, and whether or not they pursued it.
