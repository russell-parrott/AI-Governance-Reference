# GQ-03 — Do you keep proper records of decisions and complaints?

> **Not records that exist somewhere. Records that are complete, accurate, retrievable, and retained for long enough to matter.**

---

## What

This question is asking whether the organisation maintains a structured, reliable, and complete record of what its automated systems decided and what people said about those decisions — in a form that can be produced, examined, and relied upon when needed.

Proper records means four things. Complete — every decision that should be recorded is recorded, and every complaint that was received is documented. Accurate — records reflect what actually happened, not what should have happened or what the organisation would prefer to have happened. Retrievable — records can be located and produced on demand, within the timeframes that governance and law require, without depending on memory or searching personal files. Retained — records are kept for long enough to serve their governance, legal, and accountability purposes before being disposed of in a controlled way.

A record that exists but cannot be found is not a proper record. A record that is accurate but incomplete is not a proper record. A record that was retained but disposed of before it was needed is not a proper record. Proper is the whole thing — not any single dimension of it.

---

## Why

Records are how governance becomes visible over time. The decisions an automated system made, the complaints those decisions generated, the responses the organisation gave — these are the raw material of accountability. Without them, accountability is a claim. With them, it is a demonstrable fact.

Complaints are a particular category that organisations consistently undervalue. A complaint is governance intelligence — a signal from the population affected by the system that something may be wrong. An organisation that receives complaints, resolves them, and keeps no systematic record of them has not learned from its affected population. It has managed individual incidents without ever asking what they reveal collectively. Patterns that should have been visible — the same decision going wrong for the same type of person, the same explanation failing to satisfy the same kind of question — remain invisible because the individual records were never connected.

The absence of proper records is also, in many contexts, a legal breach independent of whatever the records might have shown. Record-keeping obligations do not ask whether the records would have been favourable. They require that records exist.

---

## When

**When a complaint escalates.** A complaint that began as a request for explanation becomes a formal complaint, then a regulatory referral, then a legal claim. At each step, the record of what happened — when the complaint was received, what was said, what was done, what was decided — is the foundation of the organisation's response. An organisation without that record is constructing its defence from memory.

**When a pattern needs to be identified.** Multiple complaints arrive about similar decisions. The organisation wants to know whether they share a common cause. That analysis requires a complete, structured record of complaints — what they alleged, what system output they concerned, what the outcome was. Without it, the pattern is invisible.

**When a regulator examines complaint handling.** A regulatory review of the organisation's complaint handling asks for the complaint record — volumes, categories, outcomes, timeframes. The organisation that has managed complaints individually without systematic recording cannot answer those questions. The one that can answer them demonstrates that complaint handling was a governance activity, not just a customer service one.

**When the organisation is sued.** Litigation involving an automated decision requires disclosure of the decision record and the complaint record. A claimant who can show that the organisation received earlier complaints about the same type of decision, and did not act on them, has established that the organisation had knowledge it chose not to use.

**When governance is reviewed.** An internal or external governance review asks what the organisation's automated systems have been doing and how affected individuals have responded. The answer comes from records. An organisation that cannot produce a systematic account of its decisions and complaints cannot conduct a meaningful governance review — it can only describe its governance in the abstract, not assess it against evidence.
