# GF-03 — Oversight Without Intervention

> **Human oversight exists in principle but the reviewer lacks the ability to change or stop system decisions.**

---

## What This Failure Is

A human reviewer is present in the process. They receive the system's output. They are recorded as having reviewed it. But they cannot change the decision, cannot stop the system producing further decisions of the same kind, and have no escalation route that would result in meaningful action within the timeframe in which decisions take effect. The oversight is recorded. It is not real.

This failure often coexists with genuine intent. The organisation created a review step. The reviewer performs the step. The governance documentation accurately describes the process. What neither the documentation nor the reviewer acknowledges is that the review has no power to alter outcomes.

---

## Where It Occurs

Oversight without intervention occurs in high-volume automated decision processes where individual decisions execute faster than any escalation mechanism can respond; in vendor-operated systems where the deploying organisation has no technical ability to intervene in system operation; in organisational structures where the reviewer lacks the authority or escalation route to have a decision reversed; and in systems where override is technically possible but operationally impractical given review volumes and time constraints.

---

## Why It Matters

Regulatory frameworks requiring meaningful human oversight — the EU AI Act, GDPR Article 22 and sector-specific regimes — require oversight that is capable of changing outcomes, not oversight that is present but powerless. An organisation that presents powerless oversight as a governance control may satisfy a formal checkbox while failing the substantive standard.

When harm occurs and the organisation points to its human review process as the governance control that should have caught it, investigators will ask whether the reviewer had the ability to intervene. If the answer is no, the governance control did not exist in any meaningful sense.

---

## How It Is Discovered

This failure is discovered when investigators examine what a reviewer could actually do, rather than what the governance documentation says the review process is. It is exposed by low or zero override rates in high-volume processes; by reviewer testimony that they had no ability to change decisions; and by governance documentation that describes a review step without specifying the authority that step carries.

---

## Evidence That Reveals It

Near-zero override rates in high-volume review processes; absence of any documented escalation by reviewers; reviewer roles defined without authority specification; absence of records showing that reviewer concerns produced changed decisions; and timing records showing decisions executed before review could complete.

---

## Governance Concepts Involved

→ AR-03 (Human Oversight) — the control that appears to exist but does not
→ CS-13 (Human Override) — the specific capability that is absent
→ CS-03 (Human Review) — the mechanism whose substance this failure removes
→ CS-04 (Intervention Authority) — the authority dimension that is missing
→ SP-28 (Automation Bias) — the psychological dimension that compounds the structural failure

---

## What Organisations Often Assume Instead

That the presence of a review step constitutes oversight regardless of whether the reviewer can act. That human involvement in a process satisfies human oversight requirements regardless of its substance. That low override rates indicate a well-functioning system rather than a non-functioning oversight process.
