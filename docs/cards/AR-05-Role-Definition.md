# AR-05 — Role Definition

> **Accountability is abstract. Roles are concrete. This card identifies the actual jobs that need doing.**

---

## What Is It?

Role definition is the process of specifying who does what in the governance of an automated system. It converts the abstract concept of accountability into named functions with specific responsibilities attached to them.

A role is not a job title. It is a defined set of tasks, authorities, and obligations that someone must own in relation to a specific system. In a well-governed organisation, every automated system has identifiable people responsible for authorising it, operating it, monitoring it, reviewing it, and stopping it if necessary.

Without role definition, accountability exists in principle but not in practice. Everyone is vaguely responsible and no one is specifically responsible — which means that in a crisis, nothing happens fast enough.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Automated systems require ongoing human attention across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Someone must ensure the system is doing what it was authorised to do. Someone must watch for signs that it is drifting or failing. Someone must be able to authorise changes. Someone must be able to escalate problems. Someone must be able to stop it.

If those functions are not explicitly assigned, they are assumed. Assumed responsibility is the gap through which most governance failures travel. When an incident occurs and an organisation discovers that everyone thought someone else was watching, the absence of role definition is usually the explanation.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Gaps in coverage.** Without defined roles, critical functions — monitoring, escalation, sign-off — are performed inconsistently or not at all. The system runs, but no one is formally watching it.

**Overlap and conflict.** Where roles are undefined, two people may believe they have authority over the same decision, or neither may act because each assumes the other is responsible. Both produce the same outcome: delay when speed matters.

**Accountability without authority.** When something goes wrong and an organisation searches for who was responsible, it frequently finds that the person nominally accountable had no actual authority to intervene. That is a role definition failure, not a personnel failure.

**Regulatory exposure.** Regulators examining governance failures consistently find the same thing: roles existed on paper but were not clearly defined, not adequately resourced, and not genuinely understood by the people in them.

Ownership of role definition sits with the organisation's leadership. The technical team can describe what a system does. Only leadership can decide who is responsible for governing it.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Role definition for automated systems is relevant across any organisation that deploys them:

- **System authorisation** — someone must have the formal authority to approve a system for deployment and sign off on its intended use
- **Operational oversight** — someone must be responsible for monitoring the system's outputs and performance on an ongoing basis
- **Incident response** — someone must be designated to act when the system produces an unexpected or harmful result
- **Change control** — someone must authorise modifications to the system before they are implemented
- **Vendor management** — where a third party supplies part of the system, someone must own that relationship and be accountable for what the vendor delivers
- **Escalation** — someone must be the designated recipient when a problem exceeds the authority of the person who found it

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes role definition seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- For this system, who is responsible for each of its critical governance functions?
- Does each person in a defined role know they hold it and understand what it requires of them?
- Are roles matched to authority — can the person responsible for a function actually act on it?
- What happens to role coverage when someone leaves, is absent, or moves to a different team?
- When was role definition last reviewed against the system's current scope and risk level?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation has accountability in name only. Named roles without clarity, authority, and active awareness are not governance. They are paperwork.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Converts abstract accountability into specific, checkable obligations | Defining roles takes time and requires decisions that organisations often defer |
| Ensures critical governance functions are covered and owned | Roles assigned without adequate authority or resource create false assurance |
| Creates clarity during incidents — people know who acts and who escalates | Role definitions written once and not maintained become inaccurate quickly |
| Supports audit and regulatory review — ownership is traceable | In practice, roles are often assigned to whoever is available, not whoever is appropriate |
| Reduces the risk of gaps and overlaps in oversight | Large or complex systems may require more roles than the organisation has capacity to fill meaningfully |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is moving toward explicit requirements for named, functioning governance roles in automated systems — not just documented policies.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions already require organisations to be able to demonstrate who oversees automated decision-making and how that oversight operates. A list of role titles without evidence of active function does not satisfy that requirement.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements further, with high-risk systems increasingly subject to obligations that specify governance structures, not just outcomes. Regulators are beginning to look beyond compliance documentation to ask whether governance roles are real — whether the people in them have the knowledge, authority, and resource to perform them.

The practical legal risk is consistent: an organisation that cannot demonstrate functioning, populated governance roles for its automated systems is exposed when a regulator investigates or an incident requires explanation.
