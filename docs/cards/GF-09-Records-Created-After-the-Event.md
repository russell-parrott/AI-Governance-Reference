# GF-09 — Records Created After the Event

> **Governance records appear only after an incident or investigation has begun.**

---

## What This Failure Is

The investigation begins. Documents are requested. Governance records that should have existed — approval papers, risk assessments, oversight records, impact assessments — are produced for the first time. They are dated before the incident. They describe governance that was not practised at the time they purport to record. They are created to satisfy the investigation, not to document governance that occurred.

This may be deliberate fabrication. More often it is rationalisation — the organisation had informal governance practices and is now formalising them retrospectively, believing that the substance was there even if the record was not. The effect is the same: the contemporaneous record does not exist, and what replaces it is unreliable.

---

## Where It Occurs

Post-event record creation occurs when governance documentation was never maintained operationally; when incident investigations reveal record gaps that teams attempt to fill; when regulatory inquiries prompt the creation of governance records that regulatory requirements always required; and when organisations mistake documenting what they did do for documenting what they should have done.

---

## Why It Matters

Contemporaneous records carry evidential weight that retrospective records do not. Investigators are trained to identify inconsistencies in document metadata, creation dates, version histories and formatting that indicate retrospective creation. When post-event records are identified as such, they do not just fail to help the organisation — they become evidence of the original governance failure and of an attempt to conceal it.

---

## How It Is Discovered

Post-event records are identified through document metadata examination; inconsistencies in dating and version control; the absence of the records from earlier document productions; content that addresses concerns that were not known at the purported creation date; and the absence of any evidence that the records were used or acted on before the incident.

---

## Evidence That Reveals It

Document metadata inconsistent with creation dates; records that address governance concerns post-dating their stated creation; absence of contemporaneous distribution or use records; version histories that do not match stated document dates; and records that appear for the first time after an investigation request.

---

## Governance Concepts Involved

→ RE-01 (Audit Trails) — the contemporaneous record whose absence this failure attempts to remedy
→ RE-02 (Decision Records) — the records that should have existed at the point of decision
→ RE-13 (System Documentation) — the baseline documentation that should have been maintained
→ RE-14 (Traceability) — the capacity that retrospective records cannot genuinely restore

---

## What Organisations Often Assume Instead

That documenting governance retrospectively satisfies the same requirement as documenting it contemporaneously. That the substance of what was done is what matters rather than the record of when it was documented. That regulators and courts will treat a complete set of records as adequate regardless of when they were created.
