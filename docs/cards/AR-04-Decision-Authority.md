# AR-04 — Decision Authority

> **The problem has been escalated. Who actually gets to make the final call? This card defines that power.**

---

## What Is It?

Decision authority is the formally assigned power to make a binding determination about an automated system — to approve it, modify it, suspend it, or act on what it has produced.

It is not influence. It is not seniority. It is not the person who happens to be available when a decision is needed. Decision authority is a defined, documented allocation of power that specifies who can make which calls about which systems, under what circumstances, and within what limits.

Without defined decision authority, escalated problems arrive at higher levels of the organisation and stall. Everyone has a view. No one has the power — or the obligation — to decide.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Automated systems generate decisions continuously. When those decisions are challenged, when the system behaves unexpectedly, or when an escalated problem demands a response, someone must have the authority to act. Not to advise. Not to recommend. To decide.

The absence of defined decision authority does not prevent decisions from being made. It prevents them from being made clearly, quickly, and accountably. Decisions get made by default — by inaction, by the most senior person in the room, or by whoever is willing to act without a formal mandate. Those decisions are difficult to defend, difficult to attribute, and difficult to reverse when they turn out to be wrong.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Paralysis under pressure.** When a serious problem with an automated system requires an urgent decision and no one has defined authority to make it, the organisation hesitates. The system continues operating. The harm continues accumulating while people discuss who should act.

**Decisions made without mandate.** Where authority is undefined, decisions get made by whoever steps forward. That person may lack the information, the expertise, or the organisational standing to make the decision soundly. When it goes wrong, the absence of a formal mandate becomes an additional problem layered on top of the original one.

**Accountability gaps after the fact.** When a decision about an automated system is later scrutinised — by a regulator, in litigation, or in an internal review — the first question is who made it and on what authority. An organisation that cannot answer that question clearly has a governance failure that is independent of whether the decision itself was correct.

**Authority without accountability.** Decision authority that is assigned without corresponding accountability — for the quality of the decision and its consequences — is not governance. It is power without obligation, which produces poor decisions and no learning.

Responsibility for defining and maintaining decision authority structures sits with the organisation's leadership. It cannot be left to emerge organically from seniority or circumstance.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Decision authority for automated systems is required at every level and stage of operation:

- **Deployment** — who has the authority to approve a system for live use, and under what conditions can that authority be exercised
- **Operational intervention** — who can modify a system's parameters, pause its operation, or override its outputs in real time
- **Escalated cases** — who has the authority to make a binding determination on a case that has exceeded front-line review capacity
- **System changes** — who must authorise modifications to a system before they are implemented
- **Incident response** — who has the authority to suspend a system, notify affected individuals, or engage external parties during an incident
- **Vendor and third-party decisions** — who has the authority to instruct a vendor, invoke contractual remedies, or terminate a supplier relationship

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes decision authority seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- For each critical decision about this system, is there a named person or role with documented authority to make it?
- Does that person have the information, expertise, and organisational standing to exercise that authority soundly?
- Are the limits of each authority clearly defined — what can be decided at each level, and what must be escalated further?
- Is there a record of significant decisions made, by whom, on what authority, and on what basis?
- What happens when the person holding authority is unavailable — is there a defined deputy or fallback?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation has a governance structure in which power is assumed rather than assigned. Assumed power produces decisions that cannot be defended, attributed, or learned from.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Ensures that critical decisions about automated systems have a defined, accountable owner | Defining authority requires organisational decisions that are often politically difficult to make |
| Prevents paralysis when urgent action is required | Authority structures defined on paper may not reflect actual power dynamics within the organisation |
| Creates a documented record of who decided what and on what basis | Over-centralised authority creates bottlenecks — decisions that require senior sign-off can be delayed when speed matters |
| Supports accountability by connecting decisions to named individuals with formal mandates | Under-defined authority boundaries create disputes about who should act when situations fall between categories |
| Enables consistent decision-making across different people and circumstances | Authority without adequate information or expertise produces confident but poorly grounded decisions |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: organisations must be able to demonstrate not just that decisions about automated systems were made, but that they were made by someone with the authority, competence, and information to make them.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require that processing of personal data — including automated decision-making — be subject to defined governance structures with identifiable responsible parties. The inability to name who held decision authority over a system, and what that authority covered, is a governance gap that regulators treat seriously.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for defined human authority over automated systems, particularly for high-risk applications. Some frameworks are moving toward requirements that specific categories of decision be reserved for named senior roles — removing the possibility of decisions being made by default or by the most available person.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that cannot demonstrate who had decision authority over its automated system, and show that authority was exercised in an informed and documented way, cannot credibly claim it was in control of what the system did in its name.
