# JX-09 — India

> **India is building its AI governance framework from the ground up — with new data protection law in force, a national AI mission underway, and sector regulators developing specific expectations faster than central legislation.**

---

## The Framework

India's AI governance landscape is in active development. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA), enacted in 2023, provides the foundational data protection framework. Implementation regulations are being developed and the Act is not yet fully operational, but its structure is established and organisations should treat compliance as a current obligation.

India does not yet have AI-specific legislation equivalent to the EU AI Act. The government has instead pursued a "responsible AI" policy approach through NITI Aayog — which published responsible AI principles and national AI strategy documents — and through sector-specific guidance from financial, healthcare and telecommunications regulators.

The National AI Mission, launched in 2024 with significant government investment, signals India's strategic commitment to AI development. Governance frameworks are expected to develop alongside capability investment, with a regulatory sandbox approach being explored for AI applications in financial services and healthcare.

India's regulatory approach to AI is being shaped significantly by its experience with large-scale digital public infrastructure — Aadhaar, UPI, DigiLocker — which has created a distinctive perspective on the governance of automated systems affecting very large populations. The scale of AI deployment in India — in credit, agriculture, healthcare and government services — means governance frameworks will need to address contexts that have no direct equivalent in smaller economies.

---

## Key Regulators

**Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY)** — digital policy, DPDPA implementation, AI governance policy. Primary government body for AI regulation.

**Data Protection Board of India** — to be established under the DPDPA. Will have enforcement authority over data protection obligations including those affecting AI systems.

**Reserve Bank of India (RBI)** — AI in financial services, credit decisions, payment systems. Has published guidelines on model risk management for regulated entities.

**Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)** — AI in investment management, algorithmic trading, securities markets.

**Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI)** — AI in insurance underwriting and claims.

**NITI Aayog** — AI policy, responsible AI principles, national AI strategy. Advisory rather than regulatory, but influential in shaping the governance environment.

**Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI)** — AI in telecommunications.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**The DPDPA** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal data of individuals in India. The Act creates obligations to obtain consent, limit processing to stated purposes, maintain data accuracy, implement security safeguards and respond to individual rights requests. Automated decision-making that significantly affects individuals creates heightened obligations under the Act's framework.

**RBI guidelines** trigger for financial institutions using AI in regulated activities. The RBI has published guidance on model risk management that requires financial institutions to validate, monitor and govern AI models used in credit assessment and risk management. Algorithmic trading on Indian exchanges is subject to SEBI oversight.

**Sector-specific regulation** triggers for healthcare AI (medical device classification under the Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation), telecommunications (TRAI oversight), and insurance (IRDAI guidelines on underwriting algorithms).

**Competition law** — the Competition Act administered by the Competition Commission of India — triggers for algorithmic pricing and anti-competitive market behaviour, including in digital markets.

## Governance Connection

India's governance environment rewards organisations that build strong internal governance now, both because existing sector regulation requires it and because the DPDPA framework will impose substantive obligations as implementation develops.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a DPDPA principle. Data fiduciaries — organisations that determine the purpose of data processing — bear specific obligations that cannot be transferred to processors. Identifying the accountable data fiduciary for each AI system is a governance prerequisite.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is required under the DPDPA. Individuals must be given a privacy notice before their data is processed. AI systems making decisions based on personal data must operate within disclosed purposes.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under DPDPA accountability obligations. The Data Protection Board will have powers to demand documentation. RBI model risk management guidelines require detailed records of model development, validation and performance monitoring.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is expected in financial services contexts by the RBI. Given the scale of automated decision-making in Indian credit markets — affecting hundreds of millions of individuals — the governance of human review processes is a significant practical challenge as well as a regulatory requirement.

**Fairness** (→ SP-19, LL-03) has particular significance in the Indian context. AI systems used in credit, employment and government services affect populations across significant socioeconomic, regional and linguistic dimensions. Fairness assessment that considers these dimensions is not just a governance best practice — it is increasingly an expected element of responsible AI deployment in the Indian market.
