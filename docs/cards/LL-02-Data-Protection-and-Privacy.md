# LL-02 — Data Protection and Privacy

> **The legal framework most organisations already operate under — and the one most directly triggered by automated systems that process information about people.**

---

## What It Covers

Data protection and privacy law governs how organisations collect, store, use, share and delete personal data — information that identifies or can identify a living individual. It applies across almost every jurisdiction in which AI systems operate, and it applies to automated systems whenever those systems process personal data as part of their function.

The major frameworks include the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018, the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA), Brazil's Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados (LGPD), China's Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL), Canada's PIPEDA, Japan's Act on the Protection of Personal Information, South Korea's Personal Information Protection Act, and Australia's Privacy Act. These frameworks share common principles while differing in specific requirements, rights and enforcement mechanisms.

The GDPR is the most influential of these frameworks globally — its extraterritorial reach, its individual rights provisions, and its enforcement record have shaped data protection practice well beyond the EU's borders.

---

## Why AI Systems Trigger It

Automated systems trigger data protection law in several distinct ways, each of which carries specific obligations.

**Processing personal data to make decisions.** Any AI system that takes personal data as input — names, addresses, financial records, health information, behavioural data, location data, biometric data — and uses it to produce outputs that affect individuals is processing personal data. That processing must have a lawful basis. The organisation must be able to identify and document what that basis is before processing begins.

**Automated decision-making with significant effects.** Most data protection frameworks contain specific provisions addressing automated decisions that produce significant effects on individuals — credit refusals, employment rejections, benefit denials, insurance pricing. These provisions typically require that individuals be informed that a decision was automated, give individuals the right to request human review, and impose obligations on organisations to be able to explain the logic of automated decisions. Under the GDPR, Article 22 addresses this directly.

**Profiling.** AI systems that build profiles of individuals — assembling data about behaviour, preferences, characteristics or predicted future behaviour — engage profiling obligations under most frameworks. Profiling for certain purposes requires explicit consent or other defined lawful basis and carries specific transparency obligations.

**Data minimisation and purpose limitation.** Data protection law requires that organisations collect only the data necessary for defined purposes and use data only for those purposes. AI systems trained on large datasets, or deployed in ways that extend beyond the original purpose for which data was collected, frequently create tension with these principles.

**Individual rights.** Data protection frameworks give individuals rights over their personal data — rights of access, correction, deletion, restriction of processing, and data portability. Where AI systems process personal data, these rights apply to that processing. An individual can request to know what data the system holds about them, what decisions it made, and on what basis.

## Governance Connection

Data protection law translates several core Atlas governance concepts into enforceable legal obligations.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15) is a legal requirement. Individuals must be informed that automated processing is occurring, what data is used, what the logic of the system is, and what the consequences are. The legal standard requires that this information be provided in clear, plain language — not technical documentation.

**Explainability** (→ SP-16, RE-10) is directly required for automated decision-making. The right to an explanation of automated decisions is a legal right in most major frameworks, not a governance aspiration. Organisations that cannot provide individual-level explanations of specific decisions are in breach of that right.

**Record-keeping** (→ RE-07, LR-05) is a legal obligation. Most frameworks require organisations to maintain records of processing activities — what data is processed, for what purpose, on what legal basis, for how long. Those records must be available to regulators on demand.

**Data retention** (→ RE-08) is governed by law. Personal data cannot be kept indefinitely. Retention periods must be defined, documented and applied. AI systems that retain decision records containing personal data must comply with applicable retention limits.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01) is a legal principle under the GDPR and many equivalent frameworks — the accountability principle requires that organisations not just comply but be able to demonstrate compliance. The demonstration requirement makes records the foundation of legal compliance, not an optional governance activity.

**Enforcement** (→ LR-07) under GDPR includes fines of up to 20 million euros or four percent of global annual turnover for the most serious breaches. Equivalent enforcement powers exist under most major national frameworks.

---

## Jurisdictions

Data protection law applies in the EU and EEA (GDPR), the UK (UK GDPR), Brazil (LGPD), China (PIPL), Canada (PIPEDA), Japan, South Korea, Australia, and across US states including California, Virginia and Colorado. The US lacks a federal privacy law, creating a patchwork of state-level obligations that vary in scope and enforcement.
