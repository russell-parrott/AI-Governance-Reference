# MK-01 — Corporate Directors

> **Directors are accountable for the systems their organisations deploy — including automated ones they may never have seen. When an automated system causes harm, the governance question traces back to the board.**

---

## What This Market Is

Corporate directors — executive and non-executive — sit at the apex of organisational governance. They carry legal duties to their organisation and, in many jurisdictions, personal liability for governance failures. They appear in companies, charities, public bodies and regulated institutions. Their position in the governance structure is defined by authority and accountability: they approve, they oversee, and they answer.

In the context of AI governance, directors occupy the role that governance frameworks assign to named accountable individuals. They may not operate automated systems. They are nonetheless responsible for the conditions under which those systems are deployed and the consequences those systems produce.

---

## Why AI Governance Matters to Them

Automated systems create direct legal and reputational exposure for directors. Director duties — to exercise reasonable care, skill and diligence; to act in the interests of the organisation and its stakeholders; to avoid conflicts of interest — apply to AI deployment decisions in the same way they apply to any other significant operational commitment.

A director who approves the deployment of an AI system without adequate governance, or who allows a system to continue operating without oversight, has potentially breached their duty of care. In regulated sectors, personal accountability regimes — including the UK Senior Managers and Certification Regime and equivalent frameworks — assign named individual responsibility for AI systems explicitly.

The risk to directors is both institutional — regulatory action against the organisation — and personal — individual liability, disqualification and reputational damage.

---

## What Questions They Ask

Directors approach AI governance through the lens of authority, accountability and risk:

- Who in this organisation is formally accountable for this system?
- Was the deployment properly authorised, and by whom?
- What oversight exists, and does it function in practice?
- What could go wrong, and what is the organisation's exposure if it does?
- Are we meeting our legal and regulatory obligations in the jurisdictions where we operate?
- If something goes wrong tomorrow, what is our governance position?

Directors are not typically asking technical questions. They are asking governance questions — about authority, accountability and the adequacy of institutional controls.

---

## What Evidence They Expect

Directors rely on governance evidence rather than technical evidence:

- Board papers and minutes recording governance decisions about AI systems
- Deployment authorisation records showing who approved what and when
- Risk assessments and legal opinions addressing AI-specific obligations
- Assurance reports from internal audit, compliance and external reviewers
- Management information on system performance and incident history
- Named accountability structures showing who is responsible for each system

The evidence directors expect is the evidence that demonstrates the organisation exercised proper governance — not the evidence that demonstrates the system works.

---

## Where They Appear in Investigations

Directors appear in AI governance investigations at the accountability layer. Regulators and courts ask whether the board knew about the system, whether it was adequately governed, and whether the director discharged their duty of care.

In enforcement actions, regulators examine board papers, minutes and governance frameworks to assess whether AI deployment decisions received appropriate board-level attention. In litigation, directors may be asked to give evidence about what they knew, what they approved and what oversight they received.

In the most serious cases — where AI systems have caused significant harm — directors may face personal regulatory sanction or civil liability, particularly where they failed to ensure adequate governance despite having the authority and opportunity to do so.

## Typical Failure Points

**Governance by assurance.** Directors rely on management assurances that systems are properly governed without independently verifying that the underlying controls exist. When something goes wrong, the assurance is shown to have been unfounded — and the director who relied on it without adequate scrutiny is exposed.

**Authorisation without oversight.** Boards approve AI deployment in principle but establish no mechanism to receive ongoing information about how systems are performing, what incidents have occurred, or whether the governance arrangements remain adequate.

**No named accountability.** Boards approve AI use without assigning named individual accountability. When harm occurs, the accountability diffuses across the organisation and no one answers.

**Unexamined expansion.** Systems approved for one purpose are extended to other uses without returning to the board for reauthorisation. The board's governance approval no longer covers the system it approved.
