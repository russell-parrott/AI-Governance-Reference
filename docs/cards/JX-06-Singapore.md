# JX-06 — Singapore

> **Singapore built the world's first national AI governance framework and has continued to develop it with unusual deliberateness — producing guidance that is practical, sector-specific and internationally influential.**

---

## The Framework

Singapore's approach to AI governance is principled, voluntary at the cross-sector level, and mandatory within regulated sectors. The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) and other sector regulators have issued binding requirements for their industries, while the broader national framework operates as authoritative guidance rather than legislation.

The Model AI Governance Framework, first published by the Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA) in 2019 and updated in 2020, established Singapore's foundational governance principles. It is widely referenced internationally and has influenced governance frameworks in other jurisdictions. It addresses human-centric AI development, risk management, internal governance, operations and accountability.

The Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA), administered by the Personal Data Protection Commission (PDPC), applies to all organisations processing personal data of Singapore residents. Amendments in 2020 introduced mandatory data breach notification and strengthened enforcement powers. The PDPC has issued specific guidance on AI and personal data protection.

Singapore's AI Verify programme — a testing and certification toolkit — provides organisations with a practical mechanism to assess their AI systems against governance standards. While voluntary, AI Verify participation signals governance maturity and is increasingly referenced in procurement and regulatory contexts.

In 2024 the IMDA published updated generative AI governance guidance, and MAS published specific expectations for AI governance in financial services. Sector-specific requirements are becoming increasingly detailed and are where binding obligations concentrate.

---

## Key Regulators

**Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)** — cross-sector AI governance, Model AI Governance Framework, AI Verify, digital infrastructure. The primary body for AI policy development.

**Personal Data Protection Commission (PDPC)** — data protection, PDPA enforcement, AI and personal data guidance.

**Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS)** — AI in financial services. Has published FEAT principles (Fairness, Ethics, Accountability and Transparency) and specific guidance on responsible AI in financial services. One of the most developed financial AI governance frameworks globally.

**Ministry of Health / Health Sciences Authority** — AI in healthcare, software as a medical device.

**Competition and Consumer Commission of Singapore (CCCS)** — algorithmic pricing, competition concerns in digital markets.

## What Triggers Obligations

**The PDPA** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal data of Singapore residents. Organisations must have a lawful basis for processing, implement data protection policies, and respond to individual rights requests. The PDPC has specifically addressed AI systems that make decisions based on personal data, requiring that individuals be informed and that data used in decisions is accurate.

**MAS FEAT principles and guidance** trigger for any financial institution using AI in regulated activities. The FEAT principles require that AI decisions be fair, ethical, accountable and transparent. MAS's 2024 generative AI guidelines for financial services set specific expectations for risk assessment, human oversight and customer communication where generative AI is used.

**AI Verify** does not create mandatory obligations but participation is increasingly expected in regulated sectors and government procurement. The framework addresses eleven governance principles and provides testing methodologies.

**Sector-specific regulation** triggers for healthcare AI (medical device classification), transport AI (safety requirements), and telecommunications (service continuity).

## Governance Connection

Singapore's governance framework is unusually well aligned with Atlas concepts because the Model AI Governance Framework was explicitly designed around governance principles rather than legal compliance. The Atlas and Singapore's framework are solving the same problem from compatible directions.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a FEAT principle and a core element of the Model AI Governance Framework. MAS expects financial institutions to have named individuals accountable for AI systems and board-level oversight of AI risk. The governance requirement is specific and verifiable.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is addressed throughout Singapore's AI governance approach. The Model AI Governance Framework explicitly distinguishes between systems that are human-in-the-loop, human-on-the-loop and fully automated, and calibrates governance requirements accordingly.

**Fairness** (→ SP-19, LL-03) is the F in FEAT. MAS expects financial institutions to test for algorithmic bias and demonstrate fair outcomes across demographic groups. This is a sector-specific governance obligation in Singapore's financial services context.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, GV-N05) is the T in FEAT. Customers must be informed when AI is used in decisions affecting them, and organisations must be able to explain those decisions. The requirement is outcomes-focused — transparency that does not reach the customer is not sufficient.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under both PDPA accountability obligations and MAS governance expectations. MAS expects financial institutions to maintain records of AI model development, validation, monitoring and changes — equivalent to the model risk management documentation required in other major financial jurisdictions.
