# MK-02 — Compliance Officers

> **Compliance officers translate regulatory obligations into operational controls. For AI systems, their challenge is mapping a rapidly changing legal landscape onto systems whose governance properties are often undocumented, untested and poorly understood.**

---

## What This Market Is

Compliance officers work within organisations to ensure that operations meet applicable legal and regulatory requirements. They appear across financial services, healthcare, public administration and any sector subject to substantive regulatory oversight. Their position in the governance structure sits between regulatory obligation and operational reality — translating what the law requires into what the organisation does.

In the context of AI governance, compliance officers are typically the first internal function to recognise that automated systems create specific regulatory obligations. They are responsible for identifying which frameworks apply, mapping those frameworks to operational processes, designing compliance controls, and providing assurance that obligations are being met. They are also frequently the internal function that faces regulators first when something goes wrong.

---

## Why AI Governance Matters to Them

Compliance officers face a particular challenge with AI systems: the regulatory frameworks are complex, overlapping and rapidly evolving, while the systems they must assess are technically opaque and frequently underdocumented.

The obligations they must map include data protection law, sector-specific regulation, equality and anti-discrimination frameworks, consumer protection requirements and — increasingly — AI-specific regulation. Each of these frameworks imposes different requirements on different aspects of AI governance. Compliance officers must understand enough about how systems work to assess whether those requirements are met — and they must do so in organisations where technical and governance functions are often separated.

The professional risk is substantial: a compliance failure involving an AI system may constitute a regulatory breach that triggers enforcement action, and the compliance function that failed to identify or address it is directly implicated.

---

## What Questions They Ask

Compliance officers approach AI governance through a regulatory mapping lens:

- Which regulatory frameworks apply to this system in this deployment context?
- What obligations do those frameworks impose, and are they being met?
- Is there documented evidence that compliance obligations have been assessed and addressed?
- Are the data protection, equality, consumer protection and sector-specific requirements all covered?
- What is the organisation's exposure if a regulator examines this system?
- Are the compliance controls designed for the current regulatory environment or for an earlier one?

Compliance officers are the internal function most focused on the gap between what the law requires and what the organisation is actually doing.

---

## What Evidence They Expect

Compliance officers rely on documented evidence that regulatory obligations have been identified and addressed:

- Legal and regulatory mapping documents identifying which frameworks apply
- Data protection impact assessments and records of processing activities
- Evidence of compliance with automated decision rights obligations
- Documented risk classification under applicable AI frameworks
- Internal audit findings and management responses
- Training records showing that staff understand their compliance obligations
- Incident records and regulatory notification history

The evidence compliance officers expect is the evidence they would produce to a regulator — the documented record of compliance assessment and control.

---

## Where They Appear in Investigations

Compliance officers are typically among the first internal respondents when regulatory investigations begin. They coordinate the organisation's response to regulatory inquiries, identify and produce relevant documents, and provide the regulator with the organisation's account of its compliance position.

In enforcement proceedings, the compliance function's records — what was assessed, what was identified, what was addressed and when — become central evidence. A compliance function that identified a regulatory risk and failed to address it is in a significantly worse position than one that had no opportunity to identify it.

Compliance officers may also be called to give evidence in regulatory proceedings about the organisation's compliance framework and whether it was adequate for the obligations it was designed to address.

## Typical Failure Points

**Compliance without technical understanding.** Compliance officers assess regulatory obligations against governance documentation without understanding what the system actually does. The compliance assessment maps the documentation rather than the system — and the documentation does not reflect reality.

**Framework gaps.** Organisations focus compliance assessment on the most visible regulatory framework — typically data protection — and miss the intersection with equality law, sector regulation, consumer protection or AI-specific requirements.

**Static compliance in a dynamic environment.** Compliance assessments are conducted at deployment and not revisited as the regulatory environment changes, the system is modified or its deployment context expands.

**Compliance as documentation rather than control.** Compliance frameworks produce documentation — policies, impact assessments, records — that satisfies the appearance of regulatory compliance without creating the operational controls that compliance requires.
