# AR-11 — Policy vs Practice

> **The gap between what is written and what actually happens. This card explains why that gap exists — and why it matters.**

---

## What Is It?

The gap between policy and practice is the difference between what an organisation's governance documents say it does and what it actually does in daily operation.

Policy is the written statement of intent — the procedures, standards, and rules the organisation has committed to following in governing its automated systems. Practice is what happens on the ground — the actual behaviour of the people responsible for governance when they are under pressure, under-resourced, or simply accustomed to shortcuts that have never been challenged.

The gap between them is not a sign of dishonesty. It is a predictable feature of every organisation that writes governance documents without the same rigour it applies to implementing them. Policies describe the world as it should be. Practice reflects the world as it is.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Governance policies are the organisation's public commitment — to regulators, to affected individuals, and to itself — about how its automated systems are overseen. When those commitments are not reflected in practice, the organisation is misrepresenting its governance. Not necessarily deliberately. But materially.

The gap between policy and practice is where most governance failures actually live. Incidents rarely occur because an organisation had no policy. They occur because the policy was not followed, not enforced, and not verified. The document existed. The behaviour did not match it.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Policies that create liability rather than protection.** A governance policy that is not followed is worse than no policy in some respects. It demonstrates that the organisation knew what good governance looked like, committed to it, and then did not do it. That is harder to defend than genuine ignorance of the standard.

**Governance gaps hidden by documentation.** An organisation with comprehensive governance documentation appears well governed from the outside. Regulators conducting paper-based assessments may find nothing wrong. The gap between policy and practice is invisible until someone looks at what is actually happening — which typically happens during an incident investigation.

**Normalised non-compliance.** When policy requirements are routinely not met without consequence, non-compliance becomes the de facto standard. People new to the organisation learn the actual practice, not the written one. The gap between policy and practice widens and solidifies. By the time it is noticed, it is embedded in organisational culture.

**Policies that were never realistic.** Some governance policies are aspirational documents written to satisfy an external requirement without genuine intention or capacity to implement them. Those policies are not a governance framework. They are a governance fiction — and when they are tested against reality, the fiction becomes an evidentiary problem.

Responsibility for closing the gap between policy and practice sits with management and the board. Writing policy is not governance. Verifying that policy is being followed is governance.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

The policy-practice gap surfaces wherever governance commitments exceed governance capacity or culture:

- **Monitoring requirements** — policies specify monitoring frequency and scope that operational teams do not have the resource or time to deliver consistently
- **Review obligations** — governance calendars commit to review schedules that are compressed, deferred, or bypassed under operational pressure
- **Documentation standards** — policies require records that are created incompletely, after the fact, or not at all when the governance activity was informal
- **Escalation procedures** — written escalation paths that are bypassed in practice because of cultural pressure, unclear ownership, or lack of confidence in the process
- **Testing requirements** — pre-deployment testing obligations that are abbreviated or skipped when deployment timelines are tight
- **Approval gates** — formal sign-off requirements that are treated as formalities rather than genuine decision points

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes the policy-practice gap seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Is there a mechanism for verifying that governance policies are being followed in practice — not just that they exist on paper?
- When governance activities fall short of policy requirements, is that identified, recorded, and addressed rather than absorbed silently into normal operations?
- Are governance policies written to reflect what the organisation can realistically do, or do they describe an ideal that operational reality does not support?
- Is there a regular review of whether policy commitments remain aligned with actual practice and organisational capacity?
- When the gap between policy and practice is identified, who is responsible for closing it — and is that responsibility clearly assigned?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation has governance policies it cannot verify are being followed. That is not a documentation problem. It is the condition under which every other governance card in this series becomes irrelevant — because the policies exist and the practice does not.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Identifying the policy-practice gap enables targeted remediation rather than broad governance redesign | The gap is often invisible to those closest to operations — it requires deliberate effort from above to surface |
| Regular verification of actual practice against policy creates accountability at every level | Verification mechanisms — audits, spot checks, testing — require resource that governance functions often do not have |
| Honest policy design — committing only to what can be delivered — produces more reliable governance than aspirational documentation | Cultural resistance to surfacing non-compliance makes the gap difficult to identify and harder to close |
| Closing the gap builds genuine governance rather than the appearance of it | Organisations under regulatory pressure may close the gap on paper — updating records rather than changing behaviour |
| Demonstrated alignment between policy and practice is the strongest possible position before a regulator | Policies written to satisfy external requirements without genuine implementation intent are difficult to remediate quickly when tested |

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: regulators and courts assess governance not by what policies say but by what organisations demonstrably do — and the gap between the two is itself a finding.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require organisations to demonstrate compliance through evidence of actual practice, not documentation of intended practice. Audits, investigations, and enforcement actions consistently find that governance failures originate in the gap between committed policy and actual behaviour — and treat that gap as the organisation's accountability failure.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for governance verification — mechanisms that test whether governance commitments are being met in practice, not just documented. Some frameworks explicitly require organisations to audit their own compliance with governance obligations and to produce the results of those audits on request.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation whose governance policies are not reflected in its practice is not protected by those policies — it is exposed by them. The policy demonstrates knowledge of the standard. The practice demonstrates failure to meet it. That combination, in front of a regulator or a court, is the most difficult governance position to defend.
