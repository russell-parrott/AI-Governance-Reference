# LR-03 — Compliance Duties

> **The transition to law. This card explains the basic duty to follow rules — and why "we didn't know" is not a defence.**

---

## What Is It?

Compliance duties are the legal obligations an organisation carries in relation to its automated systems — the rules it must follow, the standards it must meet, and the requirements it must demonstrate it has satisfied, by virtue of operating in a regulated environment.

They are not optional. They are not aspirational. They are the baseline beneath which an organisation's conduct cannot fall without legal consequence.

Compliance duties arise from multiple sources simultaneously — data protection law, sector regulation, employment law, consumer protection, equality obligations, and increasingly AI-specific legislation. An organisation deploying an automated system inherits the compliance obligations that apply to what that system does, to whom, and in what sector — whether or not those obligations were understood at the point of deployment.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Organisations frequently approach compliance as a constraint — a set of requirements to be met sufficiently to avoid regulatory attention. That framing misses what compliance duties actually represent. They are the legal expression of the accountability obligations that governance exists to discharge. The duty to monitor a system, to document its behaviour, to explain its decisions, to assess its fairness — these are not bureaucratic impositions. They are legal codifications of the accountability that operating an automated system in a society governed by law requires.

An organisation that understands its compliance duties understands what the law requires of its governance. An organisation that does not is operating with unknown exposure.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Compliance obligations that were never identified.** An organisation that deployed an automated system without mapping the compliance obligations that apply to it is operating with legal exposure it has not assessed. Those obligations do not disappear because they were not identified. They apply from the first day of operation and accumulate with every decision the system makes.

**Obligations met in form but not in substance.** Compliance that satisfies the letter of a requirement while ignoring its purpose is increasingly recognised by regulators as non-compliance. A monitoring policy that exists but is not followed, a review process that runs but produces no meaningful findings, a documentation standard that is met by records that contain no substance — these are the forms of compliance that fail when tested.

**Obligations that change while practice does not.** The legal landscape for automated systems is evolving rapidly. Obligations that did not exist when a system was deployed may apply to it now. An organisation that has not kept its compliance mapping current may be meeting yesterday's requirements while failing today's.

**Assuming sector exemptions that do not exist.** Organisations sometimes assume that general data protection or AI compliance obligations do not apply to them because they are governed by sector-specific regulation. In most jurisdictions, sector regulation operates alongside general obligations, not instead of them. The assumption of exemption is itself a compliance risk.

Responsibility for identifying, meeting, and maintaining awareness of compliance duties sits with the organisation's leadership. Compliance is not a function that can be delegated away from governance. It is a dimension of governance.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Compliance duties for automated systems arise in every sector and operational context where law applies to automated decision-making:

- **Financial services** — duties under financial services regulation, consumer protection law, data protection frameworks, and equality obligations applying to automated credit, insurance, and trading systems
- **Healthcare** — duties under healthcare regulation, data protection law, and patient rights frameworks applying to automated clinical and administrative systems
- **Public services** — duties under administrative law, equality obligations, data protection frameworks, and human rights law applying to automated eligibility and enforcement systems
- **Employment** — duties under employment law, equality legislation, and data protection frameworks applying to automated screening, monitoring, and management systems
- **Platform and content** — duties under content regulation, consumer protection law, competition law, and data protection frameworks applying to automated moderation and recommendation systems
- **Any organisation processing personal data through automated systems** — where data protection compliance duties apply universally regardless of sector

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes compliance duties seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Has the organisation mapped the compliance obligations that apply to each of its automated systems — across all relevant legal frameworks, not just the most obvious one?
- Is there a process for monitoring changes to the legal landscape and assessing their implications for existing systems?
- Are compliance obligations reflected in governance practice — in monitoring, documentation, review, and accountability arrangements — rather than just in policy documents?
- When compliance obligations are not being met, is that identified, recorded, and addressed through a defined remediation process?
- Is the organisation's compliance position assessed by someone with the legal knowledge to identify gaps that operational teams may not recognise?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation is operating automated systems with legal obligations it may not have fully identified and governance practices that may not reflect what the law requires. Compliance duties do not wait to be acknowledged. They apply whether the organisation is ready for them or not.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| A clear compliance map creates a structured basis for governance design — obligations drive the requirements | Compliance mapping across multiple simultaneous legal frameworks is complex and requires legal expertise many organisations lack in-house |
| Meeting compliance duties protects the organisation from enforcement action and provides a basis for regulatory engagement | Compliance landscapes for automated systems are evolving rapidly — mapping done at deployment becomes outdated |
| Compliance obligations provide an external standard against which governance adequacy can be assessed | Compliance that is treated as a ceiling rather than a floor produces governance that is legally defensible but not necessarily good |
| Demonstrating compliance proactively builds regulatory trust and can influence how investigations are conducted | The interaction between multiple compliance frameworks applying simultaneously can produce conflicting requirements that are difficult to navigate |
| Compliance awareness at leadership level ensures governance decisions are made with legal obligations in view | Compliance functions that operate separately from operational governance produce paper compliance rather than embedded practice |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent and accelerating: the compliance obligations applying to automated systems are expanding in scope, specificity, and enforceability — and the standard of proof required to demonstrate compliance is rising.

Data protection and privacy frameworks impose baseline compliance duties on automated processing of personal data that apply across all sectors and all types of automated system. Those duties include accuracy, fairness, transparency, accountability, and the ability to respond to individual rights requests — obligations that are not sector-specific but universal.

AI-specific legislation is adding a layer of compliance obligations on top of existing frameworks — particularly for high-risk systems — that require conformity assessments, governance documentation, technical standards compliance, and regulatory notification. The cumulative effect is a compliance environment significantly more demanding than the one that existed when most currently operating automated systems were deployed.

The practical legal risk is foundational: every other card in this series describes a governance concept whose legal dimension is ultimately grounded in compliance duties. An organisation that does not know what it is legally required to do cannot assess whether it is doing it. And an organisation that cannot assess its own compliance position is not governing its automated systems. It is hoping.
