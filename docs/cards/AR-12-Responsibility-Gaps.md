# AR-12 — Responsibility Gaps

> **Following logically from policy and practice — where does responsibility fall through the cracks?**

---

## What Is It?

A responsibility gap is a situation in which no one is clearly accountable for a specific governance obligation — where the allocation of responsibilities across roles, functions, and structures leaves a space that everyone assumes someone else is covering.

Responsibility gaps are not the same as ignored responsibilities. They are structural absences — places where the governance design itself failed to assign accountability clearly enough, or where accountability was assigned in ways that do not reflect how the organisation actually operates. No individual is necessarily negligent. The structure is incomplete.

Responsibility gaps exist in the space between roles, at the boundaries between functions, in the handover between individuals, and wherever governance was designed for a simpler version of the organisation than the one that exists today.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Governance structures are designed with intentions. The intentions are good. But every governance design has edges — places where the logic of accountability runs out, where one person's responsibility ends and the next person's has not clearly begun, where a system or activity sits in the overlap between two functions that each believe the other has it covered.

Those edges are where harm accumulates. Not because anyone decided to leave them ungoverned. Because nobody noticed they were there — until something went wrong in exactly the place where nobody was looking.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Gaps at functional boundaries.** The most common responsibility gaps occur where accountability crosses organisational boundaries — between technology and business functions, between compliance and operations, between the organisation and its vendors. Each party governs its own side. The boundary itself is ungoverned.

**Gaps created by organisational change.** Mergers, restructures, new system deployments, and personnel changes all shift the governance landscape. Responsibility that was clearly assigned before a change may be unclaimed after it. The system continues operating. The governance of it has been orphaned.

**Gaps hidden by overlapping roles.** Sometimes the problem is not absence but ambiguity — two roles that each carry partial responsibility for the same governance obligation, with neither carrying it fully. Each assumes the other is handling the parts they are not. The overlap looks like double coverage. It functions as none.

**Gaps that are known but unresolved.** Perhaps the most troubling form: responsibility gaps that have been identified, noted, perhaps even discussed, but never formally resolved because doing so would require uncomfortable decisions about who takes on additional work, or because the gap sits between functions that cannot agree on ownership. Known gaps that persist are not oversights. They are accepted risks that have not been acknowledged as such.

Responsibility for identifying and closing responsibility gaps sits with the board and senior leadership. Gaps at the operational level are management's problem to find and fix. Gaps in the governance structure itself require authority that only leadership can exercise.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Responsibility gaps are a feature of any sufficiently complex governance structure:

- **System handovers** — when a system moves from development to operation, from one team to another, or from a project structure to a business-as-usual one, governance responsibilities may not transfer cleanly
- **Vendor boundaries** — where vendor responsibility ends and organisational responsibility begins is frequently unclear in practice, even where contracts attempt to define it
- **Cross-functional systems** — automated systems that serve multiple business functions may be claimed by none of them as their primary governance responsibility
- **Legacy systems** — older automated systems may predate current governance structures, leaving them without a clear owner in the current organisational design
- **New risk categories** — governance structures designed before a particular risk was understood may have no assigned responsibility for it — bias monitoring and explainability obligations being common recent examples
- **Incident response boundaries** — when something goes wrong, the handover between operational response, legal, communications, and regulatory teams is a frequent site of responsibility gaps under pressure

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes responsibility gaps seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Has the organisation mapped its governance obligations against its actual governance structure to identify where coverage is incomplete?
- Are the boundaries between roles, functions, and vendor responsibilities clearly enough defined to confirm that each governance obligation has a single, unambiguous owner?
- Is there a process for identifying new responsibility gaps as the organisation, its systems, and its regulatory environment change?
- When a responsibility gap is identified, is there a defined route for resolving it — including escalating it to the level with authority to assign ownership?
- Are known, unresolved responsibility gaps formally recorded as accepted risks — with named owners, defined review dates, and accountability for resolution?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation has governance obligations that are not covered — and does not have a complete picture of which ones. Responsibility gaps that are not mapped are not managed. They are waiting to become the explanation for the next incident.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Mapping responsibility gaps makes them visible and therefore manageable | Identifying all responsibility gaps requires systematic analysis that most organisations have not conducted |
| Formal gap identification creates accountability for resolution — gaps cannot persist without being acknowledged | Closing responsibility gaps requires decisions about who takes on additional work, which creates organisational resistance |
| Recording known gaps as accepted risks ensures they are reviewed and not simply forgotten | Gaps at functional boundaries require cooperation between functions that may have competing priorities |
| Regular gap reviews keep governance coverage aligned with organisational change | Responsibility gaps can re-emerge after restructuring, system changes, or personnel turnover without being detected |
| Demonstrates governance maturity to regulators — the organisation knows what it does not have covered | Organisations frequently discover responsibility gaps only when an incident reveals the absence of a clear owner |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: responsibility gaps in automated system governance are not accepted as mitigating factors in regulatory investigations — they are treated as evidence of inadequate governance design.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require organisations to be able to demonstrate that accountability for automated processing is clearly assigned and actively exercised. A gap in that assignment — even an unintentional one — does not reduce the organisation's legal exposure for what happened in the ungoverned space. It increases it.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for complete and documented accountability structures in automated system governance, with regulators specifically examining whether all governance obligations are clearly owned and whether gaps exist at functional and vendor boundaries.

The practical legal risk is direct: when something goes wrong in the space where nobody was responsible, the investigation does not conclude that nobody is to blame. It concludes that the organisation failed to design a governance structure adequate to cover what it was doing. The gap is the finding — and the organisation that created it owns the consequences.
