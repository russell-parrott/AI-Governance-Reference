# JX-01 — United Kingdom

> **The UK has chosen a deliberately different path from the EU — sector-led, principles-based, and without a single binding AI law. That choice has governance implications of its own.**

---

## The Framework

The United Kingdom does not have a single AI-specific law equivalent to the EU AI Act. Following Brexit, the UK chose a different regulatory model: existing regulators apply existing laws to AI systems within their sectors, guided by a set of cross-sector AI principles that government has published but not yet legislated.

The five cross-sector principles — safety and security, transparency and explainability, fairness, accountability and governance, and contestability and redress — were published in the government's AI Regulation White Paper in 2023. They are currently non-binding guidance, though the government has signalled legislative intent as AI risks become clearer.

In practice this means UK AI governance is shaped by a combination of existing law — UK GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018, the Equality Act 2010, the Consumer Rights Act 2015, the Companies Act 2006, the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 — and sector-specific regulatory expectations issued by individual regulators. The sector-led model means obligations vary significantly depending on what an organisation does and who regulates it.

The UK's approach is under active review. The AI Safety Institute, established in 2023, focuses on frontier model risk. The proposed AI Opportunities Action Plan signals continued engagement. The landscape is moving and organisations should treat current guidance as a floor, not a ceiling.

---

## Key Regulators

**Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)** — data protection, automated decision-making, individual rights under UK GDPR. The ICO has published specific guidance on AI and data protection, including the use of AI in automated decision-making under Article 22 equivalents.

**Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)** — AI in financial services. The FCA has published a Discussion Paper on AI (DP5/22) and is developing specific expectations for firms using AI in regulated activities. Principles-based but increasingly specific.

**Competition and Markets Authority (CMA)** — algorithmic pricing, AI in digital markets, foundation model oversight. The CMA published an initial review of AI foundation models in 2023 and has made AI markets a strategic priority.

**Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC)** — discrimination law application to automated decisions. Enforces the Equality Act 2010.

**Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA)** — AI as a medical device. Published guidance on software and AI as medical devices.

**AI Safety Institute** — evaluation of frontier AI models. Not a traditional regulator but influential in shaping government policy.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018** trigger whenever an AI system processes personal data — which includes most systems that make decisions about individuals. The UK GDPR retains Article 22 equivalent provisions giving individuals rights regarding solely automated decisions with significant effects. The ICO expects organisations to conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments for high-risk automated processing.

**The Equality Act 2010** triggers whenever automated systems influence decisions in employment, financial services, housing, education or public services. The outcome-based discrimination standard applies in full — discriminatory outputs are unlawful regardless of intent.

**FCA rules** trigger for any regulated financial services activity. The FCA's existing principles — including Principle 6 (treating customers fairly) and the Consumer Duty (effective from 2023) — apply to AI-driven customer interactions and financial decisions. The Consumer Duty raises the standard materially: firms must demonstrate good outcomes for customers, not just rule compliance.

**The Companies Act 2006** applies to boards approving AI deployment. Director duties — including the duty to exercise reasonable care, skill and diligence — apply to AI governance decisions.

**The CMA** has jurisdiction where algorithmic systems affect competition. The Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 expanded CMA powers in digital markets.

## Governance Connection

The UK's sector-led approach means governance requirements are not concentrated in a single framework but distributed across the regulators relevant to what the organisation does. The governance concepts in the Atlas map directly to what each regulator expects.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is reinforced by the Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR) in financial services, which requires named senior individuals to be accountable for AI systems within their remit. Outside financial services, Companies Act director duties apply.

**Explainability** (→ SP-16, LL-02) is a UK GDPR requirement for automated decisions. The ICO has published specific guidance on what meaningful explanation requires — not a technical description of the model, but an account of why a specific decision was made about a specific individual.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is an expectation of both the ICO and the FCA. The ICO's guidance on automated decision-making requires that humans can review and override decisions. The FCA's Consumer Duty requires that firms monitor outcomes — passive oversight does not satisfy the standard.

**Records** (→ RE-07, LL-02) are required under UK GDPR, FCA record-keeping rules and Companies Act governance obligations. The ICO can demand documentation of processing activities, risk assessments and the basis for automated decisions.

**Fairness** (→ SP-19, LL-03) is both a legal requirement under the Equality Act and a regulatory expectation under the FCA's Consumer Duty. The FCA has specifically addressed the risk of AI systems producing unfair outcomes for different customer groups.
