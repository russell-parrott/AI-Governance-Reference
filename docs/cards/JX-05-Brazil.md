# JX-05 — Brazil

> **Brazil has enacted South America's most developed AI governance framework — a risk-based law aligned with the EU AI Act, sitting alongside one of the world's strongest data protection regimes.**

---

## The Framework

Brazil's AI governance framework rests on two pillars. The first is the Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados (LGPD), the General Data Protection Law enacted in 2018 and in full effect since 2021. The LGPD is structurally similar to the GDPR and applies to any processing of personal data of individuals in Brazil, regardless of where the processing organisation is located.

The second pillar is the Brazilian Artificial Intelligence Law, enacted in 2024 following several years of parliamentary development. The law adopts a risk-based classification model aligned with the EU AI Act, dividing AI systems into risk categories with different obligations at each level. High-risk systems — those with significant potential to affect fundamental rights, health, safety or access to essential services — face the most demanding requirements.

The Marco Civil da Internet (Brazil's internet civil rights framework), the Consumer Defense Code, and sector-specific regulation in financial services, healthcare and telecommunications add further layers of obligation. Brazil has a sophisticated regulatory environment for digital services, and AI systems that interact with Brazilian consumers or process Brazilian personal data face obligations under multiple frameworks simultaneously.

---

## Key Regulators

**Autoridade Nacional de Proteção de Dados (ANPD)** — Brazil's data protection authority, established under the LGPD. Enforces data protection obligations including those relating to automated decision-making. Has issued regulations on LGPD implementation and is developing AI-specific guidance.

**Banco Central do Brasil (BCB)** — AI in financial services, open finance, credit decisions. Has published resolutions on algorithmic models in financial institutions.

**Agência Nacional de Saúde Suplementar (ANS)** — AI in health insurance and healthcare.

**Conselho Administrativo de Defesa Econômica (CADE)** — competition and antitrust, algorithmic pricing.

**Procon network** — consumer protection enforcement at federal and state level.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**The LGPD** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal data of individuals in Brazil. Automated decision-making that affects individuals' interests — particularly decisions with legal effects or similarly significant consequences — is subject to specific obligations. Individuals have the right to request review of automated decisions by a human, and to receive information about the criteria and procedures used.

**The AI Law** triggers based on risk classification. High-risk applications — including systems used in critical infrastructure, education, employment, credit, healthcare and law enforcement — face pre-deployment conformity requirements, ongoing monitoring obligations, transparency duties and human oversight requirements. The law imposes accountability obligations on both developers and deployers.

**The Consumer Defense Code** triggers in any consumer-facing AI context. Brazil's consumer protection law is robust and actively enforced. AI-driven pricing, recommendations and customer service interactions that disadvantage consumers are actionable.

**Financial regulation** triggers for AI in credit assessment, investment services and insurance. The BCB has published specific guidance on model governance for financial institutions that applies directly to AI systems used in lending and risk assessment.

---

## Governance Connection

Brazil's combined LGPD and AI Law framework creates governance obligations that map closely to the Atlas concepts, making well-governed systems straightforwardly compliant.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a named principle of the LGPD. Controllers must designate a Data Protection Officer (Encarregado) responsible for overseeing compliance. The AI Law extends accountability to AI-specific governance roles.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is a legal right under the LGPD. Individuals subject to automated decisions have the right to request human review. Organisations must have a functional human review process — not merely a stated policy.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is required under both frameworks. The LGPD requires that automated processing be disclosed. The AI Law requires that high-risk systems provide information about their logic and decision criteria.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under the LGPD's accountability principle. The ANPD can require organisations to demonstrate their compliance through documentation. Records of automated decisions, processing activities and governance arrangements must be maintained.

**Risk classification** (→ LR-13, LL-01) under the AI Law determines which obligations apply. Organisations must assess where their systems fall in the risk hierarchy before deployment — a governance requirement that precedes all other compliance activities.

**Fairness** (→ SP-19, LL-03) is addressed under both the LGPD and Brazil's discrimination law. AI systems that produce discriminatory outcomes in employment, credit or access to services are actionable under multiple frameworks.
