# GF-02 — Responsibility Without Authority

> **A person is named responsible for a system but lacks the authority to govern or intervene in it.**

---

## What This Failure Is

A name appears in the governance documentation against the system. That person is formally responsible. But they cannot commission an independent audit, cannot suspend the system, cannot direct the vendor to make changes, cannot access the technical records they would need to exercise meaningful oversight. Responsibility has been assigned. Authority has not.

The result is accountability theatre — a named individual who satisfies the governance requirement for a responsible person but who is structurally prevented from discharging the responsibility they have been given.

---

## Where It Occurs

This failure appears most frequently when accountability structures are designed to satisfy a regulatory requirement without reference to the actual power structures of the organisation; when AI systems are operated through vendor relationships that place governance-critical authority outside the organisation; when named responsible individuals are at the wrong level of the organisation to exercise the authority they nominally hold; and when responsibility is assigned to specialist functions — ethics, AI governance — that have no formal authority over operational or technical decisions.

---

## Why It Matters

When regulators or courts examine whether a system was properly governed, they ask whether the named responsible individual had the authority and means to govern it. A responsibility assignment unsupported by authority is not a governance control. It is a governance claim that collapses under scrutiny.

The individual named as responsible faces personal exposure for a system they were structurally unable to govern. The organisation faces exposure for governance arrangements that were designed to appear adequate rather than to be adequate.

---

## How It Is Discovered

This failure is most commonly discovered when regulators interview the named responsible individual and find that they were unable to obtain information, commission review or intervene in the system's operation; when litigation exposes that the named accountable person had no meaningful authority; and when internal audit examines whether governance roles were supported by commensurate authority.

---

## Evidence That Reveals It

Evidence includes the absence of any record of the responsible individual commissioning reviews, escalating concerns or directing changes; the responsible individual's inability to produce governance records about the system; and governance documentation that names an individual without specifying the authority that individual holds.

---

## Governance Concepts Involved

→ AR-01 (Accountability) — the obligation that cannot be discharged without authority
→ AR-05 (Role Definition) — the mechanism that should align responsibility with authority
→ AR-04 (Decision Authority) — the authority dimension that is absent
→ AR-08 (Vendor Responsibility) — a common location for this failure in externally operated systems
→ AR-12 (Responsibility Gaps) — the broader accountability failure this exemplifies

---

## What Organisations Often Assume Instead

That naming an individual satisfies the accountability requirement regardless of whether that individual has the authority to act. That responsibility and authority are the same thing. That a governance role creates governance capacity.
