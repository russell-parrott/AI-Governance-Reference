# GQ-04 — Is responsibility formally assigned in writing?

> **Not understood. Not assumed. Not distributed across a committee. Written down, assigned to a name, and signed off.**

---

## What

This question is asking whether the organisation has formally documented who is responsible for each significant aspect of its automated systems — in writing, with enough specificity that the answer to "who is responsible for this?" is a name, not a description, a team, or a reference to a committee.

Formal assignment in writing means: there is a document — a role description, a governance record, a terms of reference, an accountability framework — that names a specific individual, defines what they are responsible for in relation to a specific system, and records that the assignment was made and accepted. It means the assignment survives personnel change — a new person taking the role inherits documented responsibilities rather than discovering them gradually. It means the organisation can produce the document when asked.

The question is not asking whether responsibility is clear in practice, whether everyone knows who does what, or whether governance works well in normal operation. It is asking whether the responsibility is anchored in something that persists beyond the people currently in post — something that can be examined, tested, and held to account.

---

## Why

Unwritten responsibility is assumption. And assumptions about responsibility are the most reliable predictor of responsibility gaps — the spaces where nobody acts because everyone believed someone else was responsible.

Formal written assignment does three things that informal understanding cannot. It makes responsibility visible — the document can be produced, examined, and compared against what actually happened. It makes responsibility durable — it persists when the people who held the informal understanding leave the organisation, change roles, or simply forget. And it makes responsibility testable — when a question arises about whether responsibility was discharged, there is a documented standard to assess against.

Written responsibility also changes behaviour. People who know their responsibilities are documented — and that they may be asked to demonstrate they discharged them — govern differently from people whose responsibilities are implicit. The document creates the accountability that informal understanding cannot.

The absence of formal written assignment is not a neutral state. It is the condition that allows governance gaps to develop invisibly, allows accountability to be deflected when problems arise, and allows the organisation to believe it is governed when it is not.

---

## When

**When something goes wrong.** The first question in any incident investigation is who was responsible. An organisation with written responsibility assignments can answer immediately. One without them enters a process of reconstruction — working out retrospectively who should have been responsible, in the knowledge that everyone who might bear accountability has an interest in the answer.

**When personnel change.** A senior person leaves. They took with them an informal understanding of their responsibilities for a system that continues to operate. Their successor inherits the role but not the knowledge of what it entailed for governance of that system. Written responsibility assignment is what makes handover governance rather than hope.

**When a regulator asks.** Regulatory examinations of governance ask for accountability documentation — evidence that responsibility was formally assigned, to whom, and for what. The organisation that produces a governance chart and a set of role descriptions has answered the question. The one that describes how responsibility is understood informally has not.

**When accountability is disputed.** Something failed. Multiple people and functions were involved. Each points to the others. In the absence of written responsibility assignment, the dispute is difficult to resolve — because there is no document that says, unambiguously, who was responsible for what. The written assignment does not prevent the dispute. It ends it.

**When governance is scaled.** A single automated system with informal governance arrangements may work when the team is small and stable. As the number of systems grows, as the organisation scales, as teams change — informal understanding degrades. Written assignment is how governance scales without losing accountability in the expansion.
