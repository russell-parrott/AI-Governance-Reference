# RE-09 — Record Accuracy

> **Records are useless if wrong. This card explains why accuracy is not an administrative detail — it is a governance foundation.**

---

## What Is It?

Record accuracy is the property of governance records that makes them reliable — the degree to which they correctly capture what actually happened, what was actually decided, and what the system actually did.

An accurate record is not simply a record that exists. It is a record that correctly describes the event, decision, or activity it purports to document. It captures the right information, in the right form, at the right time, without omission, distortion, or error.

Record accuracy applies to all governance records — documentation of decisions, audit trails, decision records, system logs, and everything else the organisation creates to demonstrate that its automated systems are governed appropriately. A record that exists but is inaccurate does not support governance. It undermines it.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Governance records are only as valuable as their accuracy. An organisation that has extensive documentation of its automated system's governance appears, on the surface, to be well governed. But if those records do not accurately reflect what happened — if they were created after the fact, if they omit significant events, if they describe processes that were not followed — they are not evidence of good governance. They are evidence of poor governance that has been papered over.

Inaccurate records create a specific and serious risk: they mislead the people relying on them. Investigators reach wrong conclusions. Regulators form incorrect assessments. Courts are presented with unreliable evidence. Decisions about the organisation's governance — and its accountability for harm — are made on the basis of records that do not tell the truth.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Records that describe what should have happened rather than what did.** Governance records created after the fact — or adjusted to reflect the process as it was supposed to work rather than as it actually worked — are inaccurate by design. They create the appearance of compliance without its substance. When investigations go beyond the records to examine what actually happened, the gap between the two is itself a serious finding.

**Errors that compound across connected records.** Governance records reference each other. A decision record cites the system version. An audit trail records the approval. A system log reflects the configuration. If any one of those records contains an error, the error propagates into every record that relies on it. Inaccuracy in one record can undermine the reliability of the entire governance record set.

**Records that cannot be trusted in investigation.** When records are found to be inaccurate — even innocently, through transcription errors or incomplete capture — every other record the organisation produces comes under increased scrutiny. The question shifts from what the records show to whether the records can be believed.

**Inaccuracy that creates rather than resolves liability.** An inaccurate record that misrepresents a governance activity — overstating the scrutiny applied, understating a known problem, or omitting a relevant event — does not reduce the organisation's liability. It adds to it. Producing inaccurate records in response to a regulatory inquiry or in litigation is a distinct and serious legal problem on top of whatever triggered the inquiry.

Responsibility for record accuracy sits with whoever creates records and whoever governs the record-keeping system. Individual accuracy is a professional obligation. Systemic accuracy is a governance obligation.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Record accuracy is a requirement across every type of governance record an automated system generates:

- **Decision records** — outputs must be recorded as they were produced, not as the system was intended to produce them
- **Audit trails** — events must be captured in real time, not reconstructed from memory after the fact
- **System logs** — technical records must accurately reflect system state and activity, protected from alteration
- **Testing records** — results must reflect what testing actually found, not what was expected or desired
- **Approval records** — sign-off documentation must accurately record who approved what, when, and on what basis
- **Incident records** — accounts of what happened must reflect actual events, including unflattering details, not a curated narrative

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes record accuracy seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Are governance records created at the time of the event or activity they document, rather than reconstructed after the fact?
- Are there controls that prevent records from being altered, deleted, or overwritten once created?
- Is there a process for identifying and correcting inaccuracies in records when they are discovered — without destroying the original record?
- Are the people responsible for creating records trained and supported to do so accurately and completely?
- When records are produced in response to a regulatory inquiry or legal claim, is there a process for verifying their accuracy before they are submitted?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation's governance record cannot be relied upon — not by the organisation itself, not by regulators, and not by the courts. Records that might be inaccurate are records that cannot be used with confidence. That is not a documentation problem. It is a governance problem.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Accurate records provide a reliable foundation for investigation, audit, and regulatory examination | Creating accurate records at the time of events requires discipline that is difficult to maintain under operational pressure |
| Supports accountability by ensuring that records reflect actual decisions and activities, not intended ones | Errors in records are not always detectable without comparison to independent sources |
| Protects the organisation in investigation and litigation by ensuring submitted records withstand scrutiny | Correction of inaccurate records, while necessary, creates complexity — the original error and the correction must both be preserved |
| Builds trust with regulators — organisations whose records are consistently reliable are treated differently from those whose are not | Systems that generate records automatically may embed inaccuracies at the point of capture that are not visible until records are examined closely |
| Enables genuine governance — accurate records make the picture of system performance real rather than constructed | Cultural pressure to present governance activity favourably can produce records that are technically accurate but selectively incomplete |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: governance records produced in response to regulatory inquiries, legal proceedings, or individual rights requests must be accurate — and the production of inaccurate records, whether deliberately or through inadequate record-keeping systems, carries serious legal consequences.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions impose accuracy obligations on the processing of personal data, including records of how automated decisions affecting individuals were reached. An inaccurate decision record does not just fail a governance standard — it may constitute a breach of the individual's rights.

General legal principles in most jurisdictions treat the production of inaccurate records in regulatory proceedings or litigation as a serious matter, distinct from and additional to the underlying conduct under investigation. Organisations whose records are found to be inaccurate — even if the inaccuracy is attributed to poor record-keeping rather than deliberate falsification — face consequences beyond the original inquiry.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation whose governance records cannot be relied upon for accuracy is in a fundamentally weak position before any regulator or court. The records exist. They cannot be trusted. That combination is worse than having fewer records that can be.
