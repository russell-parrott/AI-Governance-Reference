# SP-15 — Transparency

> **The word appears in almost every governance conversation. This card explains what it actually means — and why saying it is not the same as achieving it.**

---

## What Is It?

Transparency is the condition in which the people affected by an automated system have access to the information they need to understand what is being done to them, why, and on what basis — in a form they can actually use.

It has two distinct dimensions that governance discussions frequently collapse into one.

The first is **organisational transparency** — the information an organisation makes available about how its automated systems work, what they are used for, and how they are governed. This is the transparency that governance frameworks, regulatory disclosures, and published policies address. It is directed at regulators, auditors, civil society, and the public.

The second is **individual transparency** — the information provided to a specific person about a specific decision that affected them. This is the transparency that individual rights frameworks address. It is directed at the individual whose credit was refused, whose application was rejected, whose benefit was denied.

Both matter. They require different things. Organisations that are transparent at the organisational level are not automatically transparent at the individual level — and vice versa.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Automated systems make decisions that affect people's lives — decisions about access to credit, employment, housing, healthcare, and public services. Those decisions are made by systems whose logic is not visible to the people they affect. Transparency is the mechanism by which that invisibility is partially corrected.

Without transparency, affected individuals cannot assess whether a decision about them was correct, cannot identify the basis on which to challenge it, and cannot know what information the system used. Accountability requires that someone can explain what happened and why. Transparency is what makes that explanation possible.

For organisations, transparency is also a governance discipline. The act of being able to explain what a system does — to a regulator, to an affected person, to a court — requires that the organisation understands its own systems well enough to explain them. Organisations that cannot be transparent about their automated systems frequently discover, in the attempt, that they do not understand those systems as well as they believed.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Transparency in form but not in function.** An organisation publishes documentation about its automated systems that is technically accurate and comprehensively detailed — but written in language that requires legal or technical expertise to interpret, structured in ways that make specific information difficult to locate, and directed at audiences other than the people most affected. The information exists. It does not reach the people it should reach, in a form they can use.

**Organisational transparency substituted for individual transparency.** An organisation that can explain how its systems work in general may be unable to explain why this system produced this outcome for this person. General transparency and individual transparency require different capabilities. A detailed published description of a credit model's methodology does not explain why a specific applicant was refused. Those are different questions requiring different answers.

**Transparency that arrives too late.** Information about a decision provided after the decision has been implemented and cannot be challenged, or after the window for exercising rights has closed, does not serve the purpose of transparency. The information existed. It did not function.

**Transparency claimed without the operational capability to deliver it.** An organisation commits to transparency as a governance principle, publishes a transparency policy, and describes its transparency practices. When an affected individual requests information about a specific decision, the organisation cannot produce it — because the decision records do not exist, the system cannot generate an individual-level account, or the process for responding to transparency requests does not function. The principle was stated. The capability was absent.

Responsibility for transparency — both organisational and individual — sits with whoever deployed the system. Transparency obligations cannot be met by pointing to what the vendor publishes about the model.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Transparency obligations and expectations arise at every point where automated systems affect individuals or are subject to public or regulatory accountability:

- **Financial services** — transparency to customers about automated credit, insurance, and pricing decisions, and to regulators about how models are built and governed
- **Healthcare** — transparency to patients about automated diagnostic and triage systems, and to regulators about clinical safety and validation
- **Public services** — transparency to citizens about automated eligibility and enforcement decisions, and to oversight bodies about how those decisions are made
- **Employment** — transparency to applicants and employees about automated screening and monitoring systems, and to equality bodies about their impact
- **Platform and content systems** — transparency to users about automated content moderation and recommendation systems, and to regulators about how those systems operate
- **Any organisation subject to regulatory oversight** — where transparency about automated system governance is a condition of continued operating authority

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes transparency seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Can an individual affected by an automated decision find out, in terms they can understand, that the decision was automated and what information the system used?
- Does the organisation's transparency to regulators and external bodies reflect the same picture of its systems as its transparency to affected individuals — or are different accounts given to different audiences?
- Is transparency information provided at the moment it is most useful — when a decision is made and can still be questioned — or only when specifically requested?
- Has the organisation tested whether its transparency mechanisms achieve understanding in the people they are directed at, rather than assuming that publication equals communication?
- When transparency is described as a governance commitment, does the operational capability to deliver it exist — the records, the processes, and the people needed to respond?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation's transparency is a stated position rather than a demonstrated one. Transparency is not a value that is held. It is a capability that is either present or absent — and its presence is tested in the individual request, not in the governance document.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Transparency enables affected individuals to understand and where appropriate challenge decisions that affect them | Achieving genuine transparency at the individual level requires system design investment that must be built in from the start |
| Organisational transparency supports regulatory accountability and public trust in automated systems | Transparency obligations can create tension with legitimate interests in protecting proprietary model details |
| The discipline of being transparent about systems forces organisations to understand those systems deeply | Technical and legal language in transparency documentation frequently renders it inaccessible to the individuals it is meant to inform |
| Transparency requirements drive governance investment that improves system quality and accountability | Transparency at the organisational level does not automatically translate into transparency at the individual level — both require separate investment |
| Active transparency — providing information proactively at the point of decision — is more effective than reactive transparency | Volume and complexity of transparency obligations can create compliance overhead that displaces genuine communication effort |

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: transparency obligations for automated systems are expanding in scope, specificity, and enforceability — and the standard against which they are assessed is moving from publication toward comprehension.

Data protection and privacy frameworks in many jurisdictions require that information about automated processing be provided in a concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible form, using clear and plain language. Those requirements apply to individual transparency — the information provided to affected people about decisions that concern them. They impose a communicative standard, not merely a disclosure one.

AI-specific legislation is advancing transparency requirements that address both dimensions — organisational transparency through documentation and disclosure obligations, and individual transparency through explanation and notification requirements. Some frameworks distinguish explicitly between the information that must be provided to regulators and the information that must be provided to affected individuals, recognising that different audiences require different forms of transparency.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that published information about its automated systems but cannot demonstrate that affected individuals could find, access, and understand what they needed to exercise their rights has not met its transparency obligations. The test is not what was published. It is what was understood.
