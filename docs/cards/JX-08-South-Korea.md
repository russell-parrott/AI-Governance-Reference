# JX-08 — South Korea

> **South Korea has enacted a comprehensive AI Basic Act taking effect in 2026 — making it one of the few jurisdictions outside the EU with binding AI-specific legislation already on the books.**

---

## The Framework

South Korea's AI Basic Act was enacted in late 2024 and takes full effect in 2026. The Act adopts a risk-based framework similar in structure to the EU AI Act, classifying AI systems by their potential impact and imposing obligations that scale with risk level. High-impact AI systems — those that pose significant risks to safety, fundamental rights or essential services — face the most demanding requirements.

The Act establishes definitions, governance principles, and obligations for developers and deployers of AI systems. It requires transparency for AI-generated content, notification when individuals interact with AI systems in high-stakes contexts, human oversight for high-impact decisions, and documentation and monitoring obligations for high-risk deployments.

Existing law provides the current binding framework while the AI Basic Act's implementation regulations are being developed. The Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA), significantly reformed in 2023, is among the most comprehensive data protection laws in Asia and applies to AI systems that process personal information. The Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection applies to online AI services. Financial regulation, healthcare regulation and labour law each add sector-specific requirements.

South Korea has been active internationally in AI governance discussions and has positioned itself as a significant voice in developing global AI norms. The country's technology sector — including major AI developers — means the AI Basic Act reflects both governance imperatives and competitiveness considerations.

---

## Key Regulators

**Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC)** — PIPA enforcement, data protection in AI contexts. The PIPC has enforcement authority over automated decision-making and individual rights.

**Korea Communications Commission (KCC)** — AI in broadcasting, telecommunications and online platforms.

**Financial Services Commission (FSC) and Financial Supervisory Service (FSS)** — AI in financial services, credit decisions, investment management.

**Ministry of Science and ICT** — AI policy, AI Basic Act implementation, national AI strategy.

**Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC)** — algorithmic competition concerns, digital market regulation.

**Ministry of Food and Drug Safety** — AI as a medical device in healthcare contexts.

---

## What Triggers Obligations

**The AI Basic Act** triggers for developers and deployers of high-impact AI systems in South Korea. High-impact categories include systems used in employment, credit, healthcare, education, public safety and critical infrastructure. Obligations include pre-deployment risk assessment, documentation, human oversight mechanisms, transparency disclosures and post-deployment monitoring.

**PIPA** triggers whenever an AI system processes personal information of South Korean residents. Automated decision-making with significant effects on individuals gives those individuals the right to request explanation and human review. The PIPC has published guidance on AI and data protection that sets specific expectations for organisations.

**AI-generated content provisions** of the AI Basic Act trigger when AI generates content in contexts where individuals might mistake it for human-created material. Disclosure obligations apply.

**Financial regulation** triggers for AI in financial services. The FSC and FSS have published guidance on AI governance for financial institutions, including model risk management expectations.

**Labour law** triggers for AI in employment decisions. South Korea has specific requirements for transparency about automated systems used in hiring and workplace management.

## Governance Connection

South Korea's AI Basic Act creates a governance framework that closely parallels the Atlas structure, making well-governed systems well-positioned for compliance as the Act's implementation regulations take effect.

**Risk classification** (→ LR-13, LL-01) is the organising principle of the AI Basic Act. Organisations must assess their systems' risk levels before the Act's requirements apply — the classification determines everything that follows. This assessment must be documented.

**Human oversight** (→ AR-03, CS-13) is required under the AI Basic Act for high-impact systems. The right to human review of automated decisions is also a PIPA right for decisions based on personal information. Organisations must build functional human review processes, not merely state that one exists.

**Transparency** (→ SP-15, LL-02) is required both for AI-generated content and for high-impact AI decisions. Individuals must be informed when AI is used in decisions that significantly affect them.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, LL-09) is a named obligation under the AI Basic Act. Developers and deployers each carry specific responsibilities. The deployer cannot transfer its obligations to the developer by contract.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are required under both PIPA and the AI Basic Act. Documentation of high-impact system development, testing, risk assessment and ongoing monitoring must be maintained. The PIPC and other regulators have powers to demand these records.
