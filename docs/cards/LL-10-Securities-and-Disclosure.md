# LL-10 — Securities and Disclosure

> **Securities law requires that investors receive accurate, complete information about material risks. An organisation that overstates its AI capabilities, or understates its AI risks, is not just marketing — it may be misleading the market.**

---

## What It Covers

Securities and disclosure law governs the information that publicly listed companies and issuers of securities must provide to investors and markets. It requires that material information — information that a reasonable investor would consider significant in making an investment decision — be disclosed accurately, completely and in a timely manner. It prohibits false and misleading statements about matters that affect investment decisions.

Major frameworks include the US Securities Exchange Act and SEC disclosure rules, the EU Market Abuse Regulation and Prospectus Regulation, and the UK Financial Services and Markets Act. These frameworks are enforced by specialist regulators — the SEC, ESMA, the FCA — with significant investigative and enforcement powers, including the ability to bring civil and criminal proceedings against companies and individuals.

Securities law applies to public companies and to others who make statements that affect public markets — including in prospectuses, investor presentations, earnings calls, press releases, regulatory filings, and increasingly in ESG and sustainability reports.

---

## Why AI Systems Trigger It

AI systems trigger securities and disclosure obligations when they are material to a company's business, when statements about them affect investment decisions, or when risks associated with them are not adequately disclosed.

**Materiality of AI capabilities and risks.** For organisations where AI systems are central to business operations or competitive advantage — technology companies, financial services firms, healthcare AI businesses — the capabilities and limitations of those systems are material to investment decisions. Investors need accurate information about what AI systems do, how well they perform, and what risks they present in order to assess the value and risk profile of their investment. Systematic overstatement of AI capabilities, or systematic omission of AI risks, creates securities law exposure.

**AI washing.** Regulators — particularly the US SEC — have taken enforcement action against companies that misrepresented their use of AI or machine learning in investor-facing communications. Claiming that a product "uses AI" when it does not, or describing AI capabilities in terms that materially overstate their nature or reliability, constitutes a false statement in investor communications. The SEC has issued guidance specifically addressing AI-related disclosure obligations and has brought enforcement actions for AI misrepresentation.

**Material AI risks in required disclosures.** Publicly listed companies are required to disclose material risks in periodic filings. AI-related risks — including regulatory risks from the EU AI Act and other emerging frameworks, reputational risks from biased or harmful AI outputs, operational risks from AI system failures, and legal risks from data protection and discrimination law — may be material risks that must be disclosed. The SEC has issued guidance addressing AI risk factor disclosure expectations.

**Earnings calls and investor presentations.** Statements about AI capabilities, AI-driven growth, and AI competitive advantage made by executives during earnings calls, at investor days, or in press releases are subject to securities law requirements. Where those statements are materially false or misleading, they create securities fraud exposure for the company and potentially for the individuals who made them.

**Insider information about AI failures.** An AI system failure that is material to a company's operations — a significant fraud detection failure, a clinical AI system recall, a major algorithmic trading loss — may constitute inside information that must be disclosed under market abuse regulations before it becomes public through other means.

## Governance Connection

Securities law connects Atlas governance concepts to investor protection obligations, creating a legal incentive for accurate, documented governance of AI systems.

**System documentation** (→ RE-13, SP-09) underpins accurate securities disclosure. An organisation that maintains thorough documentation of its AI systems — what they do, how they perform, what their limitations are, what risks have been identified — is in a position to make accurate disclosures. An organisation that does not is exposed to the risk of inadvertent misrepresentation.

**Model performance** (→ SP-17) is directly relevant to securities disclosure where AI performance is material to the business. Organisations that monitor and document AI system performance are able to disclose it accurately. Organisations that do not monitor performance cannot disclose it accurately — and may be making investor representations that are not grounded in evidence.

**Records** (→ RE-07, RE-13) are the foundation of disclosure accuracy. Regulatory investigations into alleged securities fraud typically involve extensive examination of internal records — what the organisation knew, when it knew it, and what was disclosed. Internal records that accurately reflect AI system performance and governance are protective. Internal records that contradict public disclosures are the central evidence in enforcement proceedings.

**Board oversight** (→ AR-06, AR-10) is directly implicated by securities disclosure requirements. The SEC has specifically addressed board oversight of AI-related risks as part of cybersecurity and technology risk disclosure requirements. A board that genuinely oversees AI risks is in a materially different position from one that approves disclosures without having received adequate management information about AI system performance and risks.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01) matters in securities law because enforcement can be brought against individuals — executives who signed off on misleading disclosures, directors who approved them — as well as against the organisation.

---

## Jurisdictions

Securities and disclosure law applies in all jurisdictions with public capital markets. US securities law has significant extraterritorial reach for companies listed on US exchanges or accessing US capital markets. The EU Market Abuse Regulation applies across EU markets. The UK operates an equivalent framework through the FCA. Equivalent securities regulators exist in all major financial jurisdictions.
