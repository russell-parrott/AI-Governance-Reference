# MK-04 — Regulators

> **Regulators set the standards that AI governance must meet and enforce them when organisations fall short. Their effectiveness depends entirely on whether the organisations they oversee can produce the evidence that governance requires — and increasingly, many cannot.**

---

## What This Market Is

Regulators are public bodies with statutory authority to oversee compliance with legal frameworks in defined sectors or domains. They include data protection authorities, financial regulators, healthcare regulators, competition authorities, sector-specific bodies and, increasingly, AI-specific oversight bodies. They appear wherever Parliament or legislature has created a framework that requires independent enforcement.

Their position in the governance structure is external and authoritative. They do not operate AI systems. They assess whether organisations that do operate AI systems have met their obligations under the applicable legal frameworks — and they have powers to investigate, sanction, direct and in some cases prohibit when those obligations are not met.

Regulators are also increasingly a primary audience for AI governance documentation. A governance framework that cannot satisfy a regulatory investigation is not a governance framework.

---

## Why AI Governance Matters to Them

Regulators face a fundamental challenge with AI governance: the legal frameworks they enforce were largely not designed for automated decision-making, while the AI-specific frameworks now entering force require enforcement capacity and technical understanding that many regulatory bodies are still developing.

The practical difficulty is evidential. Regulators can only enforce what they can establish. Establishing that an automated system caused harm, that an organisation failed to exercise adequate governance, or that a specific individual bore accountability requires evidence that organisations are frequently unable or unwilling to produce. Incomplete records, undocumented systems and diffuse accountability structures are the conditions that make AI enforcement difficult.

Regulators also face resource constraints in assessing technically complex systems. The gap between regulatory capacity and the technical sophistication of the systems being regulated creates practical enforcement limitations that bad actors can exploit.

---

## What Questions They Ask

Regulators approach AI governance through an enforcement and evidence lens:

- What legal obligations apply to this system in this context, and have they been met?
- Who is accountable for this system, and can that accountability be demonstrated?
- What records exist of the decisions this system made and the governance arrangements that surrounded it?
- Can the organisation reconstruct specific decisions that are under investigation?
- Was the harm that occurred foreseeable, and was adequate governance in place to prevent it?
- Does the organisation's governance documentation reflect what the system actually does?

Regulators are asking the questions that enforcement requires — questions about obligation, accountability, evidence and causation.

---

## What Evidence They Expect

Regulators require the formal governance record — the documented evidence that legal obligations were understood, assessed and met:

- Records of processing activities and data protection impact assessments
- Deployment authorisation records showing who approved the system and on what basis
- Risk assessments and legal compliance analyses
- Audit trails and decision logs for the period under investigation
- System documentation describing what the system does, what data it uses and what its limitations are
- Incident records and regulatory notification history
- Evidence of human oversight — not the claim that it exists, but records showing it functioned

The evidence regulators expect is the evidence the law requires to be maintained — and the absence of required records is itself a regulatory breach.

---

## Where They Appear in Investigations

Regulators initiate and lead AI governance investigations. They may act on complaints from affected individuals, on referrals from other bodies, on information disclosed under mandatory reporting obligations, or on their own initiative following adverse media coverage or sector-wide concerns.

The investigation process typically involves formal information requests, examination of governance documentation, interviews with named accountable individuals, and in serious cases on-site inspection. Regulators also have powers to require organisations to stop using systems, to impose fines, to require public disclosure of findings and — in personal accountability regimes — to sanction named individuals.

Regulators also shape governance through guidance, enforcement decisions and published findings. Their public enforcement record defines the practical standard of governance adequacy for their sector.

## Typical Failure Points

**Records that do not exist.** Organisations produce governance documentation — policies, frameworks, impact assessments — but fail to maintain the operational records that would demonstrate those frameworks were followed. When regulators investigate, the governance documentation cannot be connected to specific decisions.

**Diffuse accountability.** Governance frameworks name committees and functions rather than individuals. When regulators ask who was responsible, the answer is that responsibility was shared — which is the same as saying no one was responsible.

**Technical opacity presented as complexity.** Organisations respond to regulatory investigation by explaining that the system is too complex to explain, rather than by producing the records that governance requires. Regulators with developing technical capacity may initially accept this. Increasingly they do not.

**Governance built for normal conditions.** AI governance frameworks are designed to demonstrate compliance in normal operation. When regulators investigate a failure — which is by definition abnormal — the governance framework does not address the conditions they are examining.
