# LL-09 — Corporate Governance and Director Duties

> **Directors who approve AI deployment without adequate risk assessment are not making a technology decision. They are making a governance decision — one for which company law holds them personally accountable.**

---

## What It Covers

Corporate governance law defines the duties of directors and senior officers to the companies they lead and to the stakeholders those companies affect. It establishes standards of care, skill and diligence in decision-making, requires that directors act in the company's best interests, and holds them personally accountable for failures in oversight and risk management.

Major frameworks include the UK Companies Act 2006, which codifies director duties including the duty to exercise reasonable care, skill and diligence and the duty to promote the success of the company, US state corporate law — particularly Delaware fiduciary duty standards, which govern most large US public companies — and national company law regimes across EU member states and other jurisdictions.

Corporate governance extends beyond company law to include listing rules, corporate governance codes, and the expectations of institutional investors — all of which address how boards should govern significant risks, including those arising from technology.

---

## Why AI Systems Trigger It

AI systems trigger corporate governance and director duties obligations when boards approve their deployment, when material risks associated with AI systems are not adequately assessed or disclosed, and when governance failures allow AI-related harm to occur.

**The duty to exercise care and diligence.** Directors owe a duty to exercise reasonable care and diligence in their stewardship of the company. Approving the deployment of a significant AI system — one that makes consequential decisions at scale — without adequate risk assessment, without understanding what the system does, and without putting governance structures in place is a potential breach of that duty. The standard is not that directors must understand the technical detail of every AI system. It is that they must ensure adequate processes exist to assess and manage material risks.

**The business judgement rule and its limits.** In most jurisdictions, courts give directors substantial latitude in business decisions — the business judgement rule. But that latitude applies where directors have informed themselves adequately, acted in good faith, and considered the interests of the company and its stakeholders. A director who approved AI deployment without informed consideration of the risks, or who relied on assurances of compliance without scrutiny, has not exercised informed business judgement.

**Material risk and board awareness.** AI systems that make consequential decisions at scale — in credit, employment, healthcare, customer service — represent material operational and legal risks to the organisations that deploy them. Material risks are the proper subject of board attention. A board that was not informed about the AI systems its organisation deployed, and the risks they presented, has a governance gap. The question regulators and courts ask is whether adequate information was available to the board, not merely whether information was formally presented.

**The growing expectation of AI governance.** Regulators, institutional investors and corporate governance frameworks are increasingly explicit that board-level governance of AI systems is expected. The UK Corporate Governance Code, the EU's corporate sustainability reporting requirements, and US SEC disclosure guidance all create expectations about board oversight of technology risks. Meeting these expectations requires that boards receive adequate management information about AI systems, their risks, and their governance.

**Individual accountability regimes.** In financial services and other regulated sectors, individual accountability regimes — including the UK Senior Managers and Certification Regime — go further than company law. They require that specific named senior individuals be accountable for defined business areas, that their responsibilities be documented in statements of responsibilities, and that they take reasonable steps to ensure the activities they are responsible for are conducted in compliance with regulatory requirements.

## Governance Connection

Corporate governance law sits directly above the Atlas governance concepts — it is the legal framework that makes governance obligations personal, not just organisational.

**Accountability** (→ AR-01, AR-06) is the concept that company law reinforces through director duties. The Atlas defines accountability as direction and control exercised by a named individual. Company law makes that individual personally liable for failures in that direction and control when material harm results.

**Internal governance** (→ AR-10) is what director duties require in practice. The board must ensure that adequate governance structures exist — not because governance codes recommend them, but because the duty of care requires them. A board that delegated AI governance entirely to management, received no meaningful information about AI risks, and did not challenge management assurances has not discharged its governance obligations.

**Policy versus practice** (→ AR-11) is the gap that director duties expose. A board that approved an AI governance policy but received no evidence that the policy was being implemented has the form of governance without its substance. That gap becomes legally significant when things go wrong.

**Records** (→ RE-07, GQ-03) of board decisions — the information provided to the board, the questions asked, the decisions made and their rationale — are the evidence of whether director duties were discharged. Board minutes that reflect informed engagement with AI risk are evidence of governance. Minutes that record approval without discussion are evidence of its absence.

**Management responsibility** (→ AR-07) is the bridge between board-level duty and operational governance. Directors discharge their duties through management — by appointing competent people, setting clear expectations, and receiving adequate reporting. AI governance failures that occur at operational level but were never escalated to the board create questions about whether management fulfilled its reporting obligations.

---

## Jurisdictions

Director duties apply in all jurisdictions with company law frameworks. UK director duties under the Companies Act 2006 are codified and relatively explicit. US director duties under Delaware law are judge-made and governed by the business judgement rule. EU member states apply national company law with increasing harmonisation pressure. Individual accountability regimes in financial services exist in the UK, Ireland, Australia, Singapore and other jurisdictions.
