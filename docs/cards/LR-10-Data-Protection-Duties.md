# LR-10 — Data Protection Duties

> **Specific laws about personal information. This card explains what they require of automated systems — and why they apply whether the organisation knows it or not.**

---

## What Is It?

Data protection duties are the legal obligations that arise when an automated system processes personal information — information that relates to an identifiable individual.

Most automated systems process personal information. A credit system processes financial history. A recruitment system processes application data. A healthcare system processes medical records. A benefit system processes household circumstances. In each case, the system is handling information about real people — and data protection law applies to how that information is collected, used, stored, and acted upon.

Data protection duties are not one obligation. They are a framework of obligations — covering accuracy, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, security, and individual rights — that apply throughout the system's operation, not just at the point of data collection.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Personal information is not an input category. It is a legal status. The moment an automated system handles information that can be linked to an identifiable person, data protection law attaches to everything it does with that information — how it uses it, how long it keeps it, what decisions it makes on the basis of it, and what rights the person whose information it is can exercise.

Organisations frequently treat data protection as a privacy question — a matter of keeping information secure and not sharing it improperly. Data protection law for automated systems goes significantly further. It addresses the decisions those systems make, the logic they apply, and the rights of individuals to know, challenge, and override what has been done to them.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Processing personal data for purposes beyond what was authorised.** Data protection law requires that personal data be collected for specified, explicit purposes and not used in ways incompatible with those purposes. An automated system that uses data for a purpose not covered by the original collection — or that applies data collected in one context to decisions in another — may be in breach from the moment it does so.

**Automated decisions that individuals have the right to challenge.** In many jurisdictions, individuals have legal rights in relation to automated decisions that significantly affect them — including the right to request human review and the right to an explanation. A system deployed without the capability to meet those rights was non-compliant by design.

**Inaccurate personal data producing incorrect decisions.** Data protection law requires that personal data be accurate. Where an automated system makes decisions based on inaccurate personal data — and the organisation has not put in place adequate processes for identifying and correcting inaccuracies — both the data quality failure and the decisions it produced may constitute legal breaches.

**Retention of personal data beyond its legitimate purpose.** Data protection law requires that personal data be kept no longer than necessary. Decision records, system logs, and operational data that contain personal information must be managed within a retention framework that reflects legal requirements — not operational convenience.

Responsibility for data protection compliance in automated systems sits with the organisation that deploys them. Using a vendor's system, or processing data supplied by a third party, does not transfer data protection obligations. It may add to them.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Data protection duties apply wherever automated systems process personal information:

- **Financial services** — customer financial data processed by credit, fraud, insurance, and trading systems, subject to accuracy, purpose limitation, and individual rights obligations
- **Healthcare** — patient medical data processed by diagnostic, triage, and clinical management systems, subject to heightened protections for sensitive health information
- **Public services** — citizen and household data processed by eligibility, assessment, and enforcement systems, subject to transparency and rights obligations that are particularly significant given the power imbalance involved
- **Employment** — applicant and employee data processed by screening, monitoring, and management systems, subject to obligations around transparency, accuracy, and individual rights
- **Platform and content systems** — user behavioural and demographic data processed by recommendation, moderation, and advertising systems, subject to purpose limitation and rights obligations
- **Any automated system that uses information that can be linked to an identifiable person** — which describes the vast majority of automated decision systems in operation

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes data protection duties seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Has the organisation identified the personal data processed by each automated system and the legal basis on which it is processed?
- Does the system process personal data only for purposes consistent with the basis on which it was collected?
- Are individual rights — to access, to explanation, to challenge, to correction — built into the system's operation rather than treated as exceptional requests?
- Is personal data in the system accurate, and is there a process for identifying and correcting inaccuracies?
- Are retention periods for personal data in the system defined, applied, and consistent with legal requirements?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation is processing personal information through its automated systems without the legal foundation to do so adequately. Data protection duties apply regardless of whether the organisation is ready for them. The data subject's rights exist whether or not the system has been designed to respect them.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Data protection frameworks provide a structured legal basis for governing personal data in automated systems | The interaction between data protection obligations and other compliance frameworks requires careful navigation |
| Individual rights obligations drive governance design — systems must be built with rights compliance in mind from the start | Data protection requirements vary by jurisdiction, creating complexity for organisations operating across multiple legal environments |
| Accuracy and purpose limitation obligations align data protection compliance with good governance practice | The obligations applying to sensitive personal data — health, financial, and other special categories — are significantly more demanding |
| Transparency obligations create pressure for explainability that supports broader governance accountability | Data protection compliance in complex automated systems requires specialist legal knowledge that many organisations do not hold internally |
| Enforcement of data protection obligations by supervisory authorities creates external accountability for personal data governance | Achieving compliance across all dimensions simultaneously — accuracy, rights, retention, purpose — requires sustained organisational effort |

## The Law

Data protection and privacy frameworks exist in most developed economies and impose specific, enforceable obligations on organisations that process personal data through automated systems. Those obligations are not aspirational. They are legal requirements backed by enforcement powers that include significant financial penalties, operational orders, and in some jurisdictions criminal liability.

The obligations that apply specifically to automated decision-making — including rights to explanation, rights to human review, and requirements for fairness and transparency in automated processing — are among the most developed and actively enforced provisions of data protection law in many jurisdictions.

AI-specific legislation is adding obligations that operate alongside and build on data protection frameworks — extending requirements for transparency, explainability, and fairness to systems that may not have been fully captured by earlier data protection provisions.

The practical legal risk is direct and has been for longer than most AI governance obligations: data protection duties for automated systems are not emerging requirements. They are established law. Organisations that are not yet meeting them are not ahead of the regulatory curve. They are behind it.
