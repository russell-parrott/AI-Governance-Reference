# AR-07 — Management Responsibility

> **Zoom out to management. What do the bosses need to do day-to-day? This card explains their specific obligations.**

---

## What Is It?

Management responsibility is the set of ongoing obligations that senior managers and operational leaders carry in relation to the automated systems operating under their authority — the things they must actively do, not just nominally oversee.

It is not the same as board-level responsibility, which focuses on strategic oversight and accountability at the highest organisational level. Management responsibility is operational. It covers the day-to-day, week-to-week activities that keep governance functioning — ensuring monitoring is happening, reviews are being conducted, problems are being escalated, and the people responsible for running automated systems have what they need to do so properly.

Management responsibility is what connects governance policy to governance practice. The board sets the direction. Management makes it real.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Governance frameworks, policies, and procedures do not implement themselves. Someone must ensure that monitoring staff are adequately resourced. Someone must confirm that review schedules are being followed. Someone must be the point at which escalated problems land and receive a decision. Someone must notice when governance activity is falling short and take action before a deficiency becomes an incident.

That someone is management. Without active management engagement, governance structures that look complete on paper become hollow in practice. The policies exist. The roles are assigned. Nobody is checking whether any of it is working.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Governance that exists on paper but not in practice.** Management that delegates governance activity without verifying it is happening creates the appearance of oversight without its substance. When an incident occurs and investigation reveals that monitoring was not being conducted, reviews were not being held, and escalation procedures were not being followed, the question is not just what went wrong operationally. It is why management did not know.

**Problems that reach management too late.** Management that is not actively engaged with the governance of automated systems receives information about problems through formal channels — which means late, filtered, and often after the opportunity for early intervention has passed. Active management engagement creates earlier visibility.

**Resource constraints that are never surfaced.** Governance functions that are under-resourced relative to the systems they oversee produce inadequate oversight — not because people are negligent but because there is not enough capacity to do the job properly. Management is responsible for identifying and addressing those constraints. A governance function that is nominally in place but practically incapable is management's problem to resolve.

**Accountability that management cannot discharge.** Managers who are held accountable for the governance of automated systems but who do not actively engage with that governance cannot exercise the accountability they nominally hold. When questions are asked, they do not have answers. That is not a governance gap. It is a management failure.

Responsibility for active management engagement with automated system governance sits with the managers themselves — and with the senior leadership and board that hold them to account for it.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Management responsibility for automated systems arises in any organisation where automated systems operate under management authority:

- **Financial services** — senior managers responsible for credit, fraud, or trading functions carrying active oversight obligations for the automated systems within those functions
- **Healthcare** — clinical and operational managers responsible for ensuring that diagnostic and triage systems are monitored, reviewed, and governed as part of normal management activity
- **Public services** — departmental managers responsible for the automated systems used in service delivery, with active obligations for oversight and escalation
- **Employment** — HR and operational managers responsible for automated screening and monitoring tools, with obligations to ensure those tools are used within their governance parameters
- **Technology and platforms** — product and engineering managers responsible for the ongoing governance of the automated systems in their area, not just their technical performance
- **Any organisation where individual managers have authority over specific automated systems** — which in most medium and large organisations describes most of the management tier

---

## What It Means in Practice

A management team that takes its responsibilities seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Does each manager with authority over an automated system know what their governance obligations are in relation to it?
- Are governance activities — monitoring, review, escalation, documentation — being actively verified by management rather than assumed to be happening?
- When governance activities surface problems, do they reach management in time for early intervention rather than arriving as established incidents?
- Are governance functions adequately resourced for the systems they oversee, and is management actively addressing resource constraints when they arise?
- When managers are asked by senior leadership or the board about the governance of their automated systems, can they give accurate, evidence-based answers?

If those questions cannot be answered, management is holding accountability it is not actively exercising. Accountability that is not exercised is not a safety net. It is a liability that has not yet been called.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Active management engagement connects governance policy to operational practice | Managers with broad responsibilities may not have the specialist knowledge to identify governance deficiencies in technical systems |
| Provides early warning visibility — management close to operations receives signals before they become incidents | Management engagement without clear governance standards becomes inconsistent across different managers and systems |
| Creates a point of accountability that is both senior enough to act and close enough to operations to understand the issues | Managers under operational pressure frequently deprioritise governance activity in favour of immediate performance demands |
| Enables resource allocation decisions based on actual governance capacity rather than assumed sufficiency | Management accountability for automated systems is a relatively new expectation in many organisations, and the skills and habits are not yet established |
| Supports board-level accountability by ensuring the information reaching the board is accurate and current | The boundary between management responsibility and board responsibility is not always clearly defined, creating gaps or duplication |

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is moving toward explicit individual accountability for senior managers in relation to the automated systems operating under their authority — not just organisational accountability.

Regulatory frameworks in financial services in several jurisdictions already impose personal accountability obligations on named senior managers for the systems and processes within their functions. That accountability extends to automated decision-making systems. Governance failures that occur on a manager's watch are increasingly examined as personal accountability questions, not just organisational ones.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for defined management governance structures, with some frameworks specifying that named individuals at management level carry active, demonstrable responsibilities for automated system oversight — not just formal titles.

The practical legal risk is direct: a manager who holds nominal accountability for an automated system but cannot demonstrate active engagement with its governance is exposed both organisationally and, in regulated sectors, personally. Accountability without evidence of exercise is accountability waiting to become liability.
