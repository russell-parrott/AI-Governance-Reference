# AR-09 — Delegated Authority

> **Power can be passed down. This card explains how that works — and where it goes wrong.**

---

## What Is It?

Delegated authority is the formal transfer of defined decision-making power from a person or body that holds it to another person or body that will exercise it on their behalf — within defined limits, for defined purposes, and with the delegating party retaining overall accountability.

In the governance of automated systems, authority is delegated constantly. A board delegates oversight responsibility to a committee. A committee delegates operational accountability to management. A manager delegates day-to-day monitoring to a team. Each delegation transfers the power to act — but not the accountability for what that action produces. The person who delegates retains responsibility for what the delegate does in their name.

Delegated authority is not the same as passing a task to someone. It is the formal transfer of a defined power, with defined limits, to a defined person or role, with a record of what was transferred and what was retained.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Organisations cannot function without delegation. No board can monitor every automated system in real time. No senior manager can review every decision. Delegation is what makes governance operable at scale — distributing the work of oversight across the people and structures best positioned to perform it.

The governance risk is not delegation itself. It is delegation without definition. When authority is passed down without clear limits, without documentation, and without a mechanism for the delegating party to verify that it is being exercised appropriately, delegation becomes abdication. The work is done by someone else. The accountability remains — but the visibility to exercise it has been given away.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Delegation that becomes abdication.** When authority is delegated without active oversight of how it is being used, the delegating party loses the visibility needed to exercise the accountability they have retained. Problems develop at the delegated level and do not surface upward. The delegation has functioned as a transfer of accountability, not just authority — even though that was never the intention.

**Limits that are not defined or enforced.** Delegated authority without defined limits creates uncertainty about what the delegate can and cannot do. In practice, delegates tend to fill that uncertainty with action — exercising authority beyond what was intended, making decisions that should have been retained at a higher level, or committing the organisation in ways that were never authorised.

**Chains of delegation that lose accountability.** When authority is delegated through multiple levels — board to committee, committee to management, management to team, team to individual — accountability can become progressively diluted. At each step, the delegating party may assume the next level is exercising adequate oversight. By the time the chain reaches the person actually doing the work, accountability may be effectively invisible.

**Delegation that survives the conditions that justified it.** An authority delegated for a specific purpose, a specific period, or a specific system may continue to be exercised after those conditions have changed. Delegated authority that is not reviewed and renewed as circumstances evolve becomes authority exercised without a current mandate.

Responsibility for ensuring that delegated authority is defined, documented, and actively overseen sits with whoever holds the authority being delegated. Delegation transfers the power to act. It does not transfer the obligation to ensure that power is being used appropriately.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Delegated authority is present at every level of automated system governance:

- **Board to committee** — board-level oversight obligations delegated to audit, risk, or technology committees with defined terms of reference
- **Executive to management** — strategic accountability for automated systems delegated to operational managers with defined scope and reporting obligations
- **Management to specialist functions** — oversight of specific governance activities delegated to compliance, risk, or technology teams with defined remits
- **Organisation to vendor** — operational responsibilities for system components delegated to third-party suppliers through contractual arrangements
- **Senior to junior staff** — day-to-day monitoring and review activity delegated to operational staff within defined parameters
- **Any governance structure where responsibility is distributed across levels** — which describes every organisation operating automated systems at scale

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes delegated authority seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Is each delegation of authority formally documented — what was delegated, to whom, within what limits, and with what reporting obligations?
- Does the delegating party maintain sufficient visibility to verify that delegated authority is being exercised appropriately?
- Are the limits of each delegation defined and understood by both the delegating party and the delegate?
- Is there a process for reviewing and renewing delegations as circumstances, systems, and personnel change?
- When a delegate acts at the edge of or beyond their delegated authority, is there a mechanism for that to be identified and addressed?

If those questions cannot be answered, authority is being exercised in the organisation's name without adequate definition or oversight. Somewhere in the chain of delegation, someone is acting on authority whose limits are unclear, whose exercise is unverified, and whose accountability has become theoretical. That is not distributed governance. It is distributed risk.

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Makes governance operable at scale — distributes oversight to the level best positioned to exercise it | Delegation without definition creates uncertainty about authority limits that delegates tend to resolve through action |
| Allows specialist knowledge to be applied at the appropriate level rather than requiring senior involvement in every decision | Chains of delegation dilute visibility — each additional level reduces the delegating party's ability to verify appropriate exercise |
| Creates flexibility — authority can be delegated where it is most needed and adjusted as circumstances change | Delegation that is not reviewed becomes outdated — authority exercised under conditions that no longer apply |
| Supports accountability by creating a traceable chain from action to the ultimate authority responsible for it | Delegation records that exist at the point of creation but are not maintained become unreliable over time |
| Enables the board and senior management to focus on strategic oversight rather than operational detail | The line between legitimate delegation and accountability abdication is not always clear — and organisations frequently cross it without intending to |

---

## The Law

The legal direction across most developed economies is consistent: delegating authority for the governance of automated systems does not transfer the legal accountability of the delegating party — and regulators look through delegation chains to assess whether ultimate accountability holders exercised genuine oversight.

Regulatory frameworks in financial services in several jurisdictions have developed specific tests for whether senior managers exercised reasonable oversight of delegated functions — and have imposed personal liability where delegation was found to have been abdication rather than genuine transfer with retained oversight.

AI-specific legislation is advancing requirements for traceable accountability chains in automated system governance, with some frameworks requiring that delegations be documented and that the ultimate accountable party be identifiable regardless of how many levels of delegation exist between them and the operational activity.

The practical legal risk is direct: a board member, executive, or senior manager who delegated authority for automated system governance without retaining adequate visibility and oversight has not reduced their accountability by delegating. They have increased their exposure — because they authorised action they did not adequately oversee, in their name, producing consequences they are still responsible for.
