# GV-N08 — What A Standard Is Not

> **The organisation follows a standard. Now check: what does that actually mean — and what does it not?**

---

## What It Is Not

A standard is not compliance. It is not certification. It is not a guarantee of safety, quality, or ethical practice. It is not proof that a system works correctly, that it treats people fairly, or that it meets regulatory requirements. It is not a substitute for governance, and it is not enforced by the body that wrote it.

A standard, properly understood, is a documented set of requirements, recommendations, or guidance — produced by a standards body, an industry group, or a technical committee — that defines what a system, process, or organisation should do or be in order to be considered as meeting that standard. It defines the bar. It does not check whether the bar has been cleared. It does not impose consequences if it has not.

What organisations most often confuse with standards is one or more of the following:

**Compliance, as if following a standard and complying with law were the same thing.** Standards and legal requirements are different instruments. A standard may be referenced in regulation, adopted as a condition of a contract, or used as evidence of good practice — but a standard is not law unless it has been given legal force. An organisation that meets a standard has not necessarily met its legal obligations. An organisation that meets its legal obligations has not necessarily met any particular standard. The two overlap. They are not the same.

**Certification, as if achieving a standard and being certified to it were interchangeable.** Certification is an assessment — conducted at a point in time, by a body with the authority to conduct it, producing a finding about whether the requirements of a standard were met at that moment. Certification is valuable. It is not the standard itself, and meeting the standard in the period after certification is a separate question from whether the certification was awarded.

**A guarantee of outcome.** Standards specify process requirements, documentation requirements, and in some cases technical requirements. They do not guarantee that systems built or operated to those requirements will perform correctly, fairly, or safely in every case. A standard for AI governance may require that certain governance activities occur. It does not ensure that those activities are conducted with genuine rigour, that they catch every problem, or that the system will not cause harm. Conformity with a standard is evidence of process. It is not evidence of outcome.

**An external authority that monitors and enforces.** The body that produces a standard does not monitor whether organisations meet it, investigate failures, or impose consequences for non-conformity. Standards bodies set requirements. Enforcement — where it exists — comes from regulators, certification bodies acting under contract, or courts. Following a standard without certification and without contractual or regulatory requirement is a voluntary choice whose only accountability is internal.

---

## Why Confusion Matters

When organisations mistake standards for compliance, they believe that meeting a standard satisfies their legal obligations — and stop at the standard when the law required more. When they mistake standards for guarantees, they believe that certified conformity protects them from liability — and discover under scrutiny that a standard is not a shield.

The most consequential confusion is treating standard-setting as governance. An organisation that has adopted a governance standard — mapped its practices against it, documented conformity, perhaps sought certification — has done something useful. It has not done governance. Governance is the active, ongoing exercise of accountability, oversight, and control that the standard describes in the abstract. The standard defines what governance should look like. The organisation must build the reality.

---

## Where Confusion Appears

**Regulatory submissions that cite standard conformity as evidence of compliance.** An organisation responds to a regulatory inquiry by demonstrating conformity with a recognised AI governance standard. The standard is reputable and relevant. The regulator asks whether the legal obligations were met — obligations that the standard addresses but does not fully capture. Conformity with the standard is relevant evidence. It is not the answer to the legal question.

**Procurement requirements satisfied by certification rather than assessment.** A contracting organisation requires vendors to meet a governance standard. Vendors provide certification. The contracting organisation treats certification as assurance that the vendor's systems are governed adequately. Certification established conformity at the point of assessment. It is not ongoing assurance of governance quality.

**Standards adopted as substitutes for internal governance design.** An organisation adopts a recognised standard and maps its governance design to the standard's requirements — building its governance structure around the standard rather than around its own risk profile and accountability obligations. The standard was designed for general application. The organisation's context is specific. Conformity with a standard designed for the general case may not address the governance gaps that matter most in this particular one.

**"We follow ISO X" as a response to governance questions.** Standards references in governance documentation, marketing materials, and regulatory responses are common. They signal intent and provide a framework reference. They do not answer questions about whether governance is functioning, whether accountability is genuine, or whether the organisation is meeting its legal obligations.

---

## How To Tell The Difference

Five questions that distinguish standards from what they are commonly mistaken for:

- Does the organisation know which of its legal and regulatory obligations are addressed by the standard it follows — and which are not?
- Is the organisation's conformity with the standard verified by an independent assessment, or self-declared — and does it know the difference those two things make?
- Does conformity with the standard represent a current state of affairs, or did certification occur at a point in the past that may not reflect current practice?
- Does the organisation treat the standard as a floor — a minimum requirement below which it should not fall — or as a ceiling that defines adequacy?
- When governance questions arise that the standard does not address, does the organisation have the governance infrastructure to answer them — or does the standard represent the full extent of its governance thinking?

---

| Common Substitutes | What A Standard Actually Is |
|---|---|
| Legal compliance | A documented set of requirements that may or may not overlap with legal obligations |
| A guarantee of safety or quality | A definition of what meeting the standard requires — not an assurance of outcome |
| Ongoing conformity assurance | A requirement set that must be actively maintained — not a status that persists once achieved |
| An enforcement mechanism | A framework without enforcement power of its own — accountability comes from elsewhere |
| The totality of governance | A structure for governance thinking — not a substitute for the governance itself |

## The Law

Standards do not have legal force unless they have been given it — by being incorporated into regulation, adopted as a condition of authorisation, or referenced in a contract with legal consequences for non-conformity. An organisation that follows a standard but does not know whether that standard has legal force in its context has not assessed its compliance position. It has assessed its standard conformity.

Regulators in some sectors adopt specific standards as indicative of good practice or as conditions of authorisation — but those adoptions are sector-specific and context-specific. The same standard may be legally relevant in one context and merely advisory in another.

AI-specific legislation in multiple jurisdictions references harmonised standards as potential routes to demonstrating conformity with legal requirements — a mechanism sometimes called a presumption of conformity. Those mechanisms are valuable. They establish that meeting a defined standard is treated as evidence of meeting the corresponding legal requirement. They do not establish that meeting a standard satisfies all legal requirements, that the standard is current, or that certification under the standard is equivalent to ongoing conformity.

The practical legal risk is direct: an organisation that relied on standard conformity to satisfy its governance obligations, and that later faces a regulatory investigation or legal challenge, will be assessed against the legal standard — not the technical one. If those standards overlap imperfectly, the overlap does not extend the legal protection.
