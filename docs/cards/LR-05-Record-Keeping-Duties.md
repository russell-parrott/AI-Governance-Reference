# LR-05 — Record-Keeping Duties

> **Law often demands you keep records. This card explains what those legal obligations are — and how they connect to the governance records the organisation should already be maintaining.**

---

## What Is It?

Record-keeping duties are the legal obligations that require an organisation to create, maintain, and retain specific records in relation to its automated systems — obligations that exist independently of whether the organisation considers those records useful for its own governance purposes.

They are external requirements, imposed by law, that define what must be recorded, in what form, for how long, and in what circumstances records must be produced. They are not satisfied by having a general documentation practice. They require that specific records exist, that they contain specific information, and that they can be produced on demand.

Record-keeping duties translate the governance aspiration of documentation into a legal obligation. Where governance practice says records should be kept because they support accountability, record-keeping duties say records must be kept because the law requires it.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Legal record-keeping obligations exist because legislators and regulators have concluded that certain records are essential to accountability — that without them, the behaviour of organisations operating automated systems cannot be examined, compliance cannot be assessed, and individuals cannot exercise their rights.

An organisation that keeps records voluntarily for governance reasons and an organisation that keeps them because the law requires it may end up with identical documentation. The difference is in what happens when they do not. Voluntary records that are missing are a governance gap. Legally required records that are missing are a legal breach — with its own consequences independent of whatever the missing records might have shown.

---

## What's the Real Risk?

**Required records that were never created.** An organisation that did not know it had legal record-keeping obligations has not met them. The obligations existed from the first day of the system's operation. The records that should have been created were not. That deficit cannot be remedied retrospectively — the period for which records were required but do not exist remains a gap regardless of what is created afterwards.

**Records kept in forms that do not satisfy legal requirements.** Record-keeping duties often specify not just that records must exist but what they must contain and in what form they must be held. Records that exist but do not meet the specified content or format requirements may not satisfy the obligation — a distinction that emerges under scrutiny rather than in routine operation.

**Retention periods shorter than legal requirements.** An organisation that applies its own retention policy without reference to legal minimum periods may dispose of records before the law permits. Destroyed records that were legally required to be retained are not a data management issue. They are a legal breach with potential consequences for ongoing investigation and individual rights.

**Legal obligations that vary by record type and are not mapped comprehensively.** Record-keeping duties for automated systems arise from multiple legal frameworks simultaneously — data protection law, sector regulation, employment law, and others. Each may impose different requirements for different record types. An organisation that has mapped some of its obligations but not all is compliant in the areas it has examined and exposed in the ones it has not.

Responsibility for identifying, meeting, and maintaining legal record-keeping obligations sits with the organisation's leadership. It is a compliance obligation that cannot be delegated away from governance.

---

## Where Does It Appear?

Legal record-keeping duties for automated systems arise across every regulatory framework that governs what those systems do:

- **Data protection law** — obligations to maintain records of processing activities, data protection impact assessments, and responses to individual rights requests relating to automated systems
- **Financial services regulation** — obligations to retain transaction records, model documentation, governance records, and audit trails for automated trading, lending, and advisory systems
- **Employment law** — obligations to retain records of automated screening and assessment decisions in some jurisdictions, particularly where individual rights to access or challenge those decisions may be exercised
- **Healthcare regulation** — obligations to retain clinical records, including records of automated diagnostic and triage outputs that form part of a patient's care record
- **AI-specific legislation** — emerging obligations to maintain technical documentation, conformity records, and monitoring logs for high-risk automated systems, available to regulators on request
- **General corporate governance** — obligations that apply to records of board and management decisions, which extend to decisions about automated system deployment, governance, and oversight

---

## What It Means in Practice

An organisation that takes record-keeping duties seriously must be able to answer these questions at any time:

- Has the organisation identified the specific legal record-keeping obligations that apply to each of its automated systems across all relevant frameworks?
- Are records being created and maintained in the forms and with the content that legal obligations require — not just in forms that suit operational convenience?
- Are retention periods set at the legal minimum where that minimum exceeds the organisation's own governance preference?
- Is there a process for reviewing record-keeping obligations when legal frameworks change or new systems are deployed?
- Can the organisation produce legally required records on demand, within the timeframes that law or regulators specify?

If those questions cannot be answered, the organisation is likely meeting some of its legal record-keeping obligations and missing others — and does not have a clear picture of which is which. Partial compliance with legal record-keeping duties is not a position that holds under scrutiny. It is a position that reveals new gaps each time it is examined.

---

## Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|
| Legal record-keeping obligations create an external baseline that governance documentation practice must meet | Mapping record-keeping duties across multiple simultaneous legal frameworks requires legal expertise and ongoing maintenance |
| Meeting legal obligations ensures records exist that support governance, investigation, and individual rights regardless of internal practice | Record-keeping requirements specified in law may not align perfectly with operational governance documentation practices, requiring parallel systems |
| Compliance with record-keeping duties provides a defensible position when records are demanded by regulators or courts | Legal minimum requirements represent a floor, not a ceiling — meeting them does not guarantee governance adequacy |
| Legal retention periods protect records from premature disposal by establishing external minimums that override internal preference | Record-keeping obligations that are not clearly communicated to operational staff produce non-compliance through ignorance rather than intent |
| Documented compliance with record-keeping duties demonstrates governance maturity to regulators | The volume of records required by comprehensive legal compliance creates data management obligations that are significant and ongoing |

---

## The Law

Record-keeping duties for automated systems exist across multiple legal frameworks in most developed economies — and the obligations are specific, enforceable, and increasingly demanding.

Data protection frameworks in many jurisdictions require organisations to maintain records of processing activities, including automated decision-making, and to produce those records to supervisory authorities on request. The absence of required records is itself a compliance failure, separate from whatever the records might have shown.

Sector-specific regulation in financial services, healthcare, and other domains imposes detailed record-keeping obligations that extend to automated systems — often specifying minimum retention periods measured in years, content requirements, and conditions for production to regulatory and legal authorities.

AI-specific legislation is advancing record-keeping obligations for high-risk systems that include technical documentation, training data records, testing and validation records, and ongoing monitoring logs — creating a comprehensive documentary trail that regulators can examine to assess governance adequacy across the system's entire operational life.

The practical legal risk is direct: record-keeping duties do not wait to be discovered. They apply from the first day of operation. An organisation that has been operating automated systems without meeting its legal record-keeping obligations has not just a future compliance task. It has a historical gap — for every period the system operated without the required records — that cannot be closed by what it does from today.
