AI Governance Reference

AI Governance Reference is a plain-English reference library for AI governance, automated decision-making and accountability. It explains the terms organisations need when discussing automated systems in business, regulation, investigation and legal disputes.

The reference covers system properties, authority, responsibility, human oversight, records, audit trails, legal obligations and the wider legal landscape. It is a reference, not legal advice.

System Properties

Every automated system has a defined purpose, a set of inputs, and a range of outputs. System Properties explains the vocabulary that describes what a system is, what it does and where its limits lie.

These concepts; intended use, training data, system scope, model performance, are the starting point for any governance conversation. You cannot govern what you cannot describe.

Authority & Responsibility

When an automated system makes a decision that affects someone, the question that follows is always the same: who was responsible?

Authority & Responsibility explains the concepts that answer it; accountability, role definition, delegated authority, board and management responsibility. These are not abstract governance principles. They are the concepts regulators examine and courts apply when something goes wrong.

Control Systems

A system that runs without human attention is a system without meaningful oversight. Control Systems explains the mechanisms that keep humans in the loop; monitoring, alerts, escalation, intervention and shutdown.

Each concept describes a different layer of operational control. Together they define whether an organisation can detect problems, respond to them and stop them from continuing.

Records & Evidence

Governance is what can be demonstrated. Records & Evidence explains the documentation, logs, decision records and audit trails that allow an organisation to reconstruct what happened after a decision is challenged.

These concepts matter because investigation, litigation and regulatory scrutiny all begin with the same question: what records exist?

Law & Regulation

Automated systems operate inside a legal framework that already existed before AI became a boardroom concern. Law & Regulation explains the legal concepts most directly relevant to organisations that deploy automated decision systems; individual rights, regulatory oversight, compliance duties, liability and enforcement powers.

These are not future risks. They are current obligations.

Legal Landscape

AI governance does not exist in isolation from law. The Legal Landscape section explains the major legal frameworks that automated systems commonly trigger; from data protection and discrimination law to financial regulation, product liability and director duties.

Each briefing explains what the framework covers, why AI systems activate it and how it connects to the governance concepts in the Atlas. This section does not provide legal advice. It explains the legal terrain.
